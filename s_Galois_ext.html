    Section 1.15 Galois extensions

    At least we meet the main players of our course: Galois extensions.

    Definition 1.15.1. Galois extension.

    A field extension \(K/F\) is Galois if it is normal and separable.

    Remark 1.15.2. Galois extension.

    Note that in our definition of a Galois extension, we do not assume that the field extension \(K/F\) is finite. Note further that any Galois extension is algebraic, as by definition any separable extension is algebraic.
    Both notions involved in the definition of a Galois extension, normal and separable extensions, can be recast in terms of statements about extensions of field embeddings. The theorem below is a recap of some of these different formulations. The framing we use here, and which will be the default framing for the rest of the course, is to fix an algebraic closure \(\overline{F}/F\) and to consider only the extensions \(K\) of \(F\) lying within \(\overline{F}\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.11.1, any algebraic extension of \(F\) can be embedded in \(\overline{F}\text{,}\) and so we do not lose anything (up to isomorphism) by restricting to this setup.

    Theorem 1.15.3. Normal/separable revisited.

    Let \(\overline{F}/F\) be an algebraic closure, and let \(K/F\) be a subextension.
    1. In general we have \(\Aut_F(K)\subseteq \Hom_F(K,\overline{F})\text{.}\)
    2. \(K/F\) is normal if and only if \(\Hom_F(K,\overline{F})=\Aut_F(K)\) if and only if \(m_{\alpha,F}(x)\) splits completely over \(K\) for all \(\alpha\in K\text{.}\)
    3. If \(K/F\) is finite, then \(K/F\) is separable if and only if
      \begin{equation} \abs{\Hom_F(K,\overline{F})}=[K\colon F]\text{.}\tag{1.26} \end{equation}

    Corollary 1.15.4. Finite Galois extensions.

    Let \(\overline{F}/F\) be an algebraic closure, and let \(K/F\) be a finite subextension. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(K/F\) is Galois.
    2. \(\abs{\Aut_F(K)}=\abs{\Hom_F(K,\overline{F})}=[K\colon F]\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.15.5. Galois group.

    Let \(K/F\) be a Galois extension. The Galois group \(\Gal(K/F)\) of \(K\) over \(F\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} \Gal(K/F)=\Aut_F(K)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If \(K=\overline{F}\) is an algebraic closure of \(F\text{,}\) we often write simply \(G_F\) for \(\Gal(\overline{F}/F)\text{.}\)

    Example 1.15.6. Galois group: quadratic extension.

    Let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\) Show that \(K/\Q\) is Galois, describe explicitly the elements of \(\Gal(K/\Q)\text{,}\) and identify \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) as a familiar finite group.
    Solution.
    Since \(\character \Q=0\text{,}\) the extension is automatically separable. The extension is normal since \(K\) is the splitting field of \(x^2-2\text{.}\)
    Using Theorem 1.10.3, we see that \(\Aut_\Q(K)=\Hom_\Q(K,\overline{\Q})\) has exactly two elements, corresponding to the two roots of \(x^2-2\) lying in \(\overline{Q}\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \sigma_1(a+b\sqrt{2}) \amp = a+b\sqrt{2}\\ \sigma_2(a+b\sqrt{2}) \amp = a-b\sqrt{2}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Note that \(\sigma_1=\id\text{,}\) the identity map. Letting \(\sigma=\sigma_2\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \Gal(K/\Q)=\Aut_\Q(K)=\{\id, \sigma\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since there is up to isomorphism one group of cardinality two, we have \(\Gal(K/\Q)\cong \Z/2\Z\text{.}\)

    Example 1.15.7. Galois group: degree-8 extension.

    Let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3},\sqrt{5})\text{.}\) Show that \(K/\Q\) is Galois, describe explicitly the elements of \(\Gal(K/\Q)\text{,}\) and identify \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) as a familiar finite group.
    Solution.
    The extension is automatically separable, and is the splitting field of \(\{x^2-2, x^2-3, x^2-5\}\text{.}\) Thus \(K/\Q\) is Galois.
    We have seen previously that \([K\colon \Q]=8\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} \abs{\Gal(K/\Q)}=\abs{\Aut_\Q(K)}=\abs{\Hom_\Q(K,\Qbar)}=[K\colon \Q]=8\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We claim that
    \begin{equation*} \Gal(K/\Q)\cong \Z/2\Z\times \Z/2\Z\times \Z/2\Z\cong \{\pm 1\}\times \{\pm 1\} \times \{\pm 1\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where the group operation on \(\{\pm 1\}\) is multiplication (as integers).
    To see why this is true, first observe that if \(\sigma\) is a \(\Q\)-automorphism of \(K\) then it must satisfy
    \begin{align*} \sigma(\sqrt{2}) \amp =\pm \sqrt{2}\\ \sigma(\sqrt{3}) \amp =\pm \sqrt{3}\\ \sigma(\sqrt{5}) \amp =\pm \sqrt{5}\text{.} \end{align*}
    These are necessary conditions for \(\sigma\) to be a \(\Q\)-embedding. Since a \(\Q\)-embedding of \(K\) is completely determined by where it sends \(\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) \(\sqrt{3}\text{,}\) and \(\sqrt{5}\text{,}\) the equations above tell us there are at most \(8=2\cdot 2\cdot 2\) different elements of \(\Gal(K/\Q)\text{.}\) Since \(\abs{\Gal(K/\Q)}=8\text{,}\) we see in this case that these necessary conditions are in fact sufficient: i.e., each of the eight choices of where the generators are sent actually gives rise a unique \(\Q\)-embedding \(\sigma\colon K\rightarrow K\text{.}\)
    We will exploit the fact that an element \(\sigma\in \Gal(K/\Q)\) is determined by its action on the three generators in order to get a grip on the group structure. For example, it is easy to see that \(\sigma^2=\id\) for all \(\sigma\in \Gal(K/\Q)\text{.}\) Indeed, if we have
    \begin{align} \sigma(\sqrt{2}) \amp = (-1)^{n_1}\sqrt{2}\tag{1.27}\\ \sigma(\sqrt{3}) \amp = (-1)^{n_2}\sqrt{3}\tag{1.28}\\ \sigma(\sqrt{5}) \amp = (-1)^{n_3}\sqrt{5}\text{,}\tag{1.29} \end{align}
    then \(\sigma^2\) satisfies
    \begin{align*} \sigma^2(\sqrt{2}) \amp = \sigma((-1)^{n_1}\sqrt{2})\\ \amp = (-1)^{2n_1}\sqrt{2}=\sqrt{2}\\ \sigma^2(\sqrt{3}) \amp = \sigma((-1)^{n_2}\sqrt{3})\\ \amp = (-1)^{2n_2}\sqrt{3}=\sqrt{3}\\ \sigma^2(\sqrt{5}) \amp = \sigma((-1)^{n_3}\sqrt{5})\\ \amp = (-1)^{2n_3}\sqrt{5}=\sqrt{5}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since \(\sigma^2\) fixes each of the generators, we have \(\sigma^2=\id\text{,}\) as claimed. It now follows that \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) is abelian. Indeed, in general if a group \(G\) satisfies \(g^2=e\) for all \(g\in G\text{,}\) then \(G\) is abelian. (This is a classic exercise in elementary group theory.) Finally, we can use the structure theorem of finite abelian groups to conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \Gal(K/\Q)\cong (\Z/2\Z)^3\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as up to isomorphism, this is the only abelian group of cardinality \(8\text{,}\) all of whose elements have order at most 2.
    Alternatively, we can take a more direct approach. Define a map
    \begin{align*} \phi\colon \Gal(K/\Q) \amp \rightarrow \{\pm 1\}\times \{\pm 1\}\times \{\pm 1\}\\ \sigma \amp \mapsto \left((-1)^{n_1},(-1)^{n_2},(-1)^{n_3}\right)\text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(\sigma\) satisfies equations (1.27)–(1.29). This is an injective map since \(\sigma\in \Gal(K/\Q)\) is uniquely determined by where it sends the three generators. Since it is injective and the two groups both have cardinality equal to 8, it is bijective. To see that \(\phi\) is a group homomorphism, we argue again using the three generators of \(K\text{:}\) suppose \(\phi(\sigma)=((-1)^{n_1},(-1)^{n_2},(-1)^{n_3})\) and \(\phi(\tau)=((-1)^{m_1},(-1)^{m_2},(-1)^{m_3})\text{;}\) then we have
    \begin{align*} (\sigma\tau)(\sqrt{2}) \amp =\sigma((-1)^{m_1}\sqrt{2}) \\ \amp = (-1)^{n_1+m_1}\sqrt{2}\\ (\sigma\tau)(\sqrt{3}) \amp =\sigma((-1)^{m_2}\sqrt{3}) \\ \amp = (-1)^{n_2+m_2}\sqrt{3}\\ (\sigma\tau)(\sqrt{5}) \amp =\sigma((-1)^{m_3}\sqrt{5}) \\ \amp = (-1)^{n_3+m_3}\sqrt{5}\text{,} \end{align*}
    whence, by definition of \(\phi\text{,}\)
    \begin{align*} \phi(\sigma\tau) \amp = ((-1)^{n_1+m_1},(-1)^{n_2+m_2},(-1)^{n_3+m_3})\\ \amp =((-1)^{n_1},(-1)^{n_2},(-1)^{n_3})((-1)^{m_1},(-1)^{m_2},(-1)^{m_3})\\ \amp =\phi(\sigma)\phi(\tau)\text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.
