Theorem 1.19.8. Computing cyclotomic polynomials.

Given a positive integer \(n\text{,}\) let \(\Phi_n(x)\) denote the \(n\)-th cyclotomic polynomial.
  1. \(x^n-1=\prod_{d\mid n}\Phi_d(x)\text{.}\)
  2. If \(n> 2\) is odd, then
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{2n}(x)=\Phi_n(-x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
  3. If \(p\) is prime, then
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{p^r}(x)=\Phi_p(x^{p^{r-1}})\text{.} \end{equation*}
  4. If \(p\) is a prime and \((p,m)=1\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{p^rm}(x)=\Phi_m(x^{p^r})/\Phi_m(x^{p^{r-1}})\text{.} \end{equation*}
  5. If \(p_1,p_2,\dots, p_r\) are distinct primes, then
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{p_1^{n_1}p_2^{n_2}\cdots p_{r}^{n_r}}(x)=\Phi_{p_1p_2\dots p_n}(x^{p_1^{n_1-1}p_2^{n_2-1}\cdots p_r^{n_r-1}})\text{.} \end{equation*}
  6. \(\Phi_n(x)=x^\phi(n)\Phi_n(1/x)\text{.}\) As a result, cyclotomic polynomials are palindromic: i.e., if \(\Phi_n(x)=a_dx^d+a_{d-1}x^{d-1}+\cdots +a_1x+a_0\text{,}\) then \(a_k=a_{d-k}\) for all \(0\leq k\leq d\text{.}\)

Proof.

Statement (1) was proved as part of the proof of TheoremÂ 1.19.5.
The remaining statements can be proved using the following argument: if \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are (a) monic, (b) separable, and (c) have the same degree, then \(f=g\) if and only if they have the same roots. Note that for all \(n\in \Z_+\text{,}\) since \(\Phi_n(x)\) is irreducible and \(\character \Q=-\text{,}\) \(\Phi_n(x)\) is separable.
For example, consider (2). First observe that since \(n\) is odd, we have \(\phi(2n)=\phi(2)\phi(n)=\phi(n)\text{.}\) Thus \(\deg \Phi_{2n}(x)=\deg \Phi_n(-x)\text{.}\) Furthermore, since \(n> 2\text{,}\) \(\phi(n)\) is even. Thus \(\Phi_n(-x)\) is monic. Lastly, the roots of \(\Phi_n(-x)\) are precisely the negatives of the roots of \(\Phi_n(x)\text{.}\) Suppose \(\zeta\) is a primitive \(n\)-th root of unity. Since \(n\) is odd, we cannot have \(\zeta^j=-1\text{.}\) It follows that
\begin{align*} (-\zeta)^j=(-1)^j \zeta^j=1 \amp \iff j\text{ even and } \zeta^j=1 \\ \amp \iff 2\mid j \text{ and } n\mid j \amp (\ord \zeta=n)\\ \amp \iff 2n\mid j\text{.} \end{align*}
This shows that \(\ord(-\zeta)=2n\text{.}\) We conclude that the \(\phi(n)\) roots of \(\Phi_n(-x)\) are precisely the distinct primitive \(2n\)-th roots of unity. It follows that both polynomials are separable, and we have \(\Phi_{2n}(x)=\Phi_n(-x)\)
The proofs of the rest of the statements are left as an exercise.
