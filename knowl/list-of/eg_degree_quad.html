Example 1.2.13. Degree of quadratic extension.

Let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{,}\) where \(d\) is a square-free positive integer. Compute \([K\colon \Q]\text{.}\)
Solution.
First we claim that \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})=\{a+b\sqrt{d}\colon a,b\in \Q\}\text{.}\) This is shown in much the same manner as in Example 1.2.6. First we show that \(\{a+b\sqrt{d}\colon a,b\in \Q\}\) is indeed a field (easy), and then we follow Procedure 1.2.5.
Next we will show that \([K\colon \Q]=\dim_\Q K=2\text{,}\) following Procedure 1.2.9. We claim that the set \(B=\{1, \sqrt{d}\}\) is basis of \(K\) over \(\Q\text{.}\) Since \(K=\{a+b\sqrt{d}\colon a,b\in \Q\}\text{,}\) we see that \(\Span B=K\text{.}\) To see that \(B\) is linearly independent, assume we have \(a+b\sqrt{d}=0\) for some \(a,b\in \Q\text{.}\) If \(a\ne 0\text{,}\) then \(\sqrt{d}=-b/a\in \Q\text{,}\) and thus \(\sqrt{d}\) is a rational root of the primitive integer polynomial \(x^2-d\text{.}\) Gauss’s lemma then implies that \(\sqrt{d}\) is an integer; but this is clearly impossible, since \((\sqrt{d})^2=d\text{,}\) and \(d\) is assumed to be a square-free integer. Thus we must have \(a=0\text{.}\) But then \(b\sqrt{d}=0\) implies \(b=0\text{,}\) since \(\sqrt{d}\ne 0\text{.}\)
Having shown that \(B=\{1,\sqrt{d}\}\) is a basis of \(K\) over \(\Q\text{,}\) we conclude that \([K\colon \Q]=\dim_\Q K=\abs{B}=2\text{.}\)
