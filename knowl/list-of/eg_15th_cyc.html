Example 1.19.10. \(15\)-th cyclotomic extension.

Let \(K=\Q(\zeta)\text{,}\) where \(\zeta\in \C\) is a primitive \(15\)-th root of unity. Let \(G=\Gal(K/\Q)\text{.}\)
  1. Compute \(\Phi_{15}(x)\text{.}\)
  2. Compute the lattice of subgroups of \(G\text{.}\)
  3. Compute the lattice of intermediate fields of \(K/\Q\) and describe each field either in the form \(\Q(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha\) is written as a polynomial expression in \(\zeta\) and \(\zeta^{-1}\text{,}\) or in the form \(\Q(\sqrt{m})\) for some integer \(m\text{.}\)
Solution.
  1. Using Theorem 1.19.8, we have
    \begin{align*} \Phi_{15}(x) \amp =\Phi_{3}(x^5)/\Phi_3(x)\\ \amp =(x^{10}+x^5+1)/(x^2+x+1)\\ \amp = x^8-x^7+x^5+x^4+x^3-x+1\text{.} \end{align*}
  2. By Theorem 1.19.6 and Theorem 0.4.6 we have
    \begin{equation*} G\cong (\Z/15\Z)^*\cong (\Z/3\Z)^*\times (\Z/5\Z)^*\cong \Z/2\Z\times \Z/4\Z\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The last isomorphic description of \(G\) helps us see that in fact we have \(G=\angvec{-1}\times \angvec{2}\text{.}\) Proper subgroups of \(G\) will be of cardinality \(2\) and \(4\text{,}\) and hence are either cyclic, or of the form \(\angvec{a,b}\) for elements \(a\) and \(b\) of order \(2\text{.}\) Using the description \(G=\angvec{-1}\times \angvec{2}\text{,}\) we see that there are exactly 3 elements of order \(2\text{,}\) and thus \(\angvec{-1}, \angvec{4},\angvec{-4}\) are the three subgroups of cardinality 2. Next it is easy to see that the elements of order 4 are \(\pm 2\) and \(\pm 2^3=\pm 8\text{,}\) giving rise to the two cyclic subgroups \(\angvec{2}\) and \(\angvec{-2}\) of cardinality \(4\text{.}\) Lastly, the only possibility for a non-cyclic subgroup of cardinality \(4\) is
    \begin{equation*} \{1,-1,4,-4\}=\angvec{-1,4}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  3. Using the Galois correspondence, we derive the following lattice of subfields.
    Now to explain some of the computations above. Note that \(\zeta^3\) is a primitive \(5\)-th root of unity, and we know already that \(\Q(\zeta^3)\) is a degree-4 extension of \(\Q\text{.}\) Since \(\sigma_{-4}(\zeta^3)=\zeta^{-12}=\zeta^3\text{,}\) we see that \(\Q(\zeta^3)\leq K^{\angvec{-4}}\text{;}\) since the degrees match, we have equality. Note that the field \(K^{\angvec{-1,4}}\) must be the unique quadratic subextension of \(\Q(\zeta^3)/\Q\text{.}\) We have seen elsewhere that this is \(\Q(\sqrt{5})=\Q(\zeta^3+\zeta^{-3})\text{.}\)
    Next, since \(\zeta^5\) is a primitive cube-root of unity that is fixed by \(\sigma_{-2}\text{,}\) we see that \(K^{\angvec{-2}}=\Q(\zeta^5)=\Q(\sqrt{-3})\text{.}\) It now follows that the third quadratic extension must be \(\Q(\sqrt{-15})\text{.}\)
    Lastly, using the lattice diagram, we see that
    \begin{align*} K^{\angvec{4}}\amp =\Q(\sqrt{5},\sqrt{-3}) \\ \amp =\Q(-1+\sqrt{5}+2\sqrt{-3}) \amp (\text{not fixed by any automorphism})\\ \amp = \Q(\zeta^3+\zeta^{-3}+\zeta^{5}+\zeta^{-5})\text{.} \end{align*}
