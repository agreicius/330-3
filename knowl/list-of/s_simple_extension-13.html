Example 1.4.12. Existence of transcendental numbers over \(\Q\).

Consider the field extension \(\C/\Q\text{.}\) Let
\begin{align*} A\amp = \{\alpha\in \C\colon \alpha \text{ algebraic over } \Q} \\ T \amp = \{\alpha\in \C\colon \alpha \text{ transcendental over } \Q} \text{,} \end{align*}
so that \(\C\) is the disjoint union of \(A\) and \(T\text{.}\) The set \(A\) is countable! Why? Each element of \(A\) is the root of a polynomial \(f\in \Q[x]\text{.}\) The set \(\Q[x]\) is countable. (Proof sketch: \(\Q[x]=\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty P_n\text{,}\) where \(P_n\) is the set of all rational polynomials of degree at most \(n\text{.}\) Each \(P_n\) is countable, and thus \(\Q[x]\text{,}\) being a countable union of countable sets, is countable.)
