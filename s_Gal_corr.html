    Section 1.16 Galois correspondence

    The Galois correspondence for a Galois extension \(L/F\) is a dictionary of sorts that relates in a very precise manner the set of all intermediate fields of \(L/F\) and the set of all subgroups of \(\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\) One of the fundamental results of Galois theory is articulated in Theorem 1.16.5, which states among other things that when \(L/K\) is a finite Galois extension, the intermediate fields of \(L/F\) and the subgroups of \(\Gal(K/F)\) are in bijective correspondence. One half of this correspondence is a map that associates to a subgroup \(H\leq \Gal(L/K)\) an intermediate field \(K\) called the fixed field of \(H\text{,}\) which we now define in a slightly more general setting.

    Definition 1.16.1. Fixed field.

    Let \(L\) be a field, and let \(H\) be a group of automorphisms of \(L\text{.}\) The fixed field of \(H\text{,}\) denoted \(L^H\text{,}\) is the set of all elements of \(L\) fixed by all elements of \(H\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} L^H=\{\alpha\in L\colon \sigma(\alpha)=\alpha \text{ for all } \sigma\in H\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.16.2. Fixed fields.

    Let \(L\) be a field, and let \(H\) be a group of automorphisms of \(L\text{.}\) The set \(L^H\) is a subfield of \(L\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Since the elements of \(H\) are field automorphisms of \(L\text{,}\) we see that \(\sigma(0)=0\) and \(\sigma(1)=1\) for all \(\sigma\in H\text{.}\) Thus \(0,1\in L^H\text{.}\) Furthermore, if \(\alpha,\beta\in L^H\text{,}\) then for all \(\sigma\in H\) we have
    \begin{align*} \sigma(\alpha +\beta) \amp = \sigma(\alpha)+\sigma(\beta)\\ \amp = \alpha+\beta\\ \sigma(-\alpha) \amp =-\sigma(\alpha)\\ \amp = -\alpha\\ \sigma(\alpha\, \beta) \amp =\sigma(\alpha)\sigma(\beta)\\ \amp = \alpha\, \beta\text{,} \end{align*}
    showing that \(\alpha+\beta\text{,}\) \(-\alpha\text{,}\) and \(\alpha\,\beta\) are also elements of \(L^H\text{.}\) Lastly, if \(\alpha\in L^H\) is nonzero, then
    \begin{equation*} \sigma(\alpha^{-1})=(\sigma(\alpha))^{-1}=\alpha^{-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    showing that \(\alpha^{-1}\) is also in \(L^H\text{.}\) This proves \(L^H\) is a subfield of \(L\text{.}\)
    Given a finite Galois extension \(L/F\text{,}\) the map \(H\mapsto L^H\) associates to each subgroup of \(\Gal(L/F)\) an intermediate field \(K=L^H\) of \(L/F\text{.}\) The next result allows us to define a map going the other way.

    Theorem 1.16.3. Galois group of intermediate field.

    Let \(L/F\) be a Galois extension, and let \(K\) be an intermediate field of this extension: i.e., we have the tower of extensions \(L/K/F\text{.}\)
    1. The extension \(L/K\) is Galois.
    2. \(\displaystyle \Aut_K(L)=\Gal(L/K)=\{\sigma\in \Gal(L/F)\colon \sigma\vert_K=\id\}.\)

    Proof.

    1. Since \(L/F\) is separable, so is \(L/K\) by strong transitivity. Since \(L/F\) is normal, we have \(L=F(A)\text{,}\) where \(A\) is the set of all roots of a collection of polynomials \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\text{.}\) But since \(F\leq K\text{,}\) we have \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq K[x]\text{;}\) and since \(F(A)=K(A)=L\text{,}\) we see that \(L\) is also a splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}\) over \(K\text{.}\) Thus \(L/K\) is normal.
    2. We have
      \begin{align*} \Gal(L/K) \amp = \Aut_K(L)\\ \amp = \{\sigma\in \Aut(L)\colon \sigma\vert_K=\id\}\\ \amp = \{\sigma\in \Aut_F(L)\colon \sigma\vert_K=\id\} \amp (F\leq K)\\ \amp=\{\sigma\in \Gal(L/F)\colon \sigma\vert_K=\id\} \text{.} \end{align*}
    We now have all the necessary definitions and results to define the Galois correspondence.

    Definition 1.16.4. Galois correspondence.

    Let \(L/F\) be a Galois extension. Let \(\mathcal{F}\) be the set of all intermediate fields of \(L/F\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the set of all subgroups of \(\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\) The Galois correspondence of \(L/F\) is the pair of maps
    \begin{align*} \mathcal{F} \amp \rightarrow \mathcal{S} \amp \mathcal{S}\amp \rightarrow \mathcal{F}\\ K \amp \longmapsto \Aut_K(L) \amp H\amp\longmapsto L^H\text{.} \end{align*}
    The Galois correspondence theorem for finite groups tells us, among other things, that the two maps of the Galois correspondence are inverses of one another.

    Theorem 1.16.5. Galois correspondence.

    Let \(L/F\) be a finite Galois extension.
    1. The two maps of the Galois correspondence for \(L/F\) are inverses of one another, defining a bijection between the set of intermediate fields of \(L/F\) and the set of subgroups of \(\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\)
    2. Given an intermediate field \(K\text{,}\) the extension \(L/K\) is Galois and \(\Aut_K(L)=\Gal(L/K)\text{.}\)
    3. Given a subgroup \(H\leq \Gal(L/F)\text{,}\) we have \([L\colon L^H]=\abs{H}\) and \([L^H\colon F]=[\Gal(L/F)\colon H]\text{.}\)
    4. The Galois correspondence is inclusion reversing. In more detail, we have
      \begin{equation*} K\leq K' \iff \Aut_{K'}(L)\leq \Aut_K(L) \end{equation*}
      for all intermediate fields \(K\) and \(K'\text{,}\) and
      \begin{equation*} H\leq H' \iff L^{H'}\leq L^H\text{,} \end{equation*}
      for all subgroups \(H\) and \(H'\text{.}\)

    Example 1.16.6. Galois correspondence.

    Let \(K\leq \C\) be the splitting field of \(x^3-2\) over \(\Q\text{.}\)
    1. Show that \(K/\Q\) is Galois, describe explicitly the elements of \(\Gal(K/\Q)\text{,}\) and identify \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) as a familiar finite group.
    2. Produce the lattice of subgroups of \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) and provide generators for all subgroups involved.
    3. Use the above and the Galois correspondence to produce the lattice of intermediate fields of \(K/\Q\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    By definition \(K/\Q\) is a splitting field, hence normal. Since all algebraic extensions of \(\Q\) are separable, we conclude that \(K/\Q\) is Galois.
    Let \(\zeta=e^{2\pi i/3}\text{,}\) and let
    \begin{align*} \alpha_1 \amp = \sqrt[3]{2}\amp \alpha_2\amp=\sqrt[3]{2}\,\zeta \amp \alpha_3\amp =\sqrt[3]{2}\,\zeta^2 \end{align*}
    be the three roots of \(x^3-2\text{.}\) We have shown elsewhere that
    \begin{equation*} K=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2},\zeta)=\Q(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3) \end{equation*}
    is an extension of degree \(6\) over \(\Q\text{.}\) Thus \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) is a group of order \(6\text{.}\) Let \(R=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3\}\text{.}\) We claim that \(\Gal(K/\Q)\cong S_R\cong S_3\text{.}\) Indeed, since any \(\sigma\in \Gal(K/\Q)=\Aut_\Q(K)\) must send a root of \(x^3-2\) to a root of \(x^3-2\text{,}\) it defines via restriction a map \(\sigma\vert_R\colon R\rightarrow R\text{,}\) which must be bijective, since \(\sigma\) is injective. Define the map
    \begin{align*} \phi\colon \Gal(K/\Q) \amp \rightarrow S_R\\ \sigma \amp \mapsto \sigma\vert_R\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since the \(\alpha_i\) generate \(K\) over \(\Q\text{,}\) a \(\Q\)-automorphism is completely determined by where it sends each \(\alpha_i\text{;}\) this implies that the map \(\phi\) is injective. Since \(\abs{\Gal(K/\Q)}=\abs{S_R}=6\text{,}\) \(\phi\) must in fact be bijective. Lastly, since the group operation for both \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) and \(S_R\) is composition of functions, \(\phi\) is clearly a group homomorphism. We conclude that \(\phi\) is an isomorphism.
    The table below provides two descriptions of the elements of \(\Gal(K/\Q)\) in terms of their action on a set of generators of \(K\) over \(\Q\text{.}\) The first column gives a cycle notation description of the action of \(\sigma\) on the generating set \(R=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3\}\text{.}\) The second column describes \(\sigma\) in terms of its action on the generating set \(\{\sqrt[3]{2}, \zeta\}\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} (1)=\id \amp \amp \sqrt[3]{2}\amp\mapsto \sqrt[3]{2}\\ \amp \amp\zeta\amp\mapsto \zeta\\ (12) \amp \amp \sqrt[3]{2}\amp\mapsto \sqrt[3]{2}\,\zeta\\ \amp\amp \zeta\amp\mapsto \zeta^2\\ (13) \amp \amp \sqrt[3]{2}\amp\mapsto \sqrt[3]{2}\,\zeta^2\\ \amp\amp \zeta\amp\mapsto \zeta^2\\ (23) \amp \amp \sqrt[3]{2}\amp\mapsto \sqrt[3]{2}\\ \amp \amp\zeta\amp\mapsto \zeta^2\\ (123) \amp \amp \sqrt[3]{2}\amp\mapsto \sqrt[3]{2}\,\zeta\\ \amp \amp\zeta\amp\mapsto \zeta\\ (132) \amp \amp \sqrt[3]{2}\amp\mapsto \sqrt[3]{2}\,\zeta^2\\ \amp\amp \zeta\amp\mapsto \zeta \end{align*}
    As usual, we should think of the descriptions above as giving a “summary” of an automorphism \(\sigma\) based on what it does to the elements of a generating set. This information determines \(\sigma\) uniquely, but one needs to do a little algebra to then figure out what \(\sigma\) does to an arbitrary element of the form
    \begin{equation*} a+b\sqrt[3]{2}+c\sqrt[3]{4}+d\zeta+e\sqrt[3]{2}\,\zeta+f\sqrt[3]{4}\, \zeta, a,b,c,d,e,f\in \Q\text{.} \end{equation*}
    For notational ease, let \(G=\Gal(K/\Q)\text{.}\) We will use the cycle permutation representation of \(G\) given in the table above. The lattice of subgroups of \(G\cong S_3\) is something we have computed elsewhere. We provide it hear, along with a choice of generators of each subgroup. Note that our lattice is upside down: given two subgroups connected by an edge, the higher subgroup is a subgroup of the lower.
    Applying the Galois correspondence to this lattice results in the complete lattice of intermediate fields of \(K/\Q\text{,}\) oriented in the usual right-side up fashion.
    We’ve gone ahead and computed \(K^{\{\id\}}=K\) and \(K^{S_3}=\Q\text{.}\) The first equality is obvious: every element of \(K\) is fixed by the identity automorphism \(\id\colon K\rightarrow K\text{.}\) The second equality follows from the fact that the two maps of the Galois correspondence are inverses of one another: since \(\Q\mapsto \Aut_\Q(K)=G\text{,}\) we must have \(G\mapsto K^G=\Q\text{.}\)
    We’ve also added some information about the degrees of all the relevant extensions. The degrees of the extensions leading up to \(K\) are equal to the cardinality of the corresponding subgroup \(H_i\text{.}\) The degrees of the extensions leading down to \(\Q\) are given by the index \([G\colon H_i]\) of the corresponding subgroup. Alternatively, these degrees can be computed using the tower law.
    It remains to explicitly compute the various extensions \(K^{H_i}\text{.}\) We can use what we already know about \(K/\Q\) to help us along. For example, according to the diagram, there is exactly one subextension \(K^{H_5}/\Q\) of degree \(2\text{.}\) Since \(\Q(\zeta)/\Q\) is such a subextension, we must have \(K^{H_5}=\Q(\zeta)\text{.}\)
    Next, according to the diagram, the three extensions \(K^{H_i}/\Q\text{,}\) \(i\in \{2,3,4\}\text{,}\) are the three subextensions of \(K\) of degree \(3\text{.}\) Thus they must be the three cubic extensions \(\Q(\alpha_1), \Q(\alpha_2), \Q(\alpha_3)\text{.}\) But which is which? The subgroup \(H_2\) is generated by the \(\sigma\) with permutation representation \((12)\text{.}\) Since by definition this permutation fixes \(\alpha_3=\text{,}\) and does not fix \(\alpha_1\) or \(\alpha_2\text{,}\) we conclude that we must have \(K^{H_2}=\Q(\alpha_3)=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2}\, \zeta^2\text{.}\) Similar reasoning allows us to match the other degree-3 extensions, yielding the following completed lattice.
