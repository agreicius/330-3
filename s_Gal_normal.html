    Section 1.18 Galois correspondence: two group actions

    There are a number of layers of depth to the Galois correspondence. On its surface, it simply establishes a bijective correspondence between the set \(\mathcal{F}\) of intermediate fields of a finite Galois extension \(L/F\) and the set \(\mathcal{S}\) of subgroups of its Galois group \(G=\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\) Moving one layer deeper, we see that it further captures the identical lattice structure of, on the one hand, subextensions of \(L/F\text{,}\) and on the other, subgroups of \(G\text{.}\) We now go even deeper and reveal that the Galois correspondence respects natural group actions of \(G\) on \(\mathcal{F}\) and \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) An important consequence of this is that normal subextensions of \(L/F\) get identified under the Galois correspondence precisely with the normal subgroups of \(G\text{.}\)
    Accordingly, in this section we will introduce some group action theory into our study of Galois actions. Specifically, we will study two different group actions of \(G\) (an action on the field \(L\text{,}\) and an action on \(\mathcal{F}\)), and will relate these group actions to field extension properties.

    Subsection Galois action on \(L\)

    Let \(L/F\) be a finite Galois extension with Galois group \(G=\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\) It is easy to see that the operation \(\sigma\cdot \alpha=\sigma(\alpha)\) defines a group action of \(G\) on \(L\text{.}\) Theorem 1.18.2 articulates how this \(G\)-action relates to field properties of elements of \(L\text{.}\) With the exception of statement (3) of the theorem, which was derived during the proof of Artin’s theorem, the theorem is essentially a restatement of portions of Theorem 1.16.5 using the language of group actions. To this end, we first introduce some terminology around the action of the Galois group on \(L\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.18.1. Galois conjugates of elements.

    Let \(L/F\) be a finite Galois extension, and let \(G=\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\) Given \(\alpha\in L\text{,}\) a Galois conjugate (or just conjugate) of \(\alpha\) is an element of the form \(\sigma(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\sigma\in G\text{.}\) We denote by \(O_\alpha\) the orbit of \(\alpha\) under \(G\text{:}\) i.e., \(O_{\alpha}\) is the set
    \begin{equation*} O_\alpha=\{\sigma(\alpha)\colon \sigma\in G\} \end{equation*}
    of all conjugates of \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    The stabilizer subgroup of \(\alpha\text{,}\) denoted \(\Stab_G(\alpha)\text{,}\) is the set of all elements of \(G\) that fix \(\alpha\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \Stab_G(\alpha)=\{\sigma\in G\colon \sigma(\alpha)=\alpha\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.18.2. Galois action on \(L\).

    Let \(L/F\) be a finite Galois extension with Galois group \(G=\Gal(L/F)\text{,}\) let \(\alpha\) be an element of \(L\text{,}\) and let \(O_{\alpha}=\{\sigma(\alpha)\colon \sigma\in G\}\) be the set of Galois conjugates of \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \Aut_{F(\alpha)}(L)=\Stab_G(\alpha)\)
    2. \(m_{\alpha,F}(x)=\prod_{\beta\in O_\alpha}(x-\beta) \text{.}\)
    3. \([F(\alpha)\colon F]=\deg_F \alpha=\abs{O_{\alpha}}=[G\colon \Stab_G(\alpha)] \text{.}\)
    4. Given an intermediate field \(K\text{,}\) we have \(\alpha\in K\) if and only if \(\sigma(\alpha)=\alpha\) for all \(\sigma\in \Aut_{K}(L)\text{.}\)
      In particular, \(\alpha\in F\) if and only if \(\sigma(\alpha)=\alpha\) for all \(\sigma\in G\text{.}\)
    5. Given an intermediate field \(K\) we have \(F(\alpha)=K\) if and only if \(\Aut_{K}(L)=\Stab_G(\alpha)\text{.}\)
      In particular, we have \(F(\alpha)=L\) if and only if \(\Stab_{G}(\alpha)=\{\id\}\text{.}\)

    Subsection Galois action on intermediate fields

    Next we will study the action of \(\Gal(L/F)\) on the set
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{F}=\{K\colon F\leq K\leq L\} \end{equation*}
    of intermediate fields of a finite Galois extension \(L/F\text{.}\) Given an intermediate field \(K\in \mathcal{F}\) and automorphism \(\sigma\in \Gal(L/F)=\Aut_F(L)\text{,}\) \(\sigma\) maps \(K\) isomorphically onto \(\sigma(K)\text{.}\) Since \(\sigma\in \Aut_F(L)\) we have \(F\leq \sigma(K)\leq L\text{,}\) and thus \(\sigma(K)\in \mathcal{F}\text{.}\) It is then easy to see that the operation defined as \(\sigma\cdot K=\sigma(K)\) defines a group action of \(G=\Gal(L/F)\) on \(\mathcal{F}\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.18.3. Galois conjugates of fields.

    Let \(L/F\) be a Galois extension and let \(G=\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\) Given an intermediate field \(K\) and automorphism \(\sigma\in G\text{,}\) the field \(\sigma(K)\) is called a Galois conjugate (or simply conjugate) of \(K\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.18.4. Galois correspondence: normality.

    Let \(L/F\) be a finite Galois extension, and let \(G=\Gal(L/F)\text{.}\) Suppose the intermediate field \(K\leq L\) corresponds to the subgroup \(H\leq G\) under the Galois correspondence: i.e., we have
    \begin{align*} K \amp = L^H \amp H\amp =\Aut_K(L)\text{.} \end{align*}
    1. Given \(\sigma\in G\text{,}\) the Galois conjugate \(K'=\sigma(K)\) corresponds to \(H'=\sigma H \sigma^{-1}\text{,}\) the conjugate of \(H\) by \(\sigma\text{:}\) i.e., we have
      \begin{align*} \sigma(K)\amp =L^{\sigma H\sigma^{-1}} \amp \sigma H \sigma^{-1}=\Aut_{\sigma(K)}(L) \end{align*}
    2. The extension \(K/F\) is normal if and only if \(H\) is a normal subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) Using logical shorthand:
      \begin{equation} K/F \text{ normal} \iff H\normalin G \text{.}\tag{1.33} \end{equation}
    3. Assume \(K/F\) is normal, and write \(N=\Aut_{K}(L)\text{.}\) The map
      \begin{align*} \sigma\colon G\amp \rightarrow \Gal(K/F)\\ \sigma \amp \mapsto \sigma\vert_K \end{align*}
      gives rise to an isomorphism
      \begin{align*} G/N \amp \isomto \Gal(K/F)\\ \sigma N \amp \mapsto \sigma\vert_{K}\text{.} \end{align*}
      In particular, the Galois group of \(K/F\) is isomorphic to \(G/N\text{:}\) i.e.,
      \begin{equation} \Gal(K/F)\cong G/N\text{.}\tag{1.34} \end{equation}
