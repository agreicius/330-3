    Section 1.8 Algebraic closure

    An algebraic closure of a field is in a sense an absolute version of the notion of relative algebraic closure introduced in Section 1.5.

    Definition 1.8.1. Algebraically closed.

    A field \(F\) is algebraically closed if all polynomials \(f\in F[x]\) split completely over \(F\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.8.2. Algebraically closed.

    It is easy to see that a field \(F\) is algebraically closed if and only if all nonconstant polynomials \(f\in F[x]\) have a root in \(F\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.8.3. Algebraic closure.

    An algebraic closure of a field \(F\) is an extension \(K/F\) satisfying the following properties.
    1. \(K\) is algebraically closed.
    2. \(K/F\) is algebraic.

    Example 1.8.4.

    1. Show that \(\C\) is an algebraic closure of \(\R\text{.}\)
    2. Show that \(\C\) is not an algebraic closure of \(\Q\text{.}\)
    3. Find an algebraic closure of \(\Q\) lying within \(\C\text{.}\)
    The next result articulates the relationship between algebraically closed fields, relative algebraic closure, and algebraic closure.

    Theorem 1.8.5. Algebraically closed and algebraic closure.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension and assume \(K\) is algebraically closed. The relative algebraic closure \(\overline{F}_K\) of \(F\) in \(K\) is an algebraic closure of \(F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    We know from Corollary 1.6.14 that \(\overline{F}_K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{;}\) we must prove it is algebraically closed.
    Let \(f(x)\in \overline{F}_K[x]\) be a nonconstant polynomial. Since \(K\) is algebraically closed and \(f(x)\in \overline{F}_K[x]\subseteq K[x]\text{,}\) there is an element \(\alpha\in K\) such that \(f(\alpha)=0\text{:}\) i.e., \(f\) has a root in \(K\text{.}\) Since \(f(\alpha)=0\text{,}\) we see that \(\alpha\) is algebraic over \(\overline{F}_K\text{;}\) it follows from Corollary 1.6.11 that \(\overline{F}_K(\alpha)\) is algebraic over \(\overline{F}_K\text{.}\) Since \(\overline{F}_K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) it follows from Corollary 1.6.13 that \(\overline{F}_K(\alpha)\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) Thus \(\alpha\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) in which case \(\alpha\in \overline{F}_K\) by definition.
    We have shown that any nonconstant polynomial \(f\in \overline{F}_K[x]\) has a root in \(\overline{F}_K\text{.}\) We conclude (using Remark 1.8.2) that \(\overline{F}_K\) is algebraically closed, as desired.

    Corollary 1.8.6. Algebraically closed and algebraic closure.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(K/F\) is an algebraic closure of \(F\)
    2. \(K/F\) is the splitting field of the set \(\mathcal{P}=F[x]\) of all polynomials over \(F\text{.}\)
    3. \(K/F\) is algebraic and splits the set \(\mathcal{P}=F[x]\) of all polynomials over \(F\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.8.7. Algebraic closure exists.

    Given any field \(F\text{,}\) there is an algebraic closure \(K/F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Fix a field \(F\text{.}\) Our proof will make use of the polynomial ring
    \begin{equation*} R=F[\{ t_{f,j}\}] \end{equation*}
    in infinitely many variables \(t_{f,j}\text{,}\) where \(f\in F[x]\) is a nonconstant polynomial, and \(1\leq j\leq \deg f\text{.}\) In other words, each pair \((f,j)\text{,}\) where \(f\) is a nonconstant polynomial and \(j\) is a positive integer bounded by \(\deg f\text{,}\) is associated with a unique variable \(t_{f,j}\text{.}\) Without getting too technical, the polynomial ring \(F[\{t_{f,j}\}]\) is defined as the set of all finite polynomial combinations of the infinite variable set \(\{t_{f,j}\colon f\in F[x]-F, 1\leq j\leq \deg f\}\text{.}\)
    Next we will define an ideal \(I\) of \(R\text{.}\) For each monic nonconstant polynomial \(f\in F[x]\text{,}\) let
    \begin{align} g_f(x)\amp =f(x)-\prod_{j=1}^{n=\deg f}(x-t_{f,j}) \tag{1.11}\\ \amp =g_{f,n}x^n+g_{f,n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots +g_{f,1}x+g_{f,0}\tag{1.12}\\ \amp \in R[x]=F[\{t_{f,j}\}][x] \text{.}\tag{1.13} \end{align}
    Observe that the coefficients \(g_{f,j}\) here are polynomials in the variables \(\{t_{f,1},t_{f,2},\dots ,t_{f,n}\}\text{.}\) With this notation in place we define \(I\) to be the ideal generated by the polynomials \(g_{f,t}\in R\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} I=(\{g_{f,j}\colon f\in F[x]-F \text{ monic }, 1\leq j\leq \deg f\})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Having assembled all the necessary ingredients, the rest of the proof proceeds as follows:
    1. Show that \(I\ne R\text{,}\) and thus is contained in a maximal ideal \(\mathfrak{m}\leq R \text{.}\)
    2. Show that the field \(K=R/\mathfrak{m}\) is an algebraic extension of \(F\) that splits \(F[x]\text{.}\)
    Once we have shown (2), we conclude from Corollary 1.8.6 that \(K/F\) is an algebraic closure.

    Proof: \(I\ne R\).

    Assume by contradiction that \(I=R\text{.}\) This would mean that we have
    \begin{equation} 1=q_1g_{f_1,j_1}+q_2g_{f_2,j_2}+\cdots +q_mg_{f_m, j_m}\tag{1.14} \end{equation}
    for some \(f_1,f_2,\dots, f_m\in F[x]-F\) and \(g_{f_k,j_k}\in I\text{.}\) By Corollary 1.7.9 there is a splitting field \(L/F\) of the \(f_k\text{.}\) For each \(1\leq k\leq m\text{,}\) pick a root \(\alpha_k\in L\) of \(f_k\) and define
    \begin{equation*} \phi\colon R=F[\{t_{f,j}\}]\rightarrow L \end{equation*}
    to be the evaluation map that for each \(f_k\) sends \(t_{f_k,j}\mapsto \alpha_{k,j}\) for each \(1\leq j\leq \deg f_k\text{,}\) where \(\alpha_{k,j}\) are the roots of \(f_k\text{;}\) and which sends all other variables \(t_{f,j}\) to \(0\text{.}\) We claim that
    \begin{equation} \phi(g_{f_k, j_k})=0\tag{1.15} \end{equation}
    for all \(k\text{.}\) Assuming this is so, we have reached a contradiction since then
    \begin{equation*} \phi(1)=\phi(q_1)\phi(g_{f_1,j_1})+\cdots +\phi(q_m)\phi(g_{f_m,j_m})=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To see why the claim ((1.15)) is true, we first extend the map \(\phi\) as usual to a map
    \begin{equation*} \phi\colon R[x]\rightarrow L[x] \end{equation*}
    by sending \(x\) to \(x\text{.}\) For each \(g_{f_k}\) as in ((1.12)), we have
    \begin{align*} \phi(g_{f_k})\amp=f_k(x)-\prod_{j=1}^{\deg f_{k}}(x-\phi(t_{f_k,j})) \\ \amp=f_k(x)-\prod_{j=1}^{\deg f_{k}}(x-\alpha_{k,j})\\ \amp = 0\text{,} \end{align*}
    since we have
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\prod_{j=1}^{\deg f_k}(x-\alpha_{k,j}) \end{equation*}
    in \(L\text{.}\) On the other hand, we have
    \begin{equation*} \phi(g_{f_k})=\phi(g_{f_k,n})x^n+\cdots +\phi(g_{f_k,1})x+\phi(g_{f_k,0})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    For the polynomial on the right-hand side to be equal to 0 in \(L[x]\text{,}\) we must have the coefficients \(\phi(g_{f_k, j})=0\) for all \(j\text{,}\) as claimed.

    \(K=R/\mathfrak{m}\) is algebraic and splits \(F[x]\).

    Since \(I\ne R\text{,}\) there is a maximal ideal \(\mathfrak{m}\supseteq I\text{.}\) Let \(K=R/\mathfrak{m}\text{.}\) As usual, the resriction to \(F\) of the quotient map \(R\rightarrow R/\mathfrak{m}\) is injective, and we identify \(F\) with its isomorphic image in \(K=R/\mathfrak{m}\text{.}\)
    First we show that \(K\) splits all nonconstant polynomials in \(F[x]\text{.}\) Clearly it suffices to show that \(K\) splits all nonconstant monic polynomials \(f\in F[x]\text{.}\) To see why this is true, first observe that the quotient map \(\pi\colon R\rightarrow R/\mathfrak{m}\) extends to a map of polynomial rings
    \begin{equation*} \pi \colon R[x]\rightarrow R/\mathfrak{m}[x] \end{equation*}
    sending \(x\) to \(x\text{.}\) For each monic nonconstant polynomial \(f\in F[x]\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \pi(g_f) \amp =f(x)-\prod_{j=1}^{\deg f}(x-\overline{t_{f,j}})\\ \amp =\overline{g_{f,n}}x^n+\cdots +\overline{g_{f,1}}x+\overline{g_{f,0}}\\ \amp = 0\text{,} \end{align*}
    since by definition of \(I\) we have \(g_{f,j}\in I\leq \mathfrak{m}\) and thus \(\overline{g_{f,j}}=0\in K=R/\mathfrak{m}\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\prod_{j=1}^{\deg f}(x-\overline{t_{f,j}})\in K[x]\text{,} \end{equation*}
    showing that \(f(x)\) splits completely in \(K[x]\text{.}\)
    Lastly, we show that \(K=R/\mathfrak{m}\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) Since \(R\) is generated as a ring over \(F\) by the \(t_{f,j}\text{,}\) \(R/\mathfrak{m}\) is generated as a ring over \(F\) by the images \(\overline{t_{f,j}}\text{.}\) It follows that \(K\) is generated as a field over \(F\) by the \(\overline{t_{f,j}}\text{:}\) i.e., \(K=F(\{\overline{t_{f,j}})\text{.}\) We saw above that \(\overline{t_{f,j}}\) is a root of the polynomial \(f\in F[x]-F\text{.}\) Thus each \(\overline{t_{f,j}}\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) But then \(K=F(\{\overline{t_{f,j}})\) is algebraically generated over \(F\text{,}\) and hence algebraic by Corollary 1.6.11.

    Notation 1.8.8. Algebraic closure.

    As we will see later, an algebraic closure of \(F\) is unique up to isomorphism. As such, we will often take liberties and speak of “the” algebraic closure of \(F\text{,}\) and will denote this as \(\overline{F}\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.8.9. Splitting field of arbitrary set.

    Let \(F\) be a field. Given any set \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\text{,}\) there is a splitting field \(K/F\) of \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(\Fbar/F\) be an algebraic closure of \(F\text{.}\) Since \(\Fbar\) splits \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) Theorem 1.7.6 tells us that the field \(K=F(A)\) is a splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} A=\{\alpha\in \Fbar\colon f(\alpha)=0 \text{ for some } f\in \mathcal{P}\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
