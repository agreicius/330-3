    Section 1.7 Splitting fields

    Definition 1.7.1. Split completely.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. A polynomial \(f\in F[x]\) splits completely over \(K\) if \(f\) factors over \(K\) as
    \begin{equation} f(x)=c(x-\alpha_1)(x-\alpha_2)\cdots (x-\alpha_n)\text{,}\tag{1.10} \end{equation}
    for some integer \(n\geq 0\text{,}\) constant \(c\in F\text{,}\) and elements \(\alpha_i\in K\text{.}\) We say that the field \(K\) splits the polynomial \(f\) in this case.
    More generally, if \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\) is a set of polynomials, we say that \(K\) splits \(\mathcal{P}\) if it splits each polynomial \(f\in \mathcal{P}\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.7.2. Constant polynomials.

    Observe that according to our definition any constant polynomial \(f(x)=c\in F[x]\) splits over \(F\) itself.

    Example 1.7.3. Split completely.

    1. Show that the polynomial \(f(x)=x^2-2\) splits completely over the field \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\)
    2. Show that the polynomial \(f(x)=x^4-1\) splits completely over \(\Q(i)\text{.}\)
    3. Show that the polynomial \(f(x)=x^3-2\) does not split completely over \(K=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\text{.}\)
    Solution.

    Definition 1.7.4. Splitting field.

    Let \(F\) be a field, and let \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\) be a set of polynomials over \(F\text{.}\) A field extension \(K/F\) is called a splitting field of the collection \(\mathcal{P}\) if the following conditions hold.
    1. \(K\) splits \(\mathcal{P}\).
      For all \(f\in \mathcal{P}\text{,}\) \(f\) splits completely in \(K\text{.}\)
    2. \(K\) minimal.
      If \(K'/F\) is a subextension of \(K/F\) such that \(f\) splits completely in \(K'\) for all \(f\in \mathcal{P}\text{,}\) then \(K'=K\text{.}\) In other words, there is no proper subextension of \(K;\) that splits \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\)
    When \(\mathcal{P}=\{f_1,f_2,\dots, f_n\}\) is finite, or is described as an indexed set \(\mathcal{P}=\{f_i\colon i\in I\}\text{,}\) we will call a splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}\) a splitting field of the \(f_i\text{.}\)

    Example 1.7.5. Splitting fields.

    1. Show that \(\Q(\sqrt{2})\) is a splitting field of \(f(x)=x^2-2\text{.}\)
    2. Show that \(\Q(i)\) is a splitting field of \(x^4-1\text{.}\)
    3. Show that \(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2}, \zeta)\) is a splitting field of \(f(x)=x^3-2\text{,}\) where \(\zeta=e^{2\pi i/3}\text{,}\) a primitive cube-root of unity.
    Bit by bit we will be able to prove that (a) given any field \(F\) and collection of polynomials \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\text{,}\) there is indeed a splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) and furthermore (b) this splitting field is basically unique up to isomorphism.

    Theorem 1.7.6. Splitting field generated by roots.

    Let \(F\) be a field, let \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\) a set of polynomials, and suppose \(K/F\) is a field extension that splits \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\) Define
    \begin{equation*} A=\{\alpha\in K\colon f(\alpha)=0 \text{ for some } f\in \mathcal{P}\}\text{:} \end{equation*}
    i.e., \(A\) is the set of all the roots of all the polynomials in \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\) The field \(F(A)\) generated over \(F\) by all the roots of the polynomials in \(\mathcal{P}\) is a splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    It is clear that \(F(A)\) splits \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) since \(A\) contains all the roots of all the polynomials of \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\) We must show that \(F(A)/F\) contains no proper subextension \(K'/F\) that splits \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\) This is clear: if \(K'/F\) is a subextension of \(F(A)/F\)that splits \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) then \(K'\) must contain all roots of all polynomials of \(\mathcal{P}\text{;}\) that is, \(K'\) must contain \(A\text{.}\) Since by definition \(F(A)\) is the smallest extension of \(K/F\) containing \(F\) and \(A\text{,}\) we have \(F(A)\leq K'\text{.}\) Since \(K'\leq F(A)\text{,}\) we conclude \(K'=F(A)\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.7.7. Splitting fields are algebraic.

    Let \(F\) be a field, let \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\) be a set of polynomials. If \(K/F\) is a splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) then \(K/F\) is algebraic.

    Proof.

    Clearly \(K\) splits \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.7.6, we have \(K=F(A)\text{,}\) where \(A\) is the set of all roots of all polynomials of \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\) Since each element of \(A\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) we see by Corollary 1.6.11, that \(K=F(A)\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.7.8. Splitting field of polynomial.

    Let \(F\) be a field. Any nonzero polynomial \(f\in F[x]\) of degree \(n\) has a splitting field \(K/F\) satisfying \([K\colon F]\leq n!\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    We proceed by induction on \(n=\deg f\text{.}\)
    Base step: \(n=0\text{.}\) In this case \(f(x)=c\ne 0\) splits over \(F\text{,}\) in which case the splitting field of \(f\) is \(K=F\text{,}\) which satisfies \([F\colon F]=1=0!\text{.}\)
    Induction step: fix \(n\geq 1\text{,}\) and assume the result holds for any field and any polynomial \(g\) over that field of degree \(n-1\text{.}\) Suppose \(f\in F[x]\) satisfies \(\deg f=n\text{.}\) Let \(p(x)\) be any irreducible factor of \(f\) over \(F\text{.}\) By Corollary 1.4.8 the quotient \(F[x]/(p(x))\) is a field extension of \(F\text{,}\) satisfies \(F[x]/(p(x))=F(\alpha_1)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha_1=\overline{x}\) is a root of \(p(x)\text{,}\) and has degree \([F(\alpha_1)\colon F]=\deg p\leq n\text{.}\) Since \(\alpha_1\) is a root of \(p(x)\text{,}\) it is also a root of \(f(x)\text{,}\) and we have \(f(x)=(x-\alpha_1)g(x)\) for some nonzero polynomial \(g(x)\in F(\alpha)[x]\text{,}\) satisfying \(\deg g=\deg f-1=n-1\text{.}\) By induction there is a splitting field \(L/F(\alpha_1)\) of \(g(x)\) satisfying \([L\colon F(\alpha_1)]\leq (n-1)!\text{.}\) Since \(L\) splits \(g(x)\) and contains the root \(\alpha\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=c(x-\alpha_1)(x-\alpha_2)\cdots (x-\alpha_n) \end{equation*}
    for some \(c\in F\) and elements \(\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\in L\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.7.6, the subextension \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\) is a splitting field of \(f\) over \(F\) and satisfies
    \begin{equation*} [K\colon F]\leq [L\colon F]=[L\colon F(\alpha_1)][F(\alpha_1)\colon F]\leq (n-1)!n=n!\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Corollary 1.7.9. Splitting field of finite set.

    Let \(F\) be a field, and let \(\mathcal{P}=\{f_1, f_2,\dots, f_n\}\) be a finite set of nonzero polynomials. A splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}\) exists.

    Proof.

    By Theorem 1.7.8, the nonzero polynomial
    \begin{equation*} f=f_1f_2\cdots f_n\in F[x] \end{equation*}
    has a splitting field \(K/F\text{.}\) It is clear that \(K\) is also a splitting field of \(\{f_1,f_2,\dots, f_n\}\text{.}\)
