    Section 0.4 Group theory

    In this section we collect some facts you may or may not have learned when studying group theory. These will be useful in our exploration of Galois theory.

    Subsection Dihedral groups

    The \(n\)-th dihedral group \(D_n\) is sometime defined geometrically as the group of all rotations and permutations of a regular \(n\)-gon \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq \R^2\) centered at the origin and including \((1,0)\) as one of its vertices. For example, \(D_4\) is the group of rotations and reflections of the square with vertices \((1,0),(0,1),(-1,0),(0,-1)\text{.}\) It is typically shown that these sets constitute a group under composition of functions, that \(\abs{D_n}=2n\text{,}\) and that \(D_n\) is generated by elements \(r\) and \(s\text{,}\) where \(r\) is rotation by \(\theta=2\pi/n\) and \(s\) is reflection through the \(x\)-axis.
    This geometric definition of \(D_n\) has the advantage of being concrete and easy to visualize, and the disadvantage of being difficult to work with when computing examples. An alternative approach defines \(D_n\) more formally as the set \(2n\) distinct expressions \(r^js^k\text{,}\) where \(0\leq j\leq n-1\) and \(0\leq k\leq 1\text{.}\) The group operation is then “summarized” as follows: given two elements \(g_1=r^{j_1}s^{k_1}\) and \(g_2=r^{j_2}s^{k_2}\text{,}\) we compute their product
    \begin{equation*} g_1g_2=r^{j_3}s^{i_3} \end{equation*}
    by using the simplification rules
    \begin{align*} r^n \amp =s^2=1 \amp sr\amp=r^{n-1}s\text{.} \end{align*}
    From these simplification rules we can further derive the following inverse rules:
    \begin{align*} r^{-1} \amp = r^{n-1} \amp (r^{j})^{-1}\amp = r^{n-j} \amp s^{-1}\amp= s\text{.} \end{align*}

    Example 0.4.1. Dihedral group arithmetic.

    Let \(G=D_{12}=\{r^{j}s^{k}\colon 0\leq j\leq 11, 0\leq s\leq 1\}\text{,}\) let \(g=r^{5}s\text{,}\) and let \(h=r^{4}\)
    1. Compute \(gh\) and \(hg\text{,}\) expressed in the form \(r^{j}s^k\text{.}\)
    2. Compute \(g^{-1}\) and \(h^{-1}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. Since \(sr=r^{n-1}s=r^{-1}s\text{,}\) it follows easily by induction that \(sr^k=r^{n-k}s=r^{-k}s\text{.}\) Thus
      \begin{align*} gh \amp =(r^5s)r^4 \\ \amp = r^5(sr^4)\\ \amp = r^5(r^{8}s)\\ \amp = r^{13}s\\ \amp = rs \end{align*}
      and
      \begin{align*} hg \amp = r^4(r^5s)\\ \amp = r^9 s\text{.} \end{align*}
    2. Using the inverse rules above, along with general inverse properties of groups we compute
      \begin{align*} g^{-1} \amp = (r^5s)^{-1}=s^{-1}r^{-5}\\ \amp = sr^7\\ \amp = r^{5}s\\ h^{-1} \amp = (r^4)^{-1}\\ \amp = r^{-4}\\ \amp = r^{8}\text{.} \end{align*}
    As you might guess, this description of a group operation by way of “simplification rules” is not entirely rigorous. In particular, who is to say that the resulting operation is even associative? The theory of free groups and group presentations is what is needed to put this construction on firm ground. We will not go into this theory here, and will instead content ourselves with declaring that our formal description of \(D_n\) does in fact provide a well-defined group. We will also make use of group presentation notation to summarize our definition of \(D_n\) as follows:
    \begin{equation*} D_n=\angvec{r,s\colon r^n=s^2=1, sr=r^{n-1}s=r^{-1}s}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This notation reads as follows: “\(D_n\) is the unique group generated by elements \(r\) and \(s\) satisfying the relations \(r^n=s^2=1\) and \(sr=r^{n-1}s=r^{-1}s\)”.
    The following theorem is extremely useful for producing a group homomorphism \(\phi\colon D_n\rightarrow G\) from a dihedral group to some target group \(G\text{.}\) It says (a) that any such homomorphism is uniquely determined by the images \(\phi(r)=g_r\) and \(\phi(s)=g_s\text{,}\) since then we must have \(\phi(r^{j}s^k)=g_r^{j}g_s^{k}\)

    Theorem 0.4.2. Universal mapping property of dihedral groups.

    Fix an integer \(n\geq 2\text{,}\) let \(D_{n}=\angvec{r,s}\) be the \(n\)-th dihedral group, and let \(G\) be a group.
    1. If \(g_r\) and \(g_s\) are elements of \(G\) satisfying
      \begin{equation} g_r^{n}=g_s^2=e, \hspace{5pt} g_sg_r=g_r^{n-1}g_s\text{,}\tag{0.4} \end{equation}
      then the map
      \begin{align*} \phi\colon D_n \amp \rightarrow G\\ r^{j}s^{k} \amp \mapsto g_r^jg_s^k \end{align*}
      is a group homomorphism with image
      \begin{equation*} \im \phi=\angvec{g_r,g_s}\leq G\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. A group homomorphism \(\phi\colon D_n\rightarrow G\) is uniquely determined by where it sends the generators \(r\) and \(s\text{:}\) i.e., \(\phi\) is uniquely determined by \(\phi(r)=g_r\) and \(\phi(s)=g_s\text{.}\) Furthermore, the elements \(g_r\) and \(g_s\) must satisfy the relations (0.4).
    3. The correspondence
      \begin{equation*} \phi \longleftrightarrow (\phi(r),\phi(s))=(g_r,g_s) \end{equation*}
      is a bijection between group homomorphisms \(\phi\colon D_n\rightarrow G\) and pairs of elements \(g_r, g_s\in G\) satisfying relations (0.4).

    Example 0.4.3. \(D_3\) is isomorphic to \(S_3\).

    Use Theorem 0.4.2 to show that \(D_3\) is isomorphic to \(S_3\)
    Solution.
    Using cycle notation for \(S_3\text{,}\) it is easy to verify that the elements \(\rho=(123)\) and \(\sigma=(12)\) satisfy the following relations:
    \begin{align*} \rho^3 \amp =\sigma^2=e \amp \sigma \rho=\rho^2\sigma\text{.} \end{align*}
    According to Theorem 0.4.2 there is a homomorphism \(\phi\colon D_3\rightarrow S_3\) satisfying \(\phi(r)=\rho, \phi(s)=\sigma\text{,}\) and \(\im \phi=\angvec{\rho,\sigma}\leq S_3\text{.}\) (The full formula for \(\phi\) is \(\phi(r^js^k)=\rho^js^k\text{.}\)) Since \(\angvec{\rho,\sigma}=S_3\text{,}\) we see that \(\phi\) is surjective. Since \(\abs{D_3}=\abs{S_3}=6\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\phi\) is bijective, hence an isomorphism. This proves \(D_3\cong S_3\text{.}\)

    Subsection Euler function and groups of units

    Definition 0.4.4. Euler totient function.

    The Euler totient function is the function \(\phi\colon \Z_+\rightarrow \Z_+\) defined as
    \begin{equation*} \phi(n)=\abs{\{k\in \{1,2,\dots, n\}\colon \gcd(k,n)=1\}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, \(\phi(n)\) is the number of integers between \(1\) and \(n\) that are relatively prime to \(n\text{.}\)

    Theorem 0.4.5.

    The Euler totient function \(\phi\) satisfies the following properties.
    1. If \(\gcd(a,b)=1\text{,}\) then \(\phi(ab)=\phi(a)\phi(b)\text{.}\)
    2. If \(p\) is a prime integer, then
      \begin{align*} \phi(p) \amp = p-1\\ \phi(p^{n}) \amp = p^n-p^{n-1}=p^{n-1}(p-1)\text{.} \end{align*}

    Theorem 0.4.6. Unit groups modulo \(n\).

    Let \(m\) be a positive integer, and let \((Z/m\Z)^*\) be the group of multiplicative units of \(\Z/m\Z\text{.}\)
    1. \(\abs{(\Z/m\Z)^*}=\phi(m)\text{.}\)
    2. If \(\gcd(a,b)=1\text{,}\) then \((\Z/ab\Z)^*\cong (\Z/a\Z)^*\times (\Z/b\Z)^*\text{.}\)
    3. If \(p\) is an odd prime, then \((\Z/p^n\Z)^*\) is a cyclic group of cardinality \(p^{n-1}(p-1)\text{.}\)
    4. For \(p=2\text{,}\) we have \(\Z/2\Z\cong \Z/4\Z\cong \angvec{-1}\text{,}\) and \(\Z/2^n\Z\cong \angvec{-1}\times \angvec{5}\text{.}\) In particular, \(\angvec{5}\) is a cyclic group of cardinality \(2^{n-2}\text{.}\)
