    Section 1.4 Simple extensions

    Definition 1.4.1. Simple extension.

    Let \(L/F\) be a field extension. A subextension \(K/F\) is called a simple extension of \(F\) if \(K=F(\alpha)\) for some \(\alpha\in L\text{.}\)
    In plain English, a simple extension of \(F\) is one generated over \(F\) by a single element.

    Fiat 1.4.2. Generated fields convention.

    Let \(F\) be a field. In order to speak of a field of the form \(K=F(\alpha)\) or \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\text{,}\) we technically need to introduce a larger “ambient” field \(L/F\) where these elements \(\alpha\) and \(\alpha_i\) are drawn from.
    In situations where this ambient field \(L\) does not play an important role, we reserve the right to omit it from our discussion. For example, if introducing a simple field extension \(F(\alpha)/F\) without further details, it is assumed that in fact \(F\leq F(\alpha)\leq L\) for some larger field \(L\text{.}\)

    Example 1.4.3. Simple extensions.

    1. The extension \(\C/\R\) is simple, since \(\C=\R(i)\text{.}\)
    2. For any field \(F\text{,}\) the field of rational functions \(F(x)\) in one variable is a simple field extension of \(F\text{.}\)
      On the other hand it is possible to show that the field of rational functions \(F(x,y)\) in two variables is not simple: i.e., we do not have \(F(x,y)=F(q)\) for any rational function \(q(x,y)=f(x,y)/g(x,y)\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.4.4. Generated ring versus generated field.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension, and let \(\alpha\in K\text{.}\) Recall that \(F[\alpha]\) denotes the subring of \(K\) generated by \(\alpha\) over \(F\text{.}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle F[\alpha]\leq F(\alpha)\)
    2. \(F[\alpha]=F(\alpha)\) if and only if \(F[\alpha]\) is a field.

    Proof.

    1. By definition \(F[\alpha]\) is the intersection of all subrings containing \(F\) and \(\alpha\text{,}\) and thus is a subset of \(F(\alpha)\) which is, the intersection of all subfields containing \(F\) and \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    2. Clearly, if \(F[\alpha]=F(\alpha)\text{,}\) then \(F[\alpha]\) is a field. Assume on the other hand that \(F[\alpha]\) is a field. Since \(F[\alpha]\) is a field containing \(F\) and \(\alpha\text{,}\) and since \(F(\alpha)\) is the smallest such field, we have \(F(\alpha)\leq F[\alpha]\text{.}\) But by (1) we always have \(F[\alpha]\leq F(\alpha)\text{.}\) Thus \(F[\alpha]=F(\alpha)\text{.}\)

    Notation 1.4.5. Quotients of polynomial rings.

    Let \(F\) be a field, let \(f(x)\) be a nonconstant polynomial in \(F[x]\text{,}\) and let \(\pi\colon F[x]\rightarrow F[x]/(f)\) be the corresponding quotient ring homomorphism.
    1. Since \(\deg f\geq 1\text{,}\) we have \((f)\cap F=(0)\text{,}\) and hence the restriction of \(\pi\) to \(F\leq F[x]\) is injective. As such \(\pi(F)\cong F\text{,}\) and we identify \(F\) with its isomorphic image \(\pi(F)\leq F[x]/(f)\text{.}\)
    2. Given \(g(x)=\bmpoly\in F[x]\) we denote \(\pi(g)=\overline{g}\) for short. We have
      \begin{align*} \overline{g} \amp =\overline{b_m}\overline{x}^m+\overline{b_{m-1}}\overline{x}^{m-1}+\cdots +\overline{b_1}\overline{x}+\overline{b_0} \amp (\pi \text{ is ring homo.})\\ \amp =b_m\overline{x}^m+b_{m-1}\overline{x}^{m-1}+\cdots +b_1\overline{x}+b_0 \amp (\text{identify } F \text{ with } \pi(F))\text{.} \end{align*}

    Notation 1.4.6. Maps between polynomial rings.

    A ring homomorphism \(\phi\colon R\rightarrow S\) extends naturally to a ring homomorphism \(R[x] \longrightarrow S[x]\) that sends an \(R\)-polynomial \(\anpoly\) to the \(S\)-polynomial \(\phi(a_n)x^n+\phi(a_{n-1})x^{n-1}+\cdots +\phi(a_1)x+\phi(a_0)\text{.}\) By abuse of notation, we will also denote this map of polynonmial rings as \(\phi\text{.}\) Thus, given \(f(x)=\anpoly\) we will write
    \begin{equation*} \phi(f)=\phi(a_n)x^n+\phi(a_{n-1})x^{n-1}+\cdots +\phi(a_1)x+\phi(a_0)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.4.7. Quotients of polynomial rings.

    Let \(F\) be a field and let \(f(x)=\anpoly\) be a nonconstant polynomial in \(F[x]\text{.}\) Following Notation 1.4.5, given \(g\in F[x]\text{,}\) we denote by \(\overline{g}\) its image in \(F[x]/(f)\) under the quotient map.
    1. Simply generated.
      We have \(F[x]/(f(x))=F[\overline{x}]\text{:}\) i.e., \(F[x]/(f)\) is generated over \(F\) as a ring by \(\overline{x}\text{,}\) the image of \(x\) under the quotient map.
    2. Dimension.
      Let \(n=\deg f\text{.}\) The set \(B=\{1,\overline{x},\dots, \overline{x}^{n-1}\}\) is a basis of \(F[x]/(f)\) as a vector space over \(F\text{,}\) and thus \(\dim_F F[x]/(f)=\abs{B}=n\text{.}\)
    3. Mapping property.
      Let \(S\) be a nonzero ring. The set of all ring homomorphisms \(\overline{\phi}\colon F[x]/(f)\rightarrow S\) is in bijective correspondence with pairs \((\phi, s)\) where \(\phi\colon F\rightarrow S\) is a ring homomorphism and \(s\in S\) is a root of
      \begin{equation*} \phi(f)=\phi(a_n)x^n+\phi(a_{n-1})x^{n-1}+\cdots +\phi(a_1)x+\phi(a_0)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Indeed, given such a pair \((\phi, s)\text{,}\) we define
      \begin{align*} \overline{\phi}\colon F[x]/(f) \amp \longrightarrow S\\ b_m\overline{x}^m+b_{m-1}\overline{x}^{m-1}+\cdots +b_1\overline{x}+b_0 \amp\longmapsto \phi(b_m)s^m+\phi(b_{m-1})s^{m-1}+\cdots +\phi(b_1)s+\phi(b_0) \text{.} \end{align*}

    Proof.

    The proof of this result relies on various bits of theory from general ring theory. It is left as an (excellent) exercises.

    Corollary 1.4.8. Quotients of polynomial rings.

    Let \(F\) be a field, let \(f(x)\) be an irreducible polynomial of degree \(n\text{,}\) and let \(K=F[x]/(f)\text{.}\) Following Notation 1.4.5, we identify \(F\) with its image in \(K\) under the quotient map.
    1. \(K\) is a field and the extension \(K/F\) is simple. In fact, we have \(K=F[\overline{x}]=F(\overline{x})\text{.}\)
    2. We have \([K\colon F]=\deg f=n\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The corollary is a direct consequence of Theorem 1.4.7.

    Example 1.4.9. Field of cardinality four.

    Let \(f(x)=x^2+x+1\in \F_2[x]\text{.}\) Prove that \(\F_2[x]/(f)\) is a field of cardinality four.
    Solution.
    Denote \(\F_2=\{0,1\}\text{.}\) Since \(\deg f=2\text{,}\) it is irreducible over \(\F_2\) if and only if it has a root in \(\F_2\text{.}\) Since \(f(0)=f(1)=1\ne 0 \text{,}\) we see that \(f\) is irreducible. Using Corollary 1.4.8, we conclude that \(K=\F_2[x]/(f)\) is field, and that \(B=\{\overline{x}, 1\}\) is a basis of \(K\) over \(\F_2\text{.}\) It follows that every element of \(K\) can be uniquely expressed as \(a\overline{x}+b\text{,}\) with \(a,b\in \F_2\text{.}\) There are exactly four such expressions:
    \begin{equation*} 0=0\overline{x}+0, 1=0\overline{x}+1, \overline{x}=1\overline{x}+0, \overline{x}+1=1\overline{x}+1\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.4.10. Quotients of \(\R[x]\).

    Let \(f(x)\in \R[x]\) be any irreducible quadratic polynomial. Prove that \(\R[x]/(f)\cong \C\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Since \(f\) is irreducible, the quotient \(K=\R[x]/(f)\) is a field. Since \(\deg f=2\text{,}\) we have \(K=\{r+s\overline{x}\colon r,s\in\R\}\text{.}\) Let \(z_0\in \C\) be a root of \(f(x)\text{.}\) Using Theorem 1.4.7, the map \(\phi\colon K\rightarrow \C\) defined as \(\phi(r+s\overline{x})=r+sz_0\) is a ring homomorphism. Furthermore, since \(K\) is a field, the map \(\phi\) is injective, and thus we have \(K\cong \phi(K)\leq \C\text{.}\) We claim \(\phi(K)=\C\text{.}\) This is a result of the fact that \(\phi\) is a linear transformation of \(\R\)-vector spaces: i.e., for any \(r,s\in \R\) and \(\alpha=r_1+s_1\overline{x}, \beta=r_2+s_2\overline{x}\text{,}\) we have \(\phi(r\alpha+s\beta)=r\phi(\alpha)+s\phi(\beta)\text{,}\) as you can easily check. It then follows that \(K\cong \phi(K)\) as \(\R\)-vector spaces, and thus that \(\dim_\R \phi(K)=2\text{.}\) Since \(\phi(K)\leq \C\) and \(\dim_\R \phi(K)=\dim_\R \C=2\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\phi(K)=\C\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.4.11. Algebraic and transcendental elements.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. An element \(\alpha\in K\) is algebraic over \(F\) if there is a nonzero polynomial \(f(x)\in F[x]\) such that \(f(\alpha)=0\text{;}\) an element \(\alpha\in K\) is transcendental over \(F\) if it is not algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)

    Example 1.4.12. Some transcendental elements of \(\C/\Q\).

    In this course we will take for granted that the real numbers \(\pi\) and \(e\) are transcendental over \(\Q\text{.}\) It is in fact not exceedingly difficult to prove this, though the proofs tend to be somewhat long.
    It is a little easier to show, using Liouville’s theorem (from number theory), that the real number
    \begin{equation*} \alpha=\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{10^{n!}}=.110001\underset{x17}{\underbrace{00\dots 0}}1\dots \end{equation*}
    is transcendental over \(\Q\text{.}\)

    Example 1.4.13. Uncountably many transcendental numbers.

    Consider the field extension \(\C/\Q\text{.}\) We will show that in fact \(\C\) contains uncountably many elements that are transcendental over \(\Q\text{.}\) (The argument below is originally due to Cantor, Godfather of uncountability arguments.)
    Solution.
    Let
    \begin{align*} A\amp = \{\alpha\in \C\colon \alpha \text{ algebraic over } \Q \} \\ T \amp = \{\alpha\in \C\colon \alpha \text{ transcendental over } \Q \} \text{,} \end{align*}
    so that \(\C\) is the disjoint union of \(A\) and \(T\text{.}\) We will show that the set \(A\) is countable, from whence it follows that \(T\) must be uncountable, since \(\C=A\cup T\) is uncountable.
    So why is \(A\) countable? Each element of \(A\) is the root of a polynomial \(f\in \Q[x]\text{.}\) First we show that the set \(\Q[x]\) is countable. This is because \(\Q[x]=\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty P_n\text{,}\) where \(P_n\) is the set of all rational polynomials of degree at most \(n\text{.}\) Each set \(P_n\) is countable (\(P_n\) is in bijection with \(\Q^n\text{,}\) which is countable), and thus \(\Q[x]\text{,}\) being a countable union of countable sets, is countable. Lastly, we have
    \begin{equation*} A=\bigcup_{f\in \Q[x]}r_f\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(r_f=\{\alpha\in \C\colon f(\alpha)=0\}\) is the set of all roots of \(f\text{.}\) Since each \(r_f\) is finite, and since \(\Q[x]\) is countable, we see that \(A\) is a countable union of finite sets, and hence is countable.

    Theorem 1.4.14. Simple extensions.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension and let \(\alpha\in K\text{.}\)
    1. The following statements are equivalent.
      1. \(\alpha\) is algebraic.
      2. \(F[\alpha]=F(\alpha)\cong F[x]/(f)\) for some irreducible polynomial \(f(x)\in F[x]\text{.}\)
      3. \(F(\alpha)/F\) is finite: i.e., \([F(\alpha)\colon F]< \infty\text{.}\)
      Furthermore, when \(\alpha\) is algebraic, there is a basis of \(F(\alpha)\) of the form \(B=\{1,\alpha, \alpha^2,\dots, \alpha^{n-1}\}\text{.}\)
    2. The following statements are equivalent.
      1. \(\alpha\) is transcendental.
      2. \(F[\alpha]\cong F[x]\) and \(F(\alpha)\cong F(x)\text{.}\)
      3. \(F(\alpha)/F\) is infinite: i.e., \([F(\alpha)\colon F]=\infty\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The key to proving both statements of the theorem is to understand the “evaluation at \(\alpha\)” ring homomorphism
    \begin{align*} \phi \colon F[x] \amp \rightarrow K\\ f(x) \amp \mapsto f(\alpha)\text{.} \end{align*}
    Note first that we have
    \begin{equation*} \phi(F[x])\cong F[x]/\ker \phi\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which implies \(\ker \phi\) is a prime ideal of \(F[x]\) (since \(\phi(F[x])\leq K\) is an integral domain). It follows that \(\ker\phi=(0)\) or \(\ker \phi=(f)\) for some irreducible polynomial \(f\in F[x]\text{.}\) Furthermore, since
    \begin{equation*} \ker\phi=\{f\in F[x]\colon f(\alpha)=0\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    we see immediately that the element \(\alpha\) is transcendental if and only if \(\ker\phi=(0)\) if and only if \(\phi\) is injective. Similarly, \(\alpha\) is algebraic if and only if \(\ker \phi=(f)\) for some irreducible polynomial \(f\text{.}\)
    Lastly, observe that
    \begin{equation*} \phi(F[x])=\{\phi(f)\colon f\in F[x]\}=\{f(\alpha)\colon f(x)\in F[x]\}=F[\alpha]\text{,} \end{equation*}
    the subring of \(K\) generated over \(F\) by \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    Having given a rather thorough analysis of \(\phi\) we are ready to prove statements (1) and (2).

    Proof of (1).

    We have
    \begin{align*} \alpha \text{ algebraic} \amp \iff \phi(F[x])\cong F[x]/(f) \text{ for some irreducible } f\\ \amp \iff F[\alpha]=\phi(F[x])\cong F[x]/(f) \text{ for some irreducible } f\\ \amp \iff F[\alpha]=F(\alpha)\cong F[x]/(f) \text{ for some irreducible } f\text{,} \end{align*}
    where the last equivalence uses the fact that \(F[\alpha]=F(\alpha)\) if and only if \(F[\alpha]\) is a field. (Theorem 1.4.4) This proves (i)\(\iff\)(ii).
    Next, (ii)\(\implies\)(iii) since if \(f\) is irreducible, the quotient \(F[x]/(f)\) is a finite field extension of \(F\) of degree \(n=\deg f\text{.}\)
    Lastly (iii)\(\implies\)(i) since if \(\dim_F F(\alpha)=n\text{,}\) then the set \(\{\alpha^n, \alpha^{n-1},\dots, \alpha, 1\}\) must be linearly dependent, in which case there is a nontrivial linear combination
    \begin{equation*} a_n\alpha^{n}+a_{n-1}\alpha^{n-1}+\cdots +a_1\alpha+a_0=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But then we have \(f(\alpha)=0\text{,}\) where \(f\) is the nonzero polynomial \(f(x)=\anpoly\text{.}\) Thus \(\alpha\) is algebraic, as desired.

    Proof of (2).

    First observe that the equivalence (i)\(\iff\)(iii) follows from (1) above simply by taking negations. Furthermore, we have (ii)\(\implies\)(iii) since \([F(x)\colon F]=\infty\text{,}\) as we saw in Example 1.4.3. Thus it remains only to show (i)\(\implies\)(ii).
    Assume \(\alpha\) is transcendental. We saw above that this is equivalent to our evaluation map \(\phi\) being injective. Thus we have \(F[\alpha]=\phi(F[x])\cong F[x]\text{.}\) Since \(F(x)=\Frac F[x]\text{,}\) we may use Theorem 1.1.9 to get an injective map
    \begin{align*} \widetilde{\phi}\colon F(x) \amp \rightarrow K\\ f(x)/g(x) \amp \mapsto f(\alpha)/g(\alpha)\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus \(\widetilde{\phi}(F(x))\) is a subfield of \(K\) containing \(F\) and \(\alpha\text{.}\) Since in turn any field containing \(F\) and \(\alpha\) must also contain all elements of the form \(f(\alpha)/g(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(f\) and \(g\) are polynomials, we see that \(\widetilde{\phi}(F(x))\) in fact the smallest such field: i.e., we have \(F(\alpha)=\widetilde{\phi}(F(x))\cong F(x)\text{,}\) as desired.
