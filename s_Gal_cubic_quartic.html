    Section 1.21 Cubic and quartic polynomials

    Let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(n\in \{3,4\}\text{.}\) In this section we will introduce means of determining precisely which subgroup of \(S_n\) the Galois group \(G_f\) is isomorphic to. When \(n=3\text{,}\) the subgroup structure of \(S_3\) is simple enough that the discriminant \(\Delta_f\) tells the whole story.

    Theorem 1.21.1. Galois group of cubic.

    Let \(F\) be a field of cardinality not equal to \(2\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(3\text{.}\)
    1. \(G_f\cong A_3\) or \(G_f\cong S_3\text{.}\)
    2. \(G_f\cong A_3\) if and only if \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}\in F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The result follows directly from Theorem 1.20.3, Theorem 1.20.9, and the fact that the only transitive subgroups of \(S_3\) are \(A_3\) and \(S_3\text{.}\)

    Example 1.21.2. Cubic polynomials.

    For each cubic polynomial \(f\text{,}\) identify a subgroup of \(S_3\) that \(G_f\) is isomorphic to.
    1. \(\displaystyle f(x)=x^3-x-1\)
    2. \(\displaystyle f(x)=x^3-3x-1\)
    3. \(\displaystyle f(x)=x^3-4x-1\)

    Example 1.21.3. Family of \(A_3\) cubics.

    Let \(k\) be any integer, and define \(a=k^2+k+7\text{.}\) Show that the polynomial \(f(x)=x^3-ax-a\) is irreducible over \(\Q\) and that its Galois group \(G_f\) is isomorphic to \(A_3\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Note that \(k^2+k=k(k+1)\) is even, and hence \(a\) is odd. It follows that \(f(x)\equiv x^3+x+1\) modulo \(2\text{,}\) which is irreducible. It follows that \(f\) is irreducible over \(Z\text{,}\) and hence \(\Q\text{.}\)
    The discriminant of \(f\) is
    \begin{align*} \Delta_f \amp = -4(-a)^3-27(-a)^2=a^2(4a-27)\\ \amp = a^2(4k^2+4k+1)\\ \amp = \left(a(2k+1)^2\right)^2\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}\in \Q\text{,}\) and we have \(G_f\cong A_3\text{.}\)
    For degree-4 irreducible polynomials we need to first take a closer look at the subgroup structure of \(S_4\text{.}\) According to Theorem 1.20.3 the Galois group \(G_f\) of an separable irreducible quartic polynomial is isomorphic to a transitive subgroup of \(S_4\text{,}\) with cardinality divisible by \(4\text{.}\) We now set about describing all such subgroups. First we look inside \(A_4\text{,}\) whose subgroup lattice is depicted in Figure 1.21.4. The transitive subgroups here are \(A_4\) itself and the subgroup
    \begin{equation*} V=\angvec{(12)(34),(13)(24)}=\{e,(12)(34),(13)(24),(14)(23)\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is isomorphic to the Klein-4 group \(\Z/2\Z\times\Z/2\Z\text{.}\) (The ‘V’ stands for vier, German for “four”.)
    Next we catalogue the transitive subgroups of \(S_4\) that do not lie within \(A_4\text{.}\) The odd permutations of \(S_4\) consist of the six 2-cycles of the the form \((a\, b)\) and the the six 4-cycles of the form \((a\, b\, \,c \, d)\text{.}\) The \(4\)-cycles generate three transitive cyclic subgroups of cardinality \(4\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \angvec{(1234)} \amp=\angvec{(1432)} \\ \angvec{(1243)} \amp =\angvec{(1342)}\\ \angvec{(1324)} \amp =\angvec{(1423)}\text{.} \end{align*}
    For each of these isomorphic copies of \(C_4\text{,}\) adding a judicious choice of \(2\)-cycle yields a subgroup of cardinality \(8\) that is isomorphic to \(D_4\text{.}\) There are three of these copies of \(D_4\) and they are each conjugate:
    \begin{align*} \angvec{(1234),(13)} \amp=\angvec{(1234),(24)}=\angvec{(1234)} \\ \angvec{(1243),(14)} \amp =\angvec{(1243),(23)}\\ \angvec{(1324),(12)} \amp =\angvec{(1324),(34)}\text{.} \end{align*}
    It is not difficult to show that these are all the proper transitive subgroups of \(S_4\) that do not lie within \(A_4\text{.}\) In fact you can show that the remaining proper subgroups of \(S_4\) not lying within \(A_4\) are the six cyclic groups of the form \(\angvec{(ab)}\text{,}\) the three further copies of the Klein-4 group of the form
    \begin{equation*} \{1, (ab),(cd),(ab)(cd)\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\{a,b\}\cap \{c,d\}=\emptyset\text{,}\) and four copies of \(S_3\) of the form
    \begin{equation*} \angvec{(abc),(ab)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and none of these subgroups is transitive. Figure 1.21.5 summarizes the transitive subgroups of \(S_4\) not lying within \(A_4\text{.}\) Together with our analysis of \(A_4\text{,}\) we have more or less justified the complete description of the transitive subgroups of \(S_4\) given in Theorem 1.21.6.

    Theorem 1.21.6. Transitive subgroups of \(S_4\).

    The following is a complete list of the transitive subgroups of \(S_4\text{.}\)
    1. Permutation group \(S_4\).
      The entire group \(S_4\text{.}\)
    2. Alternating subgroup \(A_4\).
      The alternating subgroup \(A_4\text{,}\) which is normal in \(S_4\text{.}\)
    3. Klein-4 group \(V\normalin A_4\).
      A unique isomorphic copy of the Klein-4 group of the form \(V=\angvec{(12)(34),(13)(24)}\text{,}\) which is a normal subgroup of \(A_4\text{.}\)
    4. Cyclic group \(C_4\).
      Three isomorphic copies of the cyclic group \(C_4\) of the form \(\angvec{(abcd)}\text{,}\) all of which are conjugate to one another.
    5. Dihedral group \(D_4\).
      Three isomorphic copies of \(D_4\) of the form \(\angvec{(abcd),(ac)}\text{,}\) all of which are conjugate to one another.
    Surveying the landscape of transitive subgroups of \(S_4\) described in Theorem 1.21.6, we observe that \(S_4\) and \(A_4\) are the only subgroups whose cardinality is divisible by \(3\text{.}\) This means that for \(G_f\) to be isomorphic to \(S_4\) or \(A_4\text{,}\) it must contain an automorphism that, when considered as a permutation of the roots of \(f\text{,}\) acts like a 3-cycle. The cubic resolvent gives us a means of testing whether such an automorphism exists.

    Definition 1.21.7. Cubic resolvent.

    Let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a degree-4 polynomial that factors over an algebraic closure \(\overline{F}\) as
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-r_1)(x-r_2)(x-r_3)(x-r_4)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Define
    \begin{align*} \theta_1 \amp =r_1r_2+r_3r_4 \\ \theta_2 \amp =r_1r_3+r_2r_4\\ \theta_3 \amp =r_1r_4+r_2r_3\text{.} \end{align*}
    The cubic resolvent of \(f\text{,}\) denoted \(R_f(x)\text{,}\) is the polynomial
    \begin{equation*} R_f(x)=(x-\theta_1)(x-\theta_2)(x-\theta_3)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The coefficients of \(R_f(x)\) are symmetric polynomial expressions in the \(r_i\text{.}\) It follows (see Remark 1.20.12) that these coefficients are in turn polynomial expressions of the elementary symmetric polynomials in \(r_i\text{,}\) which are precisely the coefficients of \(f(x)\text{.}\) Theorem 1.21.8 makes this more precise.

    Theorem 1.21.8. Cubic resolvent.

    Let \(f(x)=x^4+ax^3+bx+c\in F[x]\) be a degree-4 polynomial, and let \(R_f(x)\) be its cubic resolvent.
    1. \(R_f(x)\in F[x]\text{.}\)
    2. \(R_f(x)=x^3-bx^2+(ac-4d)x-(a^2d+c^2-4bd)\text{.}\) In particular, we have the special case
      \begin{equation} f(x)=x^4+cx+d \implies R_f(x)=x^3-4dx-c^2\text{.}\tag{1.38} \end{equation}
    3. The discriminant of \(R_f(x)\) is equal to the discriminant of \(f(x)\text{.}\) In particular, \(R_f(x)\) has no repeated roots if and only if \(f(x)\) has no repeated roots.

    Proof.

    Statement (1) follows from (2), which can be proved using symmetric polynomial techniques that are outside the scope of this course. Statement (3) follows from the definition of the discriminant, and is left as an exercise.
    Let \(F\) be a field of characteristic not equal to \(2\text{,}\) let \(f(x)\) be a monic separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(4\text{,}\) and let \(r_1,r_2,r_3,r_4\) be the roots of \(f\text{.}\) The permutation group \(S_n\) acts on the set \(X=\{\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\}\) of roots of \(R_f(x)\) as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \sigma(r_ir_j+r_kr_l)=r_{\sigma(i)}r_{\sigma(j)}+r_{\sigma(k)}r_{\sigma(l)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It is easy to see that this action is transitive (in fact any \(3\)-cycle acts transitively on \(X\)), and that the stabilizer subgroups are
    \begin{align*} \Stab_{S_n}(r_1r_2+r_3r_4)\amp =\angvec{(1324),(12)}\\ \Stab_{S_n}(r_1r_3+r_2r_4)\amp =\angvec{(1234),(13)}\\ \Stab_{S_n}(r_1r_4+r_2r_3)\amp =\angvec{(1243),(14)}\text{.} \end{align*}
    It follows that a transitive subgroup \(H\leq S_4\) contains a \(3\)-cycle if and only if its action on \(X=\{\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\}\) is transitive. Thus our Galois group \(G_f\) has cardinality divisible by \(3\) if and only if it acts transitively on \(X=\{\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\}\text{,}\) the three roots of \(R_f(x)\text{.}\) Since
    \begin{equation*} m_{\theta_1,F}(x)=\prod_{\beta\in O_{\theta_1}}(x-\beta)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(O_{\theta_1}=\{\sigma(\theta_1)\colon \sigma\in G_f\}\text{,}\) the orbit of \(\theta_1\) under \(G_f\) (see 1.18.2), we conclude that
    \begin{align*} 3\mid \abs{G_f} \amp \iff m_{\theta_1,F}(x)=(x-\theta_1)(x-\theta_2)(x-\theta_3)\\ \amp \iff m_{\theta_1,F}(x)=R_f(x)\\ \amp \iff R_f(x) \text{ is irreducible}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The theorem below now follows from this observation about the irreducibility of \(R_f(x)\text{,}\) the observation that \(G_f\) is isomorphic to a subgroup of \(A_4\) if and only if \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}\in F\text{,}\) and our catalogue of transitive subgroups of \(S_4\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.21.9. Galois group of quartic.

    Let \(F\) be a field of characteristic not equal to \(2\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)\) be a monic separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(4\text{.}\) Using the notation of Theorem 1.21.6, we have the following equivalences:
    \begin{align} G_f\cong S_4 \amp \iff \sqrt{\Delta_f}\notin F \text{ and } R_f(x) \text{ is irreducible}\tag{1.39}\\ G_f\cong A_4 \amp \iff \sqrt{\Delta_f}\in F \text{ and } R_f(x) \text{ is irreducible}\tag{1.40}\\ G_f\cong C_4 \text{ or } G_f\cong D_4 \amp \iff \sqrt{\Delta_f}\notin F \text{ and } R_f(x) \text{ is reducible} \tag{1.41}\\ G_f\cong V \amp \iff \sqrt{\Delta_f}\in F \text{ and } R_f(x) \text{ is reducible}\tag{1.42} \end{align}

    Proof.

    Let \(G_f\cong H\leq S_4\) via the map in Theorem 1.20.3. Summarizing our discussion above, we have
    \begin{align*} H\in \{S_4,A_4} \amp \iff 3\mid \abs{H} \\ \amp \iff R_f(x) \text{ irreducible}\text{.} \end{align*}
    This, coupled with the equivalence \(H\leq A_4\) if and only if \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}\in F\text{,}\) yields the first two equivalences of the theorem. The last two statements follow in a similar manner.

    Corollary 1.21.10. Splitting behavior of resolvent.

    Let \(F\) be a field of characteristic not equal to \(2\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)\) be a monic separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(4\text{.}\) Using the notation of Theorem 1.21.6, we have the following equivalences:
    1. \(G_f\cong V\) if and only if \(R_f(x)\) splits completely over \(F\text{.}\)
    2. \(G_f\cong C_4\) or \(G_f\cong D_4\) if and only if \(R_f(x)\) has a unique root in \(F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    This is a homework exercise.
    You will notice that so far our theory gives us no means of distinguishing between the case \(G_f\cong C_4\) and \(G_f\cong D_4\text{.}\) Theorem 1.21.11 provides us a partial test over the base field \(\Q\text{;}\) and Theorem 1.21.13 gives us a complete, if somewhat involved test that works over a general base field of characteristic not equal to \(2\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.21.11. \(C_4\) or \(D_4\text{:}\) partial check.

    Let \(f(x)\in \Q[x]\) be a monic separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(4\text{.}\) Using the notation of Theorem 1.21.6, if \(G_f\cong C_4\text{,}\) then \(\Delta_f> 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    We take \(\overline{\Q}\) to be the algebraic closure of \(\Q\) in \(\C\text{.}\) Assume that \(G_f\cong C_4\text{.}\) Let \(\alpha\) be a root of \(f(x)\text{.}\) Since \([K_f\colon \Q]=[\Q(\alpha)\colon \Q]=4\text{,}\) we have \(K_f=\Q(\alpha)\text{,}\) and thus all roots of \(f\) lie in \(\Q(\alpha)\text{.}\)
    If \(\alpha\) is real, then since every other root of \(f\) is an element of \(\Q(\alpha)\text{,}\) all roots of \(f\) are real. It follows easily in this case that \(\Delta_f> 0\text{.}\)
    The only other possibility is that all the roots are imaginary, in which case the distinct roots can be described as \(\alpha,\overline{\alpha},\beta,\overline{\beta}\text{,}\) and we have
    \begin{align*} \Delta_f \amp = (\alpha-\overline{\alpha})^2(\beta-\overline{\beta})^2 (\alpha-\beta)^2(\overline{\alpha}-\overline{\beta})^2(\alpha-\overline{\beta})^2(\overline{\alpha}-\beta)^2\\ \amp= (2i\operatorname{Im} \alpha)^2(2i\operatorname{Im} \beta)^2[(\alpha-\beta)(\overline{\alpha-\beta})]^2[(\alpha-\overline{\beta})(\overline{\alpha-\overline{\beta}})]^2\\ \amp= (2i\operatorname{Im} \alpha)^2(2i\operatorname{Im} \beta)^2\abs{\alpha-\beta}^4\abs{\alpha-\overline{\beta}}^4\\ \amp = 4(\operatorname{Im} \alpha)^2(\operatorname{Im} \beta)^2\abs{\alpha-\beta}^4\abs{\alpha-\overline{\beta}}^4\\ \amp > 0\text{.} \end{align*}

    Example 1.21.12. \(C_4\) or \(D_4\text{:}\) partial check.

    Let \(f(x)=x^4+4x^2-2\text{.}\) Identify a subgroup of \(S_4\) that is isomorphic to \(G_f\text{.}\)
    You may take for granted that \(\Delta_f=-18432\) and \(R_f(x)=x^3-4x^2+8x-32\text{.}\)
    Solution.

    Theorem 1.21.13. \(D_4\) or \(C_4\).

    Let \(F\) be a field of characteristic not equal to \(2\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)\) be a monic separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(4\text{.}\) Suppose that \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}\notin F\) and that \(R_f(x)\) has a unique root \(r\in F\text{.}\) Letting \(f(x)=x^4+ax^3+bx^2+c\text{,}\) and using the notation of Theorem 1.21.6, we have
    \begin{equation} G_f\cong \begin{cases}C_4 \amp \text{if } x^2+ax+(b-r) \text{ and } x^2-rx+d \text{ split over } K(\sqrt{\Delta_f}) \\ D_4 \amp \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}\tag{1.43} \end{equation}

    Example 1.21.14. \(D_4\) or \(C_4\).

    Prove that the given quartic polynomial \(f(x)\in \Q[x]\) is irreducible and determine a subgroup of \(S_4\) that is isomorphic to \(G_f\text{.}\) We have provided the discriminant and cubic resolvent.
    1. \(f(x)=x^4+3x+3\text{,}\) \(\Delta_f=21\cdot 15^2\text{,}\) \(R_f(x)=x^3-12x-9\text{.}\)
    2. \(f(x)=x^5+5x+5\text{,}\) \(\Delta_f=5\cdot 55^2\text{,}\) \(R_f(x)=x^3-20x-25\text{.}\)
