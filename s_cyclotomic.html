    Section 1.19 Cyclotomic extensions

    Definition 1.19.1. Roots of unity.

    Let \(F\) be a field. The \(n\)-th roots of unity of \(F\), denoted \(\mu_n(F)\text{,}\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} \mu_n(F)=\{\alpha\colon F\colon \alpha^n=1\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Given an algebraic closure of \(\overline{F}/F\text{,}\) we will often write simply \(\mu_n\) for \(\mu_{n}(\overline{F})\text{.}\) Note that \(\mu_n(K)\leq \mu_n\) for every intermediate field \(F\leq K\leq \overline{F}\text{.}\)
    A primitive \(n\)-th root of unity is an element \(\zeta\in \mu_n(\overline{F})\) whose multiplicative order \(\ord \zeta\) is equal to \(n\text{:}\) i.e., \(n\) is the smallest positive integer such that \(\zeta^n=1\text{.}\)
    A cyclotomic extension is a field extension of the form \(F(\zeta)/F\text{,}\) where \(\zeta\in \overline{F}\) is a primitive \(n\)-th root of unity for some positive integer \(n\text{.}\)

    Example 1.19.2. Roots of unity.

    1. Let \(F=\Q\) and \(n=4\text{.}\) We have \(\mu_4(\Q)=\{1,-1\}\) and \(\mu_4(\overline{\Q})=\{1,-1,i,-i\}\text{.}\) Of the \(4\)-th roots of unity, \(1\) is the primitive first root of unity, \(-1\) is the primitive \(2\)-nd root of unity, and \(i\) and \(-i\) are the primitive \(4\)-th roots of unity.
    2. Let \(F=\F_2\) and \(n=4\text{.}\) Since the polynomial \(x^4-1\) factors as \((x-1)^4\) over \(\F_2\text{,}\) we have \(\mu(\F_2)=\mu(\overline{\F_2})=\{1\}\text{.}\) There are no primitive \(4\)-th roots of unity in this case.
    For the rest of the section we will focus on the situation where \(F=\Q\text{,}\) and where we choose \(\overline{\Q}\) to be the algebraic closure of \(\Q\) in \(\C\text{.}\) In this case, for all positive integers \(n\) the group \(\mu_n=\mu_n(\Qbar)\) is a cyclic subgroup of \(\Qbar^*\) of cardinality \(n\text{,}\) and in fact we have
    \begin{equation*} \mu_n=\angvec{\zeta}=\{1,\zeta,\zeta^2, \dots, \zeta^{n-1}\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\zeta\in \Qbar\) is any fixed primitive \(n\)-th root of unity. (We often take \(\zeta=e^{2\pi i/n}\in \C\text{.}\)) Furthermore, the other primitive \(n\)-th roots of unity are precisely the powers \(\zeta^i\text{,}\) where \(1\leq i\leq n\) and \((i,n)=1\text{.}\) Thus, there are exactly \(\phi(n)\) distinct primitive \(n\)-th roots of unity, where \(\phi\) is the Euler totient function.

    Definition 1.19.3. Cyclotomic polynomial.

    Let \(\overline{\Q}\) be an algebraic closure of \(\Q\text{.}\) Given a positive integer \(n\) the \(n\)-th cyclotomic polynomial is the polynomial \(\Phi_n(x)\in \Qbar[x]\) defined as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{n}(x)=\prod_{\substack{\alpha\in \mu_n\\ \ord \alpha=n}}(x-\alpha)=\prod_{\substack{1\leq j\leq n\\ (j,n)=1}}(x-\zeta^j)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\zeta\) is any primitive \(n\)-th root of unity. Note that since the number of primitive \(n\)-th roots of unity is \(\phi(n)\text{,}\) we have \(\deg \Phi_n(x)=\phi(n)\text{.}\)

    Example 1.19.4. Cyclotomic polynomials.

    Let \(\Qbar\) be the algebraic closure of \(\Q\) in \(\C\text{.}\) In this setting we typically choose \(\zeta_n=e^{2\pi i/n}\) as a primitive \(n\)-th root of unity. It is useful to observe that for any primitive \(n\)-th root of unity \(\zeta\in \C\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \zeta^{-1}=\zeta^{n-1}=\overline{\zeta}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Below we compute the first \(6\) cyclotomic polynomials.
    \begin{align*} \Phi_1(x) \amp = (x-1)\\ \Phi_2(x) \amp = (x-(-1))=(x+1)\\ \Phi_3(x) \amp = (x-\zeta_3)(x-\zeta_3^2) \amp \left(\zeta_3=-\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\\ \amp =(x-\zeta_3)(x-\overline{\zeta_3})=x^2+x+1\\ \Phi_4(x) \amp = (x-\zeta_4)(x-\zeta_4^3)=(x-i)(x+i)=x^2+1\\ \Phi_5(x)\amp = \prod_{i=1}^4(x-\zeta_5^i)=x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1\\ \Phi_6(x) \amp = (x-\zeta_6)(x-\zeta_6^5) \amp \left(\zeta_6=e^{\pi i/3}=\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)\\ \amp =(x-\zeta_6)(x-\overline{\zeta_6})=x^2-x+1 \end{align*}
    Looking at the cyclotomic polynomials in Example 1.19.4, we notice two things (beyond the fact that \(\deg \Phi_n(x)=\phi(n)\)): \(\Phi_n(x)\in \Z[x]\text{,}\) and \(\Phi_n(x)\) is irreducible over \(\Q\text{.}\) This is no accident.

    Theorem 1.19.5. Cyclotomic polynomials.

    Let \(n\) be a positive integer.
    1. \(\Phi_n(x)\in \Z[x]\text{.}\)
    2. \(\Phi_n(x)\) is irreducible over \(\Q\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. We first prove that for all positive integers \(n\) we have
      \begin{align*} x^n-1 \amp =\prod_{d\mid n}\Phi_d(x)\text{.} \end{align*}
      This result is made official in Theorem 1.19.8. The proof is by strong induction. The base case \(n=1\) is true as \(\zeta=1\) is the only primitive first root of unity, and thus \(\Phi_1(x)=(x-1)=x^1-1\text{.}\)
    Theorem 1.19.5 has some important consequences for cyclotomic field extensions of \(\Q\text{.}\) Let \(\zeta\in \Qbar\) be a primitive \(n\)-th root of unity for some positive integer \(n\text{.}\) The extension \(K=\Q(\zeta)\) is Galois over \(\Q\text{,}\) as it is the splitting field of \(x^n-1\text{.}\) (As usual, separability is automatic over a characteristic zero field.) Since \(\zeta\) is a root of \(\Phi_n(x)\text{,}\) which is irreducible by Theorem 1.19.5, we have \(m_{\zeta,\Q}(x)=\Phi_n(x)\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{equation*} [\Q(\zeta)\colon \Q]=\deg \Phi_n(x)=\phi(n)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    What can we say about the Galois group \(G=\Gal(\Q(\zeta)/\Q)\text{?}\) Recall that the group of units \((\Z/n\Z)^*\) is of \(\Z/m\Z\) has cardinality \(\abs{(\Z/n\Z)^*}=\phi(n)\text{.}\) (See Theorem 0.4.6.) The following theorem should then come as no surprise.

    Theorem 1.19.6. Cyclotomic extensions of \(\Q\).

    Let \(n\) be a positive integer. For any integer \(j\in \Z\text{,}\) we will write \(\overline{j}\) for its image \(j+n\Z\) in the quotient ring \(\Z/n\Z\text{.}\) Let \(\zeta\in \Q\) be a primitive \(n\)-th root of unity.
    1. \(\Q(\zeta)/\Q\) is a Galois extension of degree \(\phi(n)\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(G=\Gal(\Q(\zeta)/\Q)\text{.}\) For any \(\sigma\in G\text{,}\) we have \(\sigma(\zeta)=\zeta^{j}\) for some integer \(j\) with \((j,n)=1\text{.}\)
    3. Let \(G=\Gal(\Q(\zeta)/\Q)\text{.}\) The map \(\phi\colon G\rightarrow (\Z/n\Z)^*\) defined as
      \begin{equation*} \phi(\sigma)=\overline{j}=j+n\Z\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(\sigma(\zeta)=\zeta^j\text{,}\) is a group isomorphism. In particular, we have \(G\cong (\Z/n\Z)^*\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. The paragraph preceding the the theorem statement proves that \(\Q(\zeta)/\Q\) is a Galois extension of degree \(\phi(n)\text{.}\)
    2. Given \(\sigma\in G\text{,}\) since \(\sigma\) is an automorphism it is easy to see that
      \begin{equation*} \ord \sigma(\zeta)=\ord \zeta=n\text{.} \end{equation*}
      It follows that we have \(\sigma(\zeta)=\zeta^j\text{,}\) for some integer \(j\text{,}\) where \((j,n)=1\text{.}\)
    3. Since \(\zeta^j=\zeta^{j'}\) if and only if \(j\equiv j' \pmod n\) if and only if \(\overline{j}=\overline{j'}\text{,}\) the map \(\phi\colon G\rightarrow (\Z/n\Z)^*\) is well defined.
      Suppose \(\sigma,\tau\in G\) satisfy
      \begin{equation} \sigma(\zeta)=\zeta^j, \tau(\zeta)=\zeta^{k}\text{.}\tag{1.35} \end{equation}
      We have
      \begin{align*} \sigma=\tau \amp \iff \sigma(\zeta)=\tau(\zeta)\\ \amp \iff \zeta^j=\zeta^{k} \\ \amp \iff j\equiv k \pmod n\\ \amp \iff \overline{j}=\overline{k}\\ \amp \iff \phi(\sigma)=\phi(\tau)\text{.} \end{align*}
      This proves that \(\phi\) is injective. Similarly, given \(\sigma\) and \(\tau\) satisfying (1.35), we have
      \begin{equation*} \sigma\tau(\zeta)=\sigma(\tau(\zeta))=\sigma(\zeta^k)=(\zeta^{k})^j=\zeta^{jk}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      in which case
      \begin{align*} \phi(\sigma\tau) \amp = \overline{jk}\\ \amp = \overline{j}\ \overline{k}\\ \amp = \phi(\sigma)\phi(\tau)\text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\phi\) is a group homomorphism. Lastly, since \(\phi\) is injective, and since \(\abs{G}=\abs{(\Z/n\Z)^*}=\phi(n)\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\phi\) is also surjective, and hence a group isomorphism.
    It should not be forgotten that complex conjugation \(a+bi\mapsto a-bi\) is a \(\Q\)-automorphism of any Galois extension \(K/\Q\text{.}\) The corollary below identifies which element of \((\Z/n\Z)^*\) complex conjugation corresponds to when \(K\) is a cyclotomic extension.

    Corollary 1.19.7. Complex conjugation.

    Let \(K=\Q(\zeta)\text{,}\) where \(\zeta\in \C\) is a primitive \(n\)-th root of unity, and let \(\sigma\colon K\rightarrow K\) be the restriction of the complex conjugation operation on \(\C\) to \(K\text{.}\)
    1. Under the isomorphism described in Theorem 1.19.6, \(\sigma\) corresponds to the element \(\overline{-1}\in (\Z/n\Z)^*\text{.}\)
    2. Identifying \(G\) with \((\Z/n\Z)^*\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} K^{\angvec{\overline{-1}}}=\Q(\zeta+\zeta^{-1})=\Q(\zeta+\overline{\zeta})=\Q(\operatorname{Re} \zeta)\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(\operatorname{Re} \zeta=\frac{1}{2}(\zeta+\overline{\zeta})\) is the real part of \(\zeta\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. Since \(\zeta^n=1\text{,}\) we have \(\abs{\zeta}=1\) and hence, using some complex arithmetic
      \begin{equation*} \zeta^{-1}=\frac{1}{\abs{\zeta}^2}\overline{\zeta}=\overline{\zeta}=\sigma(\zeta)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. This is a homework exercise.
    Thanks to Theorem 1.19.6 and our thorough understanding of the modular unit groups \((\Z/n\Z)^*\) (see Theorem 0.4.6), the family of cyclotomic extensions of \(\Q\) furnish us with a wonderful testing ground for Galois theory. Note that since the Galois groups of a cyclotomic extension \(\Q(\zeta)/\Q\) are abelian, all subgroups are normal, and hence all subextensions of \(\Q(\zeta)/\Q\) are Galois. As such, cyclotomic examples are not particularly revealing with regard to the issue of normal versus non-normal subextensions. That said, they do give rise to interesting and computable lattices of subfields.
    Before getting into examples, we record some fascinating and useful properties of cyclotomic polynomials that allow us to compute their expanded form in a recursive manner.

    Theorem 1.19.8. Computing cyclotomic polynomials.

    Given a positive integer \(n\text{,}\) let \(\Phi_n(x)\) denote the \(n\)-th cyclotomic polynomial.
    1. \(x^n-1=\prod_{d\mid n}\Phi_d(x)\text{.}\)
    2. If \(n> 2\) is odd, then
      \begin{equation*} \Phi_{2n}(x)=\Phi_n(-x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    3. If \(p\) is prime, then
      \begin{equation*} \Phi_{p^r}(x)=\Phi_p(x^{p^{r-1}})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    4. If \(p\) is a prime and \((p,m)=1\text{,}\) then
      \begin{equation*} \Phi_{p^rm}(x)=\Phi_m(x^{p^r})/\Phi_m(x^{p^{r-1}})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    5. If \(p_1,p_2,\dots, p_r\) are distinct primes, then
      \begin{equation*} \Phi_{p_1^{n_1}p_2^{n_2}\cdots p_{r}^{n_r}}(x)=\Phi_{p_1p_2\dots p_n}(x^{p_1^{n_1-1}p_2^{n_2-1}\cdots p_r^{n_r-1}})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    6. \(\Phi_n(x)=x^\phi(n)\Phi_n(1/x)\text{.}\) As a result, cyclotomic polynomials are palindromic: i.e., if \(\Phi_n(x)=a_dx^d+a_{d-1}x^{d-1}+\cdots +a_1x+a_0\text{,}\) then \(a_k=a_{d-k}\) for all \(0\leq k\leq d\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Statement (1) was proved as part of the proof of Theorem 1.19.5.
    The remaining statements can be proved using the following argument: if \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are (a) monic, (b) separable, and (c) have the same degree, then \(f=g\) if and only if they have the same roots. Note that for all \(n\in \Z_+\text{,}\) since \(\Phi_n(x)\) is irreducible and \(\character \Q=-\text{,}\) \(\Phi_n(x)\) is separable.
    For example, consider (2). First observe that since \(n\) is odd, we have \(\phi(2n)=\phi(2)\phi(n)=\phi(n)\text{.}\) Thus \(\deg \Phi_{2n}(x)=\deg \Phi_n(-x)\text{.}\) Furthermore, since \(n> 2\text{,}\) \(\phi(n)\) is even. Thus \(\Phi_n(-x)\) is monic. Lastly, the roots of \(\Phi_n(-x)\) are precisely the negatives of the roots of \(\Phi_n(x)\text{.}\) Suppose \(\zeta\) is a primitive \(n\)-th root of unity. Since \(n\) is odd, we cannot have \(\zeta^j=-1\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{align*} (-\zeta)^j=(-1)^j \zeta^j=1 \amp \iff j\text{ even and } \zeta^j=1 \\ \amp \iff 2\mid j \text{ and } n\mid j \amp (\ord \zeta=n)\\ \amp \iff 2n\mid j\text{.} \end{align*}
    This shows that \(\ord(-\zeta)=2n\text{.}\) We conclude that the \(\phi(n)\) roots of \(\Phi_n(-x)\) are precisely the distinct primitive \(2n\)-th roots of unity. It follows that both polynomials are separable, and we have \(\Phi_{2n}(x)=\Phi_n(-x)\)
    The proofs of the rest of the statements are left as an exercise.

    Example 1.19.9. \(9\)-th cyclotomic extension.

    Let \(K=\Q(\zeta)\text{,}\) where \(\zeta=e^{2\pi i/9}\text{,}\) a primitive \(9\)-th root of unity. Let \(G=\Gal(K/\Q)\text{.}\)
    1. Compute \(\Phi_9(x)\text{.}\)
    2. Compute the lattice of subgroups of \(G\text{.}\)
    3. Compute the lattice of intermediate fields of \(K/\Q\) and describe each field in the form \(\Q(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha\) is written as a polynomial expression in \(\zeta\) and \(\zeta^{-1}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. Using Theorem 1.19.8, we have
      \begin{align*} \Phi_9(x) \amp =\Phi_{3^2}(x)\\ \amp = \Phi_3(x^3)\\ \amp = x^6+x^3+1\text{.} \end{align*}
      Alternatively, we have
      \begin{equation*} x^9-1=\Phi_9(x)\Phi_3(x)\Phi_1(x)=\Phi_9(x)(x^3-1) \end{equation*}
      and thus \(\Phi_9(x)=x^9-1/(x^3-1)=x^6+x^3+1\text{.}\)
    2. By Theorem 1.19.6 we have
      \begin{equation*} G\cong (\Z/9\Z)^*\text{.} \end{equation*}
      For notational ease we will henceforth identify these two groups. Furthermore, we will write \(n\) instead of \(\overline{n}\) for elements of \((\Z/9\Z)^*\text{.}\) Since \(9=3^2\text{,}\) \(G\) is cyclic (0.4.6), of cardinality \(6\text{.}\) A simple inspection of elements of \(G\) reveals that \((\Z/9\Z)^*=\angvec{2}\text{.}\) Since the \(G\) is cyclic, there is a unique subgroup for each \(d\) dividing \(9\text{,}\) and this subgroup is cyclic. It is easy to see that \(\angvec{-1}\) is the unique subgroup of cardinality \(2\text{,}\) and \(\angvec{4}\) is the unique subgroup of cardinality \(3\text{.}\) We thus have the following lattice of subgroups:
    3. Using the Galois correspondence, we derive the following lattice of subfields.
      Note that \(\zeta^3\) is a primitive cube-root of unity, and we know already that \(\Q(\zeta^3)\) is a degree-2 extension of \(\Q\text{.}\) Since \(K^{\angvec{4}}/\Q\) is the unique degree-2 subextension, we must have \(K^{\angvec{4}}=\Q(\zeta^3)\text{.}\) Alternatively, we could proceed more directly. The automorphism \(\sigma_4\) corresponding to \(4\in (\Z/9\Z)^*\) is summarized as
      \begin{equation*} \zeta\xmapsto{\sigma_4}\zeta^4\text{.} \end{equation*}
      It follows that
      \begin{equation*} \sigma_4(\zeta^3)=\zeta^{12}=\zeta^3\text{,} \end{equation*}
      showing that \(\angvec{4}\leq \Aut_{\Q(\zeta^3)}(K)\) and thus \(\Q(\zeta^3)\leq K^{\angvec{4}}\text{.}\) We get equality by comparing degrees.
      By Corollary 1.19.7, we know that \(-1\) corresponds to the complex conjugation automorphism, and we have
      \begin{equation*} K^{\angvec{-1}}=\Q(\zeta+\zeta^{-1})=\Q(\operatorname{Re} \zeta)=\Q(\cos(2\pi/9))\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.19.10. \(15\)-th cyclotomic extension.

    Let \(K=\Q(\zeta)\text{,}\) where \(\zeta\in \C\) is a primitive \(15\)-th root of unity. Let \(G=\Gal(K/\Q)\text{.}\)
    1. Compute \(\Phi_{15}(x)\text{.}\)
    2. Compute the lattice of subgroups of \(G\text{.}\)
    3. Compute the lattice of intermediate fields of \(K/\Q\) and describe each field either in the form \(\Q(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha\) is written as a polynomial expression in \(\zeta\) and \(\zeta^{-1}\text{,}\) or in the form \(\Q(\sqrt{m})\) for some integer \(m\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. Using Theorem 1.19.8, we have
      \begin{align*} \Phi_{15}(x) \amp =\Phi_{3}(x^5)/\Phi_3(x)\\ \amp =(x^{10}+x^5+1)/(x^2+x+1)\\ \amp = x^8-x^7+x^5+x^4+x^3-x+1\text{.} \end{align*}
    2. By Theorem 1.19.6 and Theorem 0.4.6 we have
      \begin{equation*} G\cong (\Z/15\Z)^*\cong (\Z/3\Z)^*\times (\Z/5\Z)^*\cong \Z/2\Z\times \Z/4\Z\text{.} \end{equation*}
