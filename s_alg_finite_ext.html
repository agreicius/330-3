    Section 1.6 Algebraic extensions: theory

    The main result of this section is Theorem 1.6.9, which both generalizes Example 1.5.8 and gives a full characterization of finite extensions in terms of finitely generated and algebraic field extensions. First we treat the tower law, which will be instrumental in proving Theorem 1.6.9, and is also quite useful in its own right.

    Theorem 1.6.1. Tower law.

    Let \(L/K/F\) be a tower of (possibly infinite) field extensions.
    1. If \(B_1\) is a basis of \(K\) and \(B_2\) is a basis of \(L\) as a \(K\)-vector space, then the set
      \begin{equation} B=\{\alpha\beta\colon \alpha\in B_1, \beta\in B_2\}\tag{1.5} \end{equation}
      of all pairwise products of elements of \(B_1\) and \(B_2\) is a basis of \(L\) as an \(F\)-vector space.
    2. We have
      \begin{equation} [L\colon F]=[L\colon K][K\colon F]\text{.}\tag{1.6} \end{equation}

    Proof of (1).

    Let \(B=\{\alpha\beta\colon \alpha\in B_1, \beta\in B_2\}\text{,}\) as in the theorem statement. We will show directly that \(B\) is a basis of \(L\) as an \(F\)-vector space, by showing that it spans and is linearly independent.

    \(B\) spans.

    Given any \(\alpha\in L\text{,}\) since \(B_2\) is a \(K\)-basis of \(L\text{,}\) we can write
    \begin{equation*} \alpha=c_1\beta_1+c_2\beta_2+\cdots +c_n\beta_n=\sum_{j=1}^nc_j\beta_j \end{equation*}
    for some elements \(\beta_j\in B_2\) and scalars \(c_j\in K\text{.}\) Next since \(B_1\) is a basis of \(K\) over \(F\text{,}\) we can find a finite set \(\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m\}\subseteq B_1\) such that for each \(1\leq j\leq n\) we have
    \begin{equation*} c_j=d_{1j}\alpha_1+d_{2j}\alpha_2+\cdots +d_{mj}\alpha_m=\sum_{i=1}^md_{ij}\alpha_i \end{equation*}
    for some elements \(d_{ij}\in F\text{.}\) Substituting these expressions for \(c_j\) into the first linear combination above, we conclude that
    \begin{align*} \alpha\amp = \sum_{j=1}^nc_j\beta_j \\ \amp = \sum_{j=1}^n (\sum_{i=1}^md_{ij}\alpha_i)\beta_j\\ \amp =\sum_{\substack{1\leq i\leq m\\ 1\leq j\leq n }}d_{ij}\, \alpha_i\, \beta_j \end{align*}
    for some scalars \(d_{ij}\in F\) and elements \(\alpha_i\beta_j\in B\text{.}\) This shows that every element of \(L\) can be written as a \(F\)-linear combination of elements of \(B\text{,}\) and hence that \(\Span_F B=L\text{.}\)

    \(B\) is linearly independent.

    Assume we have an \(F\)-linear combination
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{\substack{1\leq i\leq m\\ 1\leq j\leq n }}d_{ij}\alpha_i\, \beta_j=0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\alpha_i\in B_1\text{,}\) \(\beta_j\in B_2\text{,}\) \(d_{ij}\in F\) and the elements \(\alpha_i\beta_j\) are distinct. Grouping terms, we can re-write this as
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^nc_j\beta_j=0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where for each \(1\leq j\leq n\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} c_j=\sum_{i=1}^md_{ij}\alpha_j \in K\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Firstly, since \(\alpha_i\beta_j\ne \alpha_{i'}\beta_{j'}\) for all \((i,j)\ne (i',j')\text{,}\) it follows easily that \(\beta_j\ne \beta_{j'}\) for \(j\ne j'\text{.}\) Since \(B_2\) is linearly independent over \(K\text{,}\) we conclude that \(c_j=0\) for all \(1\leq j\leq n\text{.}\) But then, since \(B_1\) is linearly independent over \(F\text{,}\) for all \(1\leq j\leq n\) we have
    \begin{equation*} c_j=\sum_{i=1}^md_{ij}\alpha_j=0\implies d_{ij}=0 \text{ for all } 1\leq i\leq m\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We conclude that \(d_{ij}=0\) for all \(1\leq i\leq m\) and \(1\leq j\leq n\text{.}\) This proves \(B\) is linearly independent over \(F\text{.}\)

    Proof of (2).

    We treat two cases: (a) \(K/F\) and \(L/K\) are both finite extensions, and (b) at least one of \(K/F\) and \(L/K\) is an infinite extension.

    Case (a).

    Assume \([K\colon F]=n_1\) and \([L\colon K]=n_2\text{,}\) for positive integers \(n_1, n_2\text{.}\) By definition we can find bases
    \begin{align*} B_1 \amp =\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_{n_1}\}\\ B_2 \amp =\{\beta_1,\beta_2,\dots, \beta_{n_2}\} \end{align*}
    for \(K\) as an \(F\)-vector space, and \(L\) as a \(K\)-vector space, respectively. By statement (1) of this theorem, we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} B=\{\alpha_i\beta_j\colon 1\leq i\leq n_1, 1\leq j\leq n_2\} \end{equation*}
    is a basis of \(L\) as an \(F\)-vector space. It remains only to show that \(\abs{B}=n_1n_2\text{,}\) since then we have
    \begin{equation*} [L\colon F]=\dim_F L=\abs{B}=n_1n_2=[K\colon F][L\colon K]=[L\colon K][K\colon F]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To see that \(\abs{B}=n_1n_2\text{,}\) we show that the map
    \begin{align*} B_1\times B_2 \amp \rightarrow B\\ (\alpha, \beta) \amp \mapsto \alpha\beta \end{align*}
    is a bijection, whence \(\abs{B}=\abs{B_1}\times \abs{B_2}=n_1n_2\text{.}\) The map is surjective by definition of \(B\text{.}\) That the map is injective follows from the following chain of implications:
    \begin{align*} \alpha\beta=\alpha'\beta' \amp \implies \alpha\beta-\alpha'\beta' \\ \amp \implies \beta=\beta' \text{ or } \alpha=\alpha'=0 \amp (B_2 \text{ lin. ind. over } K) \\ \amp\implies \beta=\beta' \amp (B_1 \text{ basis } \implies \alpha\ne 0, \alpha'\ne 0) \\ \amp \implies \alpha=\alpha' \text{ and } \beta=\beta' \amp (\alpha\beta=\alpha'\beta')\\ \amp \implies (\alpha, \beta)=(\alpha', \beta')\text{.} \end{align*}

    Case (b).

    Assume first that \(K/F\) is infinite. In this case we can find an infinite set \(S\) of the form \(S=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots,\}\subseteq K\) that is linearly independent over \(F\text{.}\) But \(S\) is also a subset of \(L\text{,}\) and thus \(L/F\) has an infinite linearly independent set, making it an infinite extension.
    Now assume that \(L/K\) is infinite. In this case we can find an infinite set \(S\) of the form \(S=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots,\}\subseteq L\) that is linearly independent over \(K\text{.}\) But then \(S\) is also linearly independent over the smaller field \(F\text{,}\) since an \(F\)-linear combination of elements of \(S\) is also a \(K\)-linear combination. Thus \(L/F\) also has infinite dimension.

    Remark 1.6.2. Tower law.

    Statement (1) of Theorem 1.6.1 is just as important as (2), as it gives us a procedure for building a basis for \(L/F\) from bases \(B_1\) of \(L/K\) and \(B_2\) of \(K/F\text{:}\) namely, we simply take the set of all products of elements in \(B_1\) and \(B_2\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.6.3. Tower law: infinite extensions.

    The formula (1.6) is true even when one or more of the field extensions involved is infinite, using the following rules from “extended arithmetic”:
    \begin{align*} \infty \amp = n\cdot \infty=\infty\cdot n=\infty\cdot \infty \text{ for all positive } n\in \Z \\ \infty \amp\ne m\cdot n \text{ for all positive } m,n \in \Z \text{.} \end{align*}
    Equivalently, we can interpret (1.6) as stating that the extension \(L/F\) is infinite if and only if \(L/K\) is infinite or \(K/F\) is infinite.

    Corollary 1.6.4. Tower law.

    1. Let \(L/K/F\) be a tower of finite field extensions. The integers \([K\colon F]\) and \([L\colon K]\) divide the integer \([L\colon F]\text{:}\) i.e., we have
      \begin{equation*} [K\colon F]\, \mid [L\colon F] \text{ and } [L\colon K]\, \mid [L\colon F]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. Given a tower of extensions
      \begin{equation*} F_1\leq F_2\leq \cdots \leq F_n\text{,} \end{equation*}
      we have
      \begin{equation*} [F_n\colon F_1]=\prod_{k=1}^{n-1}[F_{k+1}\colon F_k]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Statement (1) follows directly from Theorem 1.6.1; statement (2) follows from Theorem 1.6.1 by induction.
    We now make use of Theorem 1.6.1 to prove a nice generalization of Example 1.5.8.

    Theorem 1.6.5. Finite implies algebraic.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. If \(K/F\) is finite, then \(K/F\) is algebraic. Using logical shorthand, we have
    \begin{equation} K/F \text{ finite}\implies K/F \text{ algebraic}\text{.}\tag{1.7} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    We could a proof much along the lines of the argument used in Example 1.5.8, however Theorem 1.6.1 allows us to simplify things somewhat.
    Let \(\dim_F K=n\text{.}\) Let \(\beta\in K\text{,}\) and consider the field extension \(F(\beta)/F\text{.}\) Since we have \(F\leq F(\beta)\leq K\text{,}\) and since \(K/F\) is finite, the tower law implies \(F(\beta)/F\) is finite. Theorem 1.4.14 now implies \(\beta\) is algebraic. Since \(\beta\in K\) was arbitrary, we see that \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)
    Assume \(K/F\) is algebraic. It is not true in general that \(K/F\) is finite: that is, the converse of (1.7) is not true. Theorem 1.6.9, which we will prove in the next section, identifies an additional property \(K/F\) needs to satisfy in order to be finite: namely, it must be finitely generated. By the end of that section, we will also be able to provide an example of a field extension that is algebraic but not finite.

    Example 1.6.6. Tower law.

    Use the tower law to prove that the field extension \(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})/\Q\) has no nontrivial subextension \(K/\Q\text{:}\) i.e., if \(\Q\leq K\leq \Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\text{,}\) then \(K=\Q\) or \(K=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\text{.}\)
    Now on to the finite extension theorem, which says that a field extension is finite if and only if it is algebraic and finitely generated.

    Definition 1.6.7. Finitely generated.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. We say \(K\) is finitely generated over \(F\) if we have \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\) for some finite set \(\{\alpha_1, \alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\}\subseteq K\text{.}\)
    It is not the case that every field extension is finitely generated: e.g., it is possible to show that \(\Q(\sqrt{2},\sqrt[3]{2}, \sqrt[4]{2}, \dots)\) is not finitely generated over \(\Q\text{.}\) However, according to Theorem 1.6.8, every field extension is a union of finitely generated field extensions.

    Theorem 1.6.8. Finitely generated extensions.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension, and suppose \(K=F(A)\) for some (possibly infinite) subset \(A\subseteq K\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation*} K=\bigcup_{\substack{A'\subseteq A\\ A' \text{ finite}}}F(A')\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In particular, \(K\) is a union of finitely generated field extensions of \(F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Theorem 1.6.9. Finite extension theorem.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(K/F\) is finite.
    2. \(K/F\) is finitely generated and algebraic.
    3. \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha_i\in K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) In other words, \(K\) is generated over \(F\) by finitely many algebraic elements.

    Proof.

    We prove the “cycle of implications” (1)\(\implies\)(2)\(\implies\)(3)\(\implies\) (1)

    Implication: (1)\(\implies\)(2).

    Assume \(K/F\) is finite, of dimension \(n\text{.}\)
    • By Theorem 1.6.5, since \(K/F\) is finite, it is algebraic.
    • We claim \(K/F\) is finitely generated. Let \(B=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\}\) be a basis of \(K\) as an \(F\)-vector space. Since \(\Span_F B=K\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} K=\{c_1\alpha_1+c_2\alpha_2+\cdots +c_n\alpha_n\colon c_i\in F\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      It follows that \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\text{,}\) and hence is finitely generated (by the elements \(\alpha_i\)). Indeed, since \(F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\) is a subfield of \(K\) containing \(F\) and \(\alpha_i\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\text{,}\) it must contain all \(F\)-linear combinations of the \(\alpha_i\text{.}\) But then we have
      \begin{equation*} K=\{c_1\alpha_1+c_2\alpha_2+\cdots +c_n\alpha_n\colon c_i\in F\}\leq F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\leq K\text{,} \end{equation*}
      and conclude that \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\text{.}\)

    Implication: (2)\(\implies\)(3).

    Assume \(K/F\) is finitely generated and algebraic. Since \(K/F\) is finitely generated, there are elements \(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m\) such that \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m)\text{.}\) Since \(K/F\) is algebraic, each element \(\alpha_i\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) Thus \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m)\text{,}\) and each \(\alpha_i\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) as desired.

    Implication: (3)\(\implies\)(1).

    We will show that any field extension generated by finitely many algebraic elements is finite. The proof will proceed by induction on the number \(m\) of generators of the field.
    Base step: \(m=1\text{.}\) Assume \(K=F(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.4.14, we have \(\dim_F K< \infty\text{.}\) Thus \(K/F\) is finite.
    Induction step: assume any field extension generated over the base field by \(m\) algebraic elements is a finite extension. Now suppose we have \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_{m+1})\text{,}\) where each \(\alpha_i\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) If \(\alpha_i\in F\) for all \(i\text{,}\) then \(F=K\text{,}\) in which case \(K\) is finite over \(F\text{.}\) Otherwise, we may assume without loss of generality that \(\alpha_1\in K-F\text{.}\) Since \(\alpha_1\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) the extension \(F(\alpha_1)/F\) is finite by the base step. Let \(K'=F(\alpha_1)\text{.}\) We have a tower of fields \(K/K'/F\text{,}\) and thus by the tower law, the extension \(K/F\) is finite if and only if \(K/K'\) is finite and \(K'/F\) is finite. We know already that \(K'=F(\alpha_1)\) is finite over \(F\text{.}\) As for the extension \(K/K'\) it is easy to see that
    \begin{equation*} K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_{m+1})=F(\alpha_1)(\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_{m+1})=K'(\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_{m+1})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Furthermore, since each \(\alpha_i\) is satisfies a nonzero polynomial \(f_i(x)\in F[x]\subseteq K'[x]\text{,}\) we see that each \(\alpha_i\) is algebraic over \(K'\text{.}\) Thus the extension \(K/K'\) is generated over \(K'\) by the \(m\) algebraic elements \(\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_{m+1}\text{.}\) By induction \(K/K'\) is finite. The tower law now implies that \(K/F\) is finite.

    Remark 1.6.10. Finite extension theorem.

    As we saw in the proof of Theorem 1.6.9, given a field extension \(K/F\) the following implications hold:
    \begin{align*} K/F \text{ finite} \amp\implies K/F \text{ finitely generated} \\ K/F \text{ finite} \amp\implies K/F \text{ algebraic} \text{.} \end{align*}
    On the other hand, neither of these implications goes the other way. That is, we have
    \begin{align*} K/F \text{ finite } \hspace{5pt} \amp\not\!\!\!\!\!\!\impliedby K/F \text{ finitely generated} \\ K/F \text{ finite } \hspace{5pt} \amp\not\!\!\!\!\!\!\impliedby K/F \text{ algebraic} \text{.} \end{align*}
    In other words, taken singly, neither of these two properties (finitely generated, algebraic) is strong enough to imply the property of being finite. Theorem 1.6.9 tells us that the two properties taken together are in fact equivalent to being finite: i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} K/F \text{ finite } \iff K/F \text{ finitely generated and algebraic}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Theorem 1.6.9 can be used to prove important facts about general algebraic extensions, finitely generated or not. We record a few of these now as corollaries.

    Corollary 1.6.11. Algebraically generated is algebraic.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(K/F\) is algebraic.
    2. \(K=F(A)\text{,}\) where \(A\) is a (possibly infinite) set of algebraic elements.
    In plain English: the field \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\) if and only if \(K\) is algebraically generated over \(F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The implication (1)\(\implies\)(2) is clear, since if \(K/F\) is algebraic, then we have \(K=F(K)\) and all elements of \(K\) are algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)
    We now prove the implication (2)\(\implies\) (1). Assume \(K=F(A)\text{,}\) where \(A\) is a set of algebraic elements of \(K\text{.}\) Since by Theorem 1.6.8 we have
    \begin{equation*} K=\bigcup_{\substack{A'\subseteq A\\ A' \text{ finite}}}F(A')\text{,} \end{equation*}
    given any \(\beta\in K\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \beta\in F(A')=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m) \end{equation*}
    for some \(A'=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m\}\subseteq A\text{.}\) Since each \(\alpha_i\) is algebraic, \(F(A')\) is finite over \(F\text{,}\) hence algebraic over \(F\) by Theorem 1.6.9. We conclude that \(\beta\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) as desired.

    Example 1.6.12. Algebraically generated.

    Let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2},\sqrt[3]{2},\sqrt[4]{2},\dots)\text{.}\) Show that the extension \(K/\Q\) is (a) infinite, (b) algebraic, and (c) not finitely generated.

    Corollary 1.6.14. Relative algebraic closure.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension.
    1. If \(\alpha, \beta\in K\) are algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) then so are \(-\alpha\text{,}\) \(\alpha+\beta\text{,}\) and \(\alpha\beta\text{.}\) If \(\alpha\) is nonzero and algebraic, then so is its inverse \(\alpha^{-1}\text{.}\)
    2. Let
      \begin{equation} \overline{F}_K=\{\alpha\in K\colon \alpha \text{ is algebraic over } F\}\text{.}\tag{1.9} \end{equation}
      1. \(\overline{F}_K/F\) is an algebraic subextension of \(K/F\text{.}\)
      2. \(\overline{F}_K/F\) is the unique maximal algebraic subextension of \(K/F\) in the following sense: if \(K'/F\) is an algebraic subextension of \(K/F\text{,}\) then \(K'\leq \overline{F}_K\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. Fix any algebraic elements \(\alpha, \beta\in K\text{.}\) That the other elements listed in this statement (\(-\alpha\text{,}\) \(\alpha+\beta\text{,}\) etc.) are algebraic follows from the fact that \(F(\alpha,\beta)\) is algebraic (1.6.9).
    2. That \(\overline{F}_K\) is a field containing \(F\) follows from the fact that all elements of \(F\) are algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) and that the set of all algebraic elements is closed under addition, multiplication, and inverses (by (1)).
      Clearly, if \(K'/F\) is an algebraic extension, then since all elements of \(K'\) are algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) we have \(K'subseteq K\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.6.15. Relative algebraic closure.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. The field \(\overline{F}_K\) defined in (1.9) is called the algebraic closure of \(F\) in \(K\text{.}\) We call this a relative algebraic closure of \(F\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.6.16. Relative algebraic closure.

    The field \(\overline{F}_K\) is called the relative algebraic closure of \(F\text{,}\) since it depends on \(K\text{.}\) Later we will define the plain old algebraic closure of \(F\text{,}\) denoted \(\overline{F}\text{,}\) which (up to isomorphism) depends only on \(F\text{.}\)

    Example 1.6.17. Relative algebraic closure.

    Let’s consider some relative algebraic closures of \(\Q\text{.}\)
    1. Let \(K=\Q\text{.}\) Clearly the algebraic closure of \(\Q\) in \(\Q\) is \(\Q\text{:}\) that is, \(\overline{\Q}_K=\Q\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(K=\Q(\pi)\text{.}\) As you will show in homework, every element of \(K-\Q\) is transcendental. Thus \(\overline{\Q}_K=\Q\) in this case as well.
    3. Lastly, consider \(\C/\Q\) and the relative algebraic closure \(\overline{\Q}_\C\text{.}\) By the fundamental theorem of algebra, given any rational polynomial \(f(x)\in \Q[x]\text{,}\) we can factor \(f(x)\) over \(\C\) as
      \begin{equation*} f(x)=a(x-\alpha_1)(x-\alpha_2)\cdots (x-\alpha_n), a\in \Q, \alpha_k\in \C\text{.} \end{equation*}
      For all \(1\leq k\leq n\text{,}\) since \(\alpha_k\) is a root of \(f\text{,}\) it is algebraic, and hence is an element of \(\overline{\Q}_\C\text{.}\) It follows that in fact every rational polynomial \(f(x)\in \Q[x]\) can be factored into linear terms over the field \(\overline{\Q}_\C\text{.}\) The field \(\overline{\Q}_\C\) is a good deal more manageable than \(\C\) in that it is (a) algebraic over \(\Q\text{,}\) and (b) is countable (as opposed to uncountable), as one can show along the lines of Example 1.4.13.
