    Section 1.17 Galois correspondence: proof

    We now set about proving Theorem 1.16.5. The main work here goes into proving the first statement of that theorem: that the two maps of the Galois correspondence are in fact inverses of one another. One of the directions of that proof is essentially the statement of Artin’s theorem, a classic result of Emil Artin that is very important in its own right. We state and prove that theorem at the end of the section.

    Subsection Proof of the Galois correspondence theorem

    Recall the setup of Theorem 1.16.5. We assume \(L/F\) is a finite Galois extension with Galois group \(G=\Gal(L/F)=\Aut_F(L)\text{.}\) For notational convenience, we name the two maps of the Galois correspondence \(\Gamma\) and \(\Delta\text{:}\)

    Subsubsection Proof of statement (1)

    Using the notation introduced above, the proof of statement (1) of Theorem 1.16.5 amounts to showing the following equalities of maps:
    \begin{align*} \Gamma\circ \Delta \amp = \id\\ \Delta\circ \Gamma \amp = \id \text{.} \end{align*}
    We prove each in turn.
    Proof: \(\Gamma\circ \Delta=\id\).
    Given \(H\leq G\) we must show that the composition
    \begin{equation*} H\mapsto L^H \mapsto \Aut_{L^H}(L) \end{equation*}
    is the identity: equivalently, that
    \begin{equation*} \Aut_{L^H}(L)=H\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This follows directly from Artin’s theorem.
    Proof: \(\Delta\circ\Gamma =\id\).
    Given an intermediate field \(K\text{,}\) we must show that the composition
    \begin{equation*} K\mapsto H=\Aut_K(L)\mapsto L^H \end{equation*}
    is the identity: equivalently, that
    \begin{equation*} K=L^H, \hspace{5pt} H=\Aut_K(L) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    By definition, each element of \(K\) is fixed by \(H=\Aut_K(L)\text{.}\) Thus \(K\leq L^H\text{.}\) To show that we have equality, we will show that \(L^H/K\) is an extension of degree one. Since \(L/F\) is separable, strong transitivity implies \(L/K\) is separable; another application of strong transitivity implies \(L^H/K\) is separable. Let \(\overline{L}/L\) be an algebraic closure of \(L\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.14.1 we have
    \begin{equation*} [L^H\colon K]=\abs{\Hom_K(\overline{L})}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus we need only show that \(\abs{\Hom_K(\overline{L})}=1\text{.}\) To this end, let \(\sigma\colon L^H\rightarrow \overline{L}\) be a \(K\)-embedding. Since \(L/L^H\) is algebraic, there is an extension \(\tau\colon L\rightarrow \overline{L}\) of \(\sigma\text{.}\) Since \(L/K\) is Galois and \(\tau\) is a \(K\)-embedding, we have \(\tau(L)=L\text{,}\) and thus \(\tau\in\Aut_K(L)\text{.}\) But since \(H=\Aut_K(L)\text{,}\) we see that \(\tau\in H\text{,}\) and hence \(\sigma=\tau\vert_{L^H}=\id\text{.}\) This proves that the only element of \(\Hom_K(L^H,\Lbar)=\{i\}\text{,}\) where \(i\colon L^H\hookrightarrow \Lbar\) is the inclusion map. We conclude that
    \begin{equation*} [L^H\colon K]=\abs{\Hom_{K}(L^H,\Lbar)}=1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as desired.

    Subsubsection Proof of (2)

    Statement (2) is simply a re-statement of Theorem 1.16.3.

    Subsubsection Proof of (3)

    To see why statement (3) is true, take \(H\leq G\text{.}\) Since \(\Gamma(\Delta(H))=H\text{,}\) we have \(\Aut_{L^H}(L)=H\text{.}\) Since \(L/L^H\) is Galois, we have
    \begin{equation*} [L\colon L^H]=\abs{\Aut_{L^H}(L)}=\abs{H}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Next, since \([L\colon F]=\abs{G}=\abs{\Aut_F(L)}\text{,}\) the tower law implies
    \begin{equation*} [L^H\colon F]=[L\colon F]/[L\colon L^H]=\abs{G}/\abs{H}=[G\colon H]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection Proof of (4)

    Lastly, we consider the inclusion reversing claims of statement (4) of Theorem 1.16.5. We first prove the equivalence
    \begin{equation} K\leq K'\iff \Aut_{K'}(L)\leq \Aut_{K}(L)\text{.}\tag{1.30} \end{equation}
    Clearly, if \(K\leq K'\text{,}\) then \(\Aut_{K'}(L)\leq \Aut_{K}(L)\text{:}\) if \(\sigma\in \Aut_F(L)\) fixes \(K'\text{,}\) it certainly fixes \(K\text{.}\) Now consider the reverse implication
    \begin{equation*} \Aut_{K'}(L)\leq \Aut_{K}(L)\implies K\leq K'\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Let \(H_K=\Aut_{K}(L)\) and \(H_{K'}=\Aut_{K'}(L)\text{.}\) Since
    \begin{equation*} H_{K'}=\Aut_{K'}(L)\leq H_{K}=\Aut_{K}(L)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    it clear that \(L^{H_K}\leq L^{H_{K'}} \text{:}\) if a field element is fixed by the subgroup \(H_{K}\text{,}\) it is certainly fixed by the smaller subgroup \(H_{K'}\text{.}\) But by statement (1) of Theorem 1.16.5, we have \(K=L^{H_K}\) and \(K'=L^{H_{K'}}\text{,}\) and thus
    \begin{equation*} K=L^{H_K}\leq K'=L^{H_{K'}}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as desired.
    Lastly, we will derive the equivalence
    \begin{equation*} H\leq H' \iff L^{H'}=L^{H} \end{equation*}
    for subgroups \(H,H'\leq G\text{,}\) from the equivalence (1.30). By (1), we have \(H=\Aut_K(L)\) and \(H'=\Aut_{K'}(L)\text{,}\) for the fields \(K=L^H\) and \(K'=L^{H'}\text{.}\) But then we have
    \begin{align*} H\leq H'\amp \iff \Aut_K(L)\leq \Aut_{K'}(L) \\ \amp \iff K'\leq K \amp \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq_equiv_1.html}{\text{(1.30)}}\\ \amp \iff L^{H'}\leq L^H\text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.

    Subsection Artin’s theorem

    We conclude with the statement and proof of Artin’s theorem. The argument is a wonderfully winding one, and well worth internalizing. In particular, the proof introduces group actions to the study of Galois field extensions, and by so doing reveals an important relationship between the minimal polynomial of elements of a finite Galois extension \(L/F\) and the action of the Galois group on \(L\text{.}\) We enshrine some of these group action observations in Theorem 1.18.2, which will come in handy when computing examples.

    Theorem 1.17.1. Artin’s theorem.

    Let \(L\) be a field, and let \(H\) be a finite group of automorphisms of \(L\text{.}\) The extension \(L/L^H\) is a finite Galois extension, and \(\Aut_{L^H}(L)=H\text{.}\) In particular, we have \([L\colon L^H]=\abs{H}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Given \(\alpha\in L\text{,}\) let
    \begin{equation*} O_\alpha=\{\sigma(\alpha)\colon \sigma\in H\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    the set of all images of \(\alpha\) under elements of \(H\text{.}\) (In the theory of group actions, the set \(O_\alpha\) is called the orbit of \(\alpha\) under \(H\)). Note that any \(\sigma\in H\) maps \(O_\alpha\) to itself, since if \(\beta=\tau(\alpha)\) for some \(\tau\in H\text{,}\) then \(\sigma(\beta)=\sigma(\tau(\alpha))=(\sigma\tau)(\alpha)\in O_\alpha\text{,}\) since \(\sigma\tau\in H\text{.}\) Since each element of \(H\) is injective as a map, and since \(O_\alpha\) is finite, it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \sigma\vert_{O_\alpha}\colon O_\alpha\rightarrow O_\alpha \end{equation*}
    is a bijection for all \(\sigma\in H\text{.}\)
    Let \(\alpha=\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_d\) be the distinct elements of \(O_\alpha\text{.}\) We claim that
    \begin{equation} f(x)=\prod_{j=1}^d(x-\alpha_j)\tag{1.31} \end{equation}
    has coefficients in \(L^H\text{,}\) and that in fact \(f(x)=m_{\alpha, L^H}(x)\) is the minimal polynomial of \(\alpha\) over \(L^H\text{.}\) From this it follows that \(L/L^H\) is normal, since the minimal polynomial of any element splits completely in \(L\text{,}\) and separable, since this minimal polynomial as defined in (1.31) has no repeated roots.
    Let \(f(x)=\anmonic\text{.}\) To see why \(a_j\in L^H[x]\) for all \(1\leq j\leq n-1\text{,}\) observe that we have
    \begin{align*} f^\sigma \amp = \sigma(\prod_{j=1}^d(x-\alpha_j))\\ \amp = \prod_{j=1}^d(x-\sigma(\alpha_i))\\ \amp = \prod_{j=1}^d(x-\alpha_i) \amp (\sigma \text{ permutes } O_\alpha)\\ \amp =f(x) \end{align*}
    for all \(\sigma\in H\text{.}\) On the other hand, we have
    \begin{equation*} f^\sigma(x)=x^n+\sigma(a_{n-1})x^{n-1}+\cdots +\sigma(a_1)x+\sigma(a_0) \end{equation*}
    for any \(\sigma\in H\text{.}\) Thus we see that \(f^\sigma=f\) for all \(\sigma\in H\) implies \(\sigma(a_j)=a_j\) for all \(1\leq j\leq n-1\) for all \(\sigma\in H\text{,}\) which implies \(a_j\in L^H\) for all \(1\leq j\leq n-1\text{,}\) as desired.
    We now show that \(f\) as defined in (1.31) is the minimal polynomial of \(\alpha\) over \(L^H\text{.}\) Since \(f(\alpha)=0\) and \(f\in L^H[x]\text{,}\) we have \(m_{\alpha,L^H}(x)\mid f(x)\text{.}\) To show we have an equality, it suffices to show that \(f\mid m_{\alpha, L^H}(x)\text{;}\) and for this it suffices to show that \((x-\alpha_j)\mid m_{\alpha,L^H}(x)\) for the distinct (simple) roots \(\alpha_j\) of \(f(x)\text{.}\) Fix an element \(\alpha_j\in O_\alpha\text{.}\) By definition we have \(\alpha_j=\sigma(\alpha)\) for some \(\sigma\in H\text{,}\) in which case
    \begin{align*} m_{\alpha,L^H}(\alpha_j) \amp =m_{\alpha,L^H}(\sigma(\alpha)) \\ \amp = m_{\alpha,L^H}^\sigma(\sigma(\alpha) \amp (m_{\alpha,L^H}(x)\in L^H[x])\\ \amp = \sigma(m_{\alpha,L^H}(\alpha)) \amp (\sigma \text{ ring homo.})\\ \amp =\sigma(0)\\ \amp =0\text{.} \end{align*}
    We now know that \(L/L^H\) is Galois. It remains to show that \(L/L^H\) is finite and \(\Aut(L/L^H)=H\text{.}\) Let \(\abs{H}=n\text{.}\) As we saw above, for any \(\alpha\in L\) the polynomial \(f(x)=\prod_{\beta\in O_{\alpha}}(x-\beta)\) is the minimal polynomial of \(\alpha\) over \(L^H\text{.}\) It follows that for all \(\alpha\in L\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation} \deg_{L^H}\alpha=\deg f=\abs{O_\alpha}\leq \abs{H}=n\text{.}\tag{1.32} \end{equation}
    Thus for all \(\alpha\in L\text{,}\) the subextension \(L^H(\alpha)\) has degree \([L^H(\alpha)\colon L]\leq n\text{.}\) We claim this implies that \([L\colon L^H]\leq n\text{.}\) Indeed, otherwise we could find a subextension \(K/L^H\) with \([K\colon L^H]=m > n\text{.}\) But since \(L/L^H\) is separable (shown above), so is \(K/L^H\text{,}\) and by the primitive element theorem, we have \(K=L^H(\beta)\) for some \(\beta\in K\text{.}\) This contradicts (1.32) since then
    \begin{equation*} \deg_{L^H}\beta=[K\colon L^H]=m> n\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Lastly, we show that \([L\colon L^H]=n\) and \(\Gal(L/L^H)=\Aut_{L^H}(L)=H\text{.}\) We showed above that \([L\colon L^H]\leq n\text{.}\) Since \(H\leq \Aut_{L^H}(L)\text{,}\) and since \(\abs{\Aut_{L^H}(L)}=[L\colon L^H]\) (\(L/L^H\) is a finite Galois extension), we have
    \begin{equation*} n=\abs{H}\leq \abs{\Aut_{L^H}(L)}=[L\colon L^H]\leq n\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It follows that \(n=\abs{H}=\abs{\Aut_{L^H}(L)}=[L\colon L^H]\) and \(H=\Aut_{L^H}(L)=\Gal(L/L^H)\text{.}\)
