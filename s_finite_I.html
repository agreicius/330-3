    Section 1.9 Finite fields: existence and uniqueness

    As yet we have only seen two concrete examples of algebraic closure: the extensions \(\C/\R\) and \(\overline{\Q}_\C/\Q\text{.}\) The first example is about as simple as we could hope for: since \([\C\colon \R]=2\text{,}\) elements of \(\C\) are easily described (\(\alpha=r+si\text{,}\) \(r,s\in \R\)), and furthermore there are no nontrivial subextensions of \(\C/\R\text{.}\) Contrast this with the situation for \(\Q\) and its algebraic closure \(\overline{\Q}=\overline{\Q}_\C\text{.}\) We have \([\overline{\Q}\colon \Q]=\infty\text{,}\) there is no standard way of writing an arbitrary element of \(\overline{Q}\text{,}\) and there are infinitely many nontrivial subextensions. Indeed, there are even infinitely many subextensions of degree two!
    In this and the next section we will investigate what the algebraic closure \(\overline{\F_p}/\F_p\) of the finite field \(\F_p\) looks like. Despite this algebraic closure being an infinite one, you will see that we can describe the subextensions of \(\overline{\F_p}/\F_p\) and their interdependence more or less explicitly. As such the example of an algebraic closure \(\overline{\F_p}/\F_p\) occupies a nice middle ground between our previous two examples.
    For this and the next section \(p\) will always denote a prime integer.

    Theorem 1.9.1. Finite fields.

    Let \(\Fpbar/\F_p\) be an algebraic closure and let \(n\) be a positive integer.
    1. There is a unique subextension \(K/\F_p\) of \(\Fpbar/\F_p\) of degree \(n\text{.}\)
    2. We have \(\abs{K}=p^n\text{.}\)
    3. Let \(f(x)=x^{p^n}-x\text{.}\) We have
      \begin{equation} K=\{\alpha\in \Fpbar\colon f(\alpha)=0\}=\{\alpha\in \Fpbar\colon \alpha^{p^n}=\alpha\}\text{.}\tag{1.16} \end{equation}
      It follows that \(K\) is a splitting field of \(f(x)\text{.}\)
    The essential tool in the proof of Theorem 1.9.1 is the Frobenius endomorphism, which we now investigate.

    Definition 1.9.2. Frobenius endomorphism.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring of characteristic \(p\text{.}\) The Frobenius endomorphism of \(R\) is the map
    \begin{align*} \phi\colon R \amp \rightarrow R\\ r \amp \mapsto r^p\text{.} \end{align*}

    Theorem 1.9.3. Frobenius endomorphism.

    Let \(R\) be a ring of characteristic \(p\text{,}\) and let \(\phi\colon R\rightarrow R\) be the Frobenius endomorphism.
    1. \(\phi\) is a ring homomorphism. In particular, we have
      \begin{equation*} (r+s)^p=r^p+s^p \end{equation*}
      for all \(r,s\in R\text{.}\)
    2. For all positive integers \(n\) the map
      \begin{equation*} \phi^n=\underset{n \text{ times}}{\underbrace{\phi\circ\phi\circ\cdots \circ\phi}} \end{equation*}
      is a ring homomorphism and satisfies
      \begin{equation*} \phi^n(r)=r^{p^n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Statement (2) follows easily from (1) and the fact that a composition of ring homomorphisms is a ring homomorphism. We now show that \(\phi\) is a ring homomorphism. First observe that
    \begin{align*} \phi(1)\amp =1^p=1 \amp \phi(rs)=(rs)^p=r^ps^p \text{.} \end{align*}
    Furthermore, we have
    \begin{align*} \phi(-r) \amp =\phi((-1)r)\\ \amp = (-1)^pr^p\\ \amp =(-1)r^p \amp (p=2\implies -1=1)\\ \amp = -r^p\\ \amp = -\phi(r)\text{.} \end{align*}
    It remains only to show \(\phi(r+s)=\phi(r)+\phi(s)\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{align*} \phi(r+s) \amp = (r+s)^p\\ \amp = \sum_{k=0}^p {p \choose k}r^{p-k}s^{k} \amp (\text{gen. binomial thm.})\\ \amp = r^p+s^p \amp \left(k\ne 0,p \implies p\mid {p\choose k}\right)\\ \amp = \phi(r)+\phi(s)\text{.} \end{align*}

    Remark 1.9.4. First-year exponentiation/first-year’s dream.

    The bogus claim that \((r+s)^2=r^2+s^2\) or \((r+s)^3=r^3+s^3\) for real numbers \(r,s\in \R\text{,}\) is often called “first-year exponentiation”, since this is a common mistake in introductory mathematics classes. Accordingly, when working in a ring \(R\) of characteristic \(p\text{,}\) the amazing fact that
    \begin{equation} (r+s)^{p^n}=r^{p^n}+s^{p^n}\tag{1.17} \end{equation}
    for all \(r,s\in R\) and all positive integers \(n\) is sometimes referred to as “the first-year’s dream”.
    We are now ready to prove Theorem 1.9.1.

    Proof of Theorem 1.9.1.

    Fix positive integer \(n\text{.}\) Suppose \(K/\F_p\) is a field extension of degree \(n\text{.}\) Since \(\dim_{\F_p} K=n\text{,}\) we have \(K\cong \F_p^n\) and thus \(\abs{K}=p^n\text{.}\) Next, since \(K^*\) is a group of order \(p^n-1\text{,}\) we have \(\alpha^{p^n-1}=1\) for all \(\alpha\in K-\{0\}\text{,}\) which implies \(\alpha^{p^n}-\alpha=0\) for all elements \(\alpha\in K\) (including \(0\)). Since \(K\) has \(p^n\) elements, and since \(f(x)=x^{p^n}-x\) has at most \(p^n\) distinct roots, we see that we must have
    \begin{equation} K=\{\alpha\in\Fpbar\colon \alpha^{p^n}=\alpha\}\text{.}\tag{1.18} \end{equation}
    In particular \(K\) splits \(f(x)\) and is minimal in this regard (by a counting argument). Thus \(K\) is a a splitting field of \(f\text{.}\)
    So far, we have shown that there can be at most one subfield \(K\) of \(\Fpbar\) of degree \(n\text{,}\) that it is a splitting field of \(f(x)=x^{p^n}-x\text{,}\) and that it has the explicit description (1.18). Thus it remains to show that (a) the set \(K\) described by (1.18) is indeed a subfield of \(\Fpbar\text{,}\) and (b) that \(\abs{K}=p^n\text{.}\)
    It follows easily from the definition (1.18) that \(K\) contains \(0\) and \(1\text{,}\) and is closed under multiplication and inverses. It suffices then to show that \(K\) is closed under addition. To this end, given \(\alpha,\beta\in K\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} (\alpha+\beta)^{p^r} \amp = \alpha^{p^r}+\beta^{p^r} \amp (\text{first-year's dream}) \\ \amp =\alpha+\beta \amp (\alpha, \beta\in K)\text{.} \end{align*}
    This completes the proof that \(K=\{\alpha\in\Fpbar\colon \alpha^{p^n}=\alpha\}\) is a field.
    Since \(K\) is by definition the set of all roots of \(f(x)=x^{p^n}-x\) in \(\Fpbar\text{,}\) it remains only to show that these roots are distinct: i.e., \(f\) has no repeated roots. Such polynomials are called separable, and we will have a lot more to say about this property in coming lectures, including some useful tools for proving that a polynomial is separable. We forego these tools for now and instead rely on some algebraic trickery.
    Let \(\alpha\in K\) be a root of \(f(x)\text{.}\) Since \(\alpha^{p^n}=\alpha\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp = x^{p^n}-x-(\alpha^{p^n}-\alpha)\\ \amp = x^{p^n}-\alpha^{p^n}-(x-\alpha)\\ \amp = (x-\alpha)^{p^n}-(x-\alpha) \amp (\text{first-year's dream!})\\ \amp = (x-\alpha)((x-\alpha)^{p^{n}-1}-1)\\ \amp =(x-\alpha)g(x)\text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(g(x)=(x-\alpha)^{p^{n}-1}-1\text{.}\) Since \(g(\alpha)=-1\ne 0\text{,}\) we see that \(\alpha\) is not a root of \(g(x)\text{.}\) Since \(f(x)=(x-\alpha)g(x)\text{,}\) and \(\alpha\) is not a root of \(g(x)\text{,}\) we see that \(\alpha\) is not a repeated root of \(f\text{,}\) as desired.
    We end this portion of our investigation of \(\Fpbar/\F_p\) with the claim that for all positive integers \(n\text{,}\) the unique subfield \(K\) of cardinality \(n\) is in fact a simple extension of \(\F_p\text{.}\) To prove this claim, we need the following cute application of the structure theorem of finitely generated abelian groups.

    Let \(K\) be a field. Any finite subgroup of \(K^*\text{,}\) the group of units, is cyclic.

    Theorem 1.9.6. Finite fields are simple.

    If \(K/\F_p\) is a finite extension, then \(K=\F_p(\alpha)\) for some \(\alpha\in \Fpbar\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Since \(K^*\) is cyclic, we have \(K^*=\langle \alpha\rangle=\{1, \alpha, \alpha^2,\dots\}\) for some \(\alpha\in K\text{.}\) It follows easily that \(K=F(\alpha)\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.9.7. Existence of irreducibles.

    For all positive integers \(n\text{,}\) there is an irreducible polynomial of \(\F_p[x]\) of degree \(n\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Fix a positive integer \(n\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.9.1 there is an extension \(K/\F_p\) of degree \(n\text{.}\) By Corollary 1.9.8, we have \(K=\F_p(\alpha)\) for some \(\alpha\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.4.14 we have \(K\cong \F_p[x]/(f(x))\) for some irreducible polynomial of degree \(n\text{.}\) In particular, there is at least one irreducible polynomial of degree \(n\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.9.8. Uniqueness of finite fields.

    Fix a positive integer \(n\text{.}\) Any two fields of cardinality \(p^n\) are isomorphic. In particular, given any two irreducible polynomials \(f,g\in \F_p[x]\) of degree \(n\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \F_p[x]/(f)\cong \F_p[x]/(g)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Fix a positive integer \(n\text{.}\) Let \(\Fpbar/\F_p\) and let \(K/\F_p\) be the unique subextension of \(\Fpbar/\F_p\) of cardinality \(p^n\text{.}\) Given any other field \(K'\) of cardinality \(p^n\text{,}\) we must have \(\character K'=p\text{,}\) and hence \(\F_p\leq K'\) is a finite extension. By Corollary 1.9.8, \(K'=\F_p(\alpha)\) for some \(\alpha\in K'\text{,}\) and thus \(K'\cong \F_p[x]/(f)\) for some irreducible polynomial \(f\in \F_p[x]\) of degree \(n\text{.}\) Let \(\alpha\in \Fpbar\) be a root of \(f\text{,}\) and let
    \begin{align*} \phi\colon \F_p[x]/(f(x)) \amp \Fpbar \\ \overline{h}(x)\amp\mapsto h(\alpha) \text{.} \end{align*}
    By Theorem 1.4.7, \(\phi\) is a ring homomorphism. It follows that \(K'\cong\phi(K')\leq \Fpbar\text{,}\) Since \(\abs{\phi(K')}=p^n\text{,}\) we must have \(\phi(K')=K\text{,}\) by the uniqueness claim in Theorem 1.9.1. This proves that any field of cardinality \(p^n\) is isomorphic to the unique subfield of \(\Fpbar/\F_p\) of cardinality \(p^n\text{;}\) we conclude by transitivity that any two such fields are isomorphic.
