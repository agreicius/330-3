    Section 1.5 Algebraic extensions: definition

    In this section we be begin to investigate in earnest the concept of an element (and a field extension) being algebraic. (In fact, the focus of the course from here on out will be more on algebraic elements, and less on transcendental elements.) An essential tool in our investigation will be the notion of the minimal polynomial of an algebraic element.

    Theorem 1.5.1. Minimal polynomial.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension, let \(\alpha\in K\) be algebraic, and let \(\phi\colon F[x]\rightarrow K\) be the evaluation at \(\alpha\) map. There is a unique polynomial \(f(x)=x^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots +a_1x+a_0\) satisfying the following equivalent conditions.
    1. \(f(x)\) is monic and satisfies \(\ker \phi=(f)\text{.}\)
    2. \(f(x)\) is a monic polynomial of minimal degree satisfying \(f(\alpha)=0\text{.}\)
    3. \(f(x)\) is a monic irreducible polynomial satisfying \(f(\alpha)=0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(\phi\colon F[x]\rightarrow K\) be the evaluation at \(\alpha\) map. Since \(\alpha\) is algebraic, according to Theorem 1.4.14 \(\ker\phi\) is nonzero and generated by an irreducible polynomial. Although the generator of \(\ker\phi\) is not in general unique, there is a unique monic irreducible generator \(f(x)\text{.}\) This gives us the unique existence of a polynomial as described in (1).
    Continue to assume that \(f\) is the unique monic generator of \(\ker \phi\text{.}\) The equivalence of conditions (1)-(3) follows from the following observations:
    • Since \(\ker\phi\) is a nonzero prime ideal, any generator of it must be irreducible.
    • A polynomial \(g\in \ker \phi\) if and only if \(g(\alpha)=0\text{.}\)
    • In general for a nonzero ideal \(I\leq F[x]\text{,}\) we have \(I=(g)\) if and only if \(g\) is an element of \(I\) of minimal degree.
    • Since in this case \(\ker \phi\) is a nonzero prime ideal, we have \(I=(g)\) if and only if \(g\) is an irreducible element of \(\ker \phi\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.5.2. Minimal polynomial.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension, and let \(\alpha\in K\) be algebraic. The unique monic polynomial satisfying the three equivalent conditions of Theorem 1.5.1 is called the minimal polynomial (or irreducible polynomial) of \(\alpha\) over \(F\text{,}\) and is denoted \(m_{\alpha, F}(x)\text{,}\) or just \(m_\alpha(x)\) if the base field \(F\) is understood.
    The degree of the algebraic element \(\alpha\) over \(F\text{,}\) denoted \(\deg_F \alpha\text{,}\) is defined as the degree of the minimal polynomial of \(\alpha\text{:}\) i.e., \(\deg_F\alpha=\deg m_{\alpha, F}(x)\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.5.3. Minimal polynomial.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension, and let \(\alpha\in K\) be an algebraic element with minimal polynomial \(m_{\alpha}(x)\text{.}\)
    1. Let \(n=\deg m_\alpha(x)\text{.}\) The set \(B=\{1,\alpha, \dots, \alpha^{n-1}\}\) is an \(F\)-basis of \(F(\alpha)\text{,}\) and thus \([F(\alpha)\colon F]=n\text{.}\)
    2. Given \(f\in F[x]\text{,}\) we have \(f(\alpha)=0\) if and only if \(m_{\alpha, F}(x)\mid f(x)\text{.}\) In particular, if \(f(\alpha)=0\text{,}\) then \(m_{\alpha,F}(x)\) is one of the monic irreducible factors of \(f(x)\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. This follows from the fact that \(F(\alpha)\cong F[x]/(m_\alpha(x))\text{,}\) according to Theorem 1.4.7.
    2. Let \(\ker \phi\) be the kernel of the evaluation at \(\alpha\) map \(\phi\colon F[x]\rightarrow K\text{.}\) By Theorem 1.5.1, we have \(\ker\phi=(m_\alpha(x))\text{.}\) We conclude that
      \begin{align*} f(\alpha)=0 \amp \iff f\in \ker\phi\\ \amp \iff m_{\alpha,F}(x)\mid f\text{,} \end{align*}
      as desired.

    Example 1.5.4. Minimal polynomials for \(\C/\R\).

    Consider the field extension \(\C/\R\) and the element \(i\in \C\text{.}\) Since \(i\) is a root of of the polynomial \(f(x)=x^2+1\text{,}\) and since this polynomial is irreducible over and monic over \(\R\text{,}\) we have \(m_{i,\R}(x)=x^2+1\)
    More generally, given any \(\alpha=a+bi\in \C-\R\text{,}\) since \(\alpha\) is the root of
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-\alpha)(x+\overline{\alpha})=x^2-2ax+(a^2+b^2)\in \R[x]\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and since \(\alpha\) is not the root of any linear polynomial (since \(\alpha\notin \R\)), we see that
    \begin{equation*} m_{\alpha,\R}(x)=x^2-2ax+(a^2+b^2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.5.5. Minimal polynomial of root of unity.

    Consider the extension \(\C/\Q\text{,}\) and let
    \begin{equation*} \zeta=e^{2\pi i/8}=e^{\pi i/4}=\cos \pi/4+i\, \sin \pi/4\text{,} \end{equation*}
    a primitive \(8\)-th root of unity.
    1. Compute \(m_{\zeta,\R}(x)\text{.}\)
    2. Compute \(m_{\zeta, \Q}(x)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. Following Example 1.5.4, we have
      \begin{equation*} m_{\zeta,\R}(x)=x^2-2\cos \pi/4 x+(\cos^2 \pi/4+\sin^2 \pi/4)=x^2-\sqrt{2}x+1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. Since \(\zeta^8=1\text{,}\) we have \(f(\zeta)=0\text{,}\) where \(f(x)=x^8-1\text{.}\) According to Corollary 1.5.3, the minimal polynomial of \(\zeta\) is one of the monic irreducible polynomials appearing in the factorization of \(f(x)\) over \(\Q\text{.}\) Let’s factor:
      \begin{align*} x^8-1 \amp = (x^4-1)(x^4+1)\\ \amp = (x^2-1)(x^2+1)(x^4+1)\\ \amp = (x-1)(x+1)(x^2+1)(x^4+1)\text{.} \end{align*}
      It is not difficult to show that \(x^4+1\) is irreducible over \(\Q\text{.}\) (By Gauss’s lemma we need only show that it is irreducible over \(\Z\text{.}\)) Since \(\zeta\) is not a root of \((x-1)\text{,}\)\((x+1)\text{,}\) or \(x^2+1\) (whose roots are \(\pm i\)), we conclude that \(m_{\zeta,\Q}(x)=x^4+1\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.5.6. Algebraic extension.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. We say \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\) if all elements of \(K\) are algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)

    Example 1.5.7. Algebraic extensions.

    The extension \(\R/\Q\) is not algebraic, since \(\pi\in \R\) is transcendental over \(\Q\text{.}\)
    The extension \(\C/\R\) is algebraic. As we have shown previously, any element \(a+bi\in \C\) satisfies the quadratic polynomial \(x^2+2ax+(a^2+b^2)\text{.}\)

    Example 1.5.8. Simple extension with algebraic generator.

    Let \(K=F(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha\) is algebraic. Prove that \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    According to Theorem 1.4.14, we have \(\dim_F K=[K:F]=n\) for some finite \(n\text{.}\) Given \(\beta\in K\text{,}\) consider the set \(P=\{\beta^n,\beta^{n-1},\dots, \beta, 1\}\) of the powers of \(\beta\text{.}\) If we have \(\beta^j=\beta^k\) for two powers \(1\leq i< j\leq n\text{,}\) then we have \(\beta^{k-j}=1\text{,}\) and \(\beta\) satisfies the nonzero polynomial \(f(x)=x^{k-j}-1\text{,}\) and is thus algebraic. If the powers are all distinct, since then \(\abs{P}=n+1> n\text{,}\) \(P\) is linearly dependent (1.3.3,“street smarts”). Thus there is a nontrivial linear combination
    \begin{equation*} a_n\beta^n+a_{n-1}\beta^{n-1}+\cdots +a_1\beta+a_0=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, we have \(f(\beta)=0\text{,}\) where \(f(x)=\anpoly\text{,}\) and \(f(x)\ne 0\text{.}\) Thus \(\beta\) is algebraic in this case as well. Since \(\beta\) was arbitrary, we conclude that \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)
