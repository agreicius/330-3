    Section 1.5 Algebraic extensions

    Definition 1.5.1. Finitely generated.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. We say \(K\) is finitely generated over \(F\) if we have \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\) for some finite set \(\{\alpha_1, \alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\}\subseteq K\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.5.2. Algebraic extension.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. We say \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\) if all elements of \(K\) are algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)

    Example 1.5.3. Algebraic extensions.

    The extension \(\R/\Q\) is not algebraic, since \(\pi\in \R\) is transcendental over \(\Q\text{.}\)
    The extension \(\C/\R\) is algebraic. As we have shown previously, any element \(a+bi\in \C\) satisfies the quadratic polynomial \(x^2+2ax+(a^2+b^2)\text{.}\)

    Example 1.5.4. Simple extension with algebraic generator.

    Let \(K=F(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha\) is algebraic. Prove that \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    According to Theorem 1.4.14, we have \(\dim_F K=[K:F]=n\) for some finite \(n\text{.}\) It follows that given any \(\beta\in K\text{,}\) the set \(\{\beta^n,\beta^{n-1},\dots, \beta, 1\}\) is linearly dependent, since it has more than \(n\) elements (1.3.3,“street smarts”). Thus there is a nontrivial linear combination
    \begin{equation*} a_n\beta^n+a_{n-1}\beta^{n-1}+\cdots +a_1\beta+a_0=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, we have \(f(\beta)=0\text{,}\) where \(f(x)=\anpoly\text{.}\) This proves \(\beta\) is algebraic, as desired.
    The main result of this section is Theorem 1.5.10, which both generalizes Example 1.5.4 and gives a full characterization of finite extensions in terms of finitely generated and algebraic field extensions. First we treat the tower law, which will be instrumental in proving Theorem 1.5.10, and is also quite useful in its own right.

    Theorem 1.5.5. Tower law.

    Let \(L/K/F\) be a tower of (possibly infinite) field extensions.
    1. If \(B_1\) is a basis of \(K\) and \(B_2\) is a basis of \(L\) as a \(K\)-vector space, then the set
      \begin{equation} B=\{\alpha\cdot \beta\colon \alpha\in B_1, \beta\in B_2\}\tag{1.5} \end{equation}
      is a basis of \(L\) as an \(F\)-vector space.
    2. We have
      \begin{equation} [L\colon F]=[L\colon K][K\colon F]\text{.}\tag{1.6} \end{equation}

    Remark 1.5.6. Tower law.

    Statement (1) of Theorem 1.5.5 is just as important as (2), as it gives us a procedure for building a basis for \(L/F\) from bases \(B_1\) of \(L/K\) and \(B_2\) of \(K/F\text{:}\) namely, we simply take the set of all products of elements in \(B_1\) and \(B_2\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.5.7. Tower law: infinite extensions.

    The formula (1.6) is true even when one or more of the field extensions involved is infinite, using the following rules from “extended arithmetic”:
    \begin{align*} \infty \amp = n\cdot \infty=\infty\cdot n=\infty\cdot \infty \text{ for all positive } n\in \Z \\ \infty \amp\ne m\cdot n \text{ for all positive } m,n \in \Z \text{.} \end{align*}
    Equivalently, we can interpret (1.6) as stating that the extension \(L/F\) is infinite if and only if \(L/K\) is infinite or \(K/F\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.5.8. Tower law.

    Let \(L/F\) be a field extension.
    1. If \(K/F\) is a subextension of \(L/F\text{,}\) then \([K\colon F]\mid [L\colon F]\) and \([L\colon K]\mid [L\colon F]\text{.}\)
    2. Given a tower of extensions
      \begin{equation*} F_1\leq F_2\leq \cdots \leq F_n\text{,} \end{equation*}
      we have
      \begin{equation*} [F_n\colon F_1]=\prod_{k=1}^{n-1}[F_{k+1}\colon F_k]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.5.9. Tower law.

    Use the tower law to prove that the field extension \(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})/\Q\) has no nontrivial subextension \(K/\Q\text{:}\) i.e., if \(\Q\leq K\leq \Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\text{,}\) then \(K=\Q\) or \(K=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.5.10. Finite extension theorem.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(K/F\) is finite.
    2. \(K/F\) is finitely generated and algebraic.
    3. \(K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_m)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha_i\in K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) In other words, \(K\) is generated over \(F\) by finitely many algebraic elements.

    Remark 1.5.11. Finite extension theorem.

    As we saw in the proof of Theorem 1.5.10, given a field extension \(K/F\) the following implications hold:
    \begin{align*} K/F \text{ finite} \amp\implies K/F \text{ finitely generated} \\ K/F \text{ finite} \amp\implies K/F \text{ algebraic} \text{.} \end{align*}
    On the other hand, neither of these implications goes the other way. That is, we have
    \begin{align*} K/F \text{ finite } \hspace{5pt} \amp\not\!\!\!\!\!\!\impliedby K/F \text{ finitely generated} \\ K/F \text{ finite } \hspace{5pt} \amp\not\!\!\!\!\!\!\impliedby K/F \text{ algebraic} \text{.} \end{align*}
    In other words, taken singly, neither of these two properties (finitely generated, algebraic) is strong enough to imply the property of being finite. Theorem 1.5.10 tells us that the two properties taken together are in fact equivalent to being finite: i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} K/F \text{ finite } \iff K/F \text{ finitely generated and algebraic}\text{.} \end{equation*}
