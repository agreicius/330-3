    Section 1.20 Galois groups of polynomials

    Definition 1.20.1. Galois group of polynomial.

    Let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a separable polynomial, and let \(\Fbar/F\) be an algebraic closure. Denote by \(K_f\) the splitting field of \(f\text{:}\) i.e., we have
    \begin{equation*} K=F(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\leq \overline{F}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\) are the roots of \(f\) over \(\overline{F}\text{.}\) We call \(\Gal(K_f/F)\) the Galois group of \(f\), and write \(G_f=\Gal(K_f/F)\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.20.2. Permutation group.

    Let \(X=\{1,2,\dots, n\}\) and let \(S_n=S_X\text{,}\) the group of permutations of \(X\text{.}\) A permutation group is a subgroup \(H\) of \(S_n\text{.}\) A permutation group \(H\leq S_n\) is transitive if for any pair \(i\ne j\in X\) there is a \(\sigma\in H\) such that \(\sigma(i)=j\text{:}\) i.e., if the action of \(H\) on \(X\) is transitive.

    Theorem 1.20.3. Galois group as permutation group.

    Let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a separable irreducible polynomial of degree \(n\text{,}\) let \(\Fbar/F\text{,}\) and let \(R=\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\}\subseteq \Fbar\) be the roots of \(f\text{.}\)
    1. Each \(\sigma\in G_f\) defines a permutation
      \begin{align*} \sigma\colon R \amp \rightarrow R\\ \alpha_i \amp\mapsto \sigma(\alpha_i)=\alpha_{i'} \text{.} \end{align*}
      There is an injective group homomorphism
      \begin{align*} \phi\colon G_f \amp \rightarrow S_n \end{align*}
      defined as follows: \(\phi(\sigma)\) is the permutation
      \begin{align*} \{1,2,\dots, n\} \amp \xrightarrow{\ \phi(\sigma)\ } \{1,2,\dots, n\}\\ i \amp \xmapsto{\hspace{20pt}} i'\text{,} \end{align*}
      where \(\sigma(\alpha_i)=\alpha_{i'}\text{.}\)
    2. The image \(\im \phi=\phi(G_f)\) is a transitive subgroup of \(S_n\) satisfying
      \begin{equation*} n\mid \abs{\phi(G_f)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      In other words, \(G_f\) is isomorphic to a transitive subgroup of \(S_n\) whose cardinality is a multiple of \(n\text{.}\)
    According to theorem Theorem 1.20.3, we can identify \(G_f\) with a transitive subgroup of \(S_n\) whose cardinality is a multiple of \(n\text{.}\) We now introduce tools that help us narrow down the possibilities even further, the first of which is the discriminant.

    Definition 1.20.4. Discriminant of polynomial.

    Let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a monic polynomial of degree \(n\text{,}\) let \(\Fbar/F\) be an algebraic closure, and assume \(f\) factors over \(\Fbar\) as
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-\alpha_1)(x-\alpha_2)\cdots (x-\alpha_n)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The discriminant of \(f\), denoted \(\Delta_f\) is defined as
    \begin{equation} \Delta_f=\prod_{1\leq i< j\leq n}(\alpha_j-\alpha_i)^2\text{.}\tag{1.36} \end{equation}
    Looking at the defining expression (1.36), we see that the discriminant \(\Delta_f\) of a polynomial \(f\) lives somewhere in the algebraic closure, or more specifically, in the splitting field of \(f\text{.}\) Somewhat surprisingly, as we will see in Theorem 1.20.9, the discriminant is in fact an element of the base field \(F\text{.}\) Even more surprisingly, the discriminant can be expressed as a polynomial expression in the coefficients of \(f\text{.}\) (See Remark 1.20.12 for an explanation of why this is the case.) Theorem 1.20.10 provides some examples of this phenomenon for a few types of small-degree polynomial. In the meantime, it is worth computing a few actual examples directly, if only to see why it is not obvious that the discriminant lies in the base field \(F\text{.}\)

    Example 1.20.5. Discriminant of quadratic.

    Let \(F\) be any field, and consider a general monic quadratic polynomial \(f(x)=x^2+bx+c\in F[x]\text{.}\) Factor \(f(x)=(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)\) over an algebraic closure \(\overline{F}\text{.}\) Expanding out this factorization we see that
    \begin{align*} b \amp =-(\alpha+\beta)\\ c \amp = \alpha\beta\text{.} \end{align*}
    It follows from a little algebra that
    \begin{align*} \Delta_f \amp = (\beta-\alpha)^2\\ \amp = (a\alpha+\beta)^2-4\alpha\beta\\ \amp = b^2-4c\text{.} \end{align*}
    In particular, since \(b,c\in F\text{,}\) we have \(\Delta_f\in F\text{.}\) You can verify our answer with Table 1.20.11.

    Theorem 1.20.6. Splitting fields of quadratics and cubics.

    Let \(F\) be a field of cardinality not equal to \(2\text{,}\) let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a monic but not necessarily irreducible polynomial, and let \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}\in \overline{F}\) be a square-root of \(\Delta_f\text{.}\)
    1. If \(\deg f=2\text{,}\) then \(K_f=F(\sqrt{\Delta_f})\)
    2. If \(\deg f=3\text{,}\) then \(K_f=F(\alpha, \sqrt{\Delta_f})\text{,}\) where \(\alpha\in \overline{F}\) is any root of \(f\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. Let \(f(x)=x^2+ax+b=(x-\alpha)(x-\beta)\text{.}\) We saw in Example 1.20.5 that \(\Delta_f=b^2-4ac\text{.}\) Since \(\character F\ne 2\text{,}\) the quadratic formula applies, and we have
      \begin{align*} \{\alpha,\beta\} \amp =\left\{\frac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4c}}{2}, \frac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4c}}{2}\right\} \\ \amp =\left\{\frac{-b+\sqrt{\Delta_f}}{2},\frac{-b-\sqrt{\Delta_f}}{2}\right\}\text{.} \end{align*}
      It follows that
      \begin{equation*} K_f=F(\alpha,\beta)=F(\sqrt{\Delta_f})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. Let \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma\) be the roots of \(f\text{,}\) and write \(f(x)=(x-\alpha)g(x)\text{,}\) where \(g(x)=(x-\beta)(x-\gamma)\) and \(g(x)\in F(\alpha)[x]\text{.}\) By (1), we have
      \begin{align*} K_f \amp =F(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)\\ \amp =F(\alpha)(\beta,\gamma)\\ \amp =F(\alpha)(\sqrt{\Delta_g})\text{,} \end{align*}
      where \(\sqrt{\Delta_g}\in \overline{F}\) is a square-root of \(\Delta_g=(\gamma-\beta)^2\text{.}\) Since
      \begin{align*} \Delta_f\amp= (\alpha-\beta)^2(\alpha-\gamma)^2(\gamma-\beta)^2 \\ \amp = g(\alpha)^2\Delta_g\text{,} \end{align*}
      we see that \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}/g(\alpha)=\sqrt{\Delta_g}\text{,}\) and since \(g(\alpha)\in F(\alpha)\text{,}\) we conclude that
      \begin{equation*} K_f=F(\alpha,\sqrt{\Delta_g})=F(\alpha,\sqrt{\Delta_f}/g(\alpha))=F(\alpha,\sqrt{\Delta_f})\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.20.7. Discriminant of quartic.

    Let \(f(x)=x^4+1\in \Q[x]\text{.}\) We recognize \(f\) as the \(4\)-th cyclotomic polynomial \(\Phi_4(x)\text{.}\) Thus the roots of \(f\) are the primitive \(4\)-th roots of unity. In other words, letting \(\zeta=e^{2\pi i/8}\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-\zeta)(x-\zeta^3)(x-\zeta^5)(x-\zeta^7)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The discriminant of \(f\) is thus
    \begin{align*} \Delta_f \amp = [(\zeta^3-\zeta)(\zeta^5-\zeta^3)(\zeta^7-\zeta^5)(\zeta^7-\zeta)(\zeta^7-\zeta^3)(\zeta^5-\zeta)]^2 ]\\ \amp = [(-\sqrt{2})(-\sqrt{2}i)(\sqrt{2})(-\sqrt{2}i)(2\zeta^7)(-2\zeta)]^2 \amp (\zeta^7=-\zeta^3, \zeta^5=-\zeta)\\ \amp = 2^8i^4\zeta^8\\ \amp = 256\text{.} \end{align*}

    As we will see, the discriminant \(\Delta_f\) detects whether or not \(G_f\text{,}\) identified as a subgroup of \(S_n\text{,}\) lies within the alternating subgroup \(A_n\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.20.8. Alternating group.

    Given \(\sigma\in S_n\text{,}\) its sign (or parity), denoted \(\sgn \sigma\text{,}\) is defined as \(\sgn \sigma=(-1)^n\text{,}\) where \(\sigma\) can be written as a product of \(n\) \(2\)-cycles. The map
    \begin{align*} \sgn\colon S_n \amp\rightarrow \{\pm 1\}\\ \sigma \amp \mapsto \sgn \sigma \end{align*}
    is a surjective group homomorphism. We define the alternating subgroup of \(S_n\text{,}\) denoted \(A_n\text{,}\) to be the kernel of \(\sgn\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} A_n=\ker\sgn=\{\sigma\in S_n\colon \sgn \sigma=1\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.20.9. Discriminant and alternating group.

    Let \(f(x)\in F[x]\) be a separable irreducible polynomial. Denote by \(\sqrt{\Delta_f}\) a square-root of \(\Delta_f\) lying in an algebraic closure \(\overline{F}\text{.}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \Delta_f\in F\)
    2. Assume \(\character F\ne 2\text{.}\) Let \(H=\phi(G_f)\) be the isomorphic image of \(G_f\) in \(S_n\) under the homomorphism \(\phi\) of Theorem 1.20.3. We have
      \begin{equation*} \sqrt{\Delta_f}\in F \iff H\leq A_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Remark 1.20.12. Discriminant and symmetric polynomials.

    The expression (1.36) defining the discriminant is symmetric in the roots \(\alpha_i\text{.}\) More precisely, and more generally, a polynomial \(f(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\in F[x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n]\) in the variables \(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n\) is symmetric if for all \(\sigma\in S_n\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f(x_{\sigma(1)},x_{\sigma(2)},\dots, x_{\sigma(n)})=f(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    There is a rich, wonderful theory of symmetric polynomials that we do not have time to go into. However, it is worth pointing out the so-called elementary symmetric polynomials, a list of \(n\) specific symmetric polynomials \(s_k(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\text{,}\) \(1\leq k\leq n\text{.}\) These are defined in the following manner. Consider the polynomial \(G(x,x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) in the \(n+1\) variables \(x,x_1,\dots, x_n\) defined as
    \begin{equation} G(x,x_1,\dots, x_n)=(x-x_1)(x-x_2)\cdots (x-x_n)\text{.}\tag{1.37} \end{equation}
    Expanding \(G\) out as a polynomial in \(x\) with coefficients in \(F[x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n]\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} G \amp = x^n-(x_1+x_2+\cdots +x_n)x^{n-1}+\cdots+(-1)^n(x_1x_2\cdots x_n)\\ \amp = x^n-s_1(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)x^{n-1}+s_2(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)x^{n-2}+\cdots +(-1)^{n-1}s_{n-1}(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)+(-1)^{n}s_n(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\\ \amp = x^n+\sum_{k=1}^n(-1)^ks_k(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)x^k\text{.} \end{align*}
    Each polynomial \(s_k(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) is a symmetric polynomial in \(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n\text{,}\) and we call these the elementary symmetric polynomials for \(n\text{.}\) When \(n=3\text{,}\) for example, we have
    \begin{align*} s_1 \amp =x_1+x_2+x_3 \amp s_2\amp = x_1x_2+x_1x_3+x_2x_3 \amp s_3\amp=x_1x_2x_3 \text{.} \end{align*}
    Amazingly, any symmetric polynomial can be written as a polynomial expression of the elementary polynomials \(s_k\text{.}\) In other words, if \(g(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\) is a symmetric polynomial, then we have
    \begin{equation*} g=H(s_1,s_2,\dots, s_n) \end{equation*}
    for some polynomial \(H(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)\text{.}\)
    Returning at last to the discriminant, with the setup as in Definition 1.20.4, we have
    \begin{equation*} \Delta_f=g(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(g\) is the symmetric polynomial
    \begin{equation*} g(x_1,x_2,\dots, x_n)=\prod_{1\leq i< j\leq n}(x_j-x_i)^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    By above we have \(g=H(s_1,s_2,\dots, s_n)\) for some polynomial \(H\text{,}\) in which case
    \begin{align*} \Delta_f \amp= g(\alpha_1,\dots, \alpha_n)\\ \amp = H(s_1(\alpha_1,\dots, \alpha_n),\dots, s_n(\alpha_1,\dots, \alpha_n))\text{.} \end{align*}
    Furthermore, since \(f(x)=G(x,\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n)\text{,}\) where \(G\) is as in (1.37), we see that by definition
    \begin{align*} s_k(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n) \amp = (-1)^ka_{n-k}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus we have
    \begin{align*} \Delta_f\amp=H(s_1(\alpha_1,\dots, \alpha_n),\dots, s_n(\alpha_1,\dots, \alpha_n))\\ \amp=H(-a_{n-1},a_{n-2},-a_{n-3}, \dots, (-1)^n\, a_0)\text{,} \end{align*}
    from whence it follows that \(\Delta_f\) is a polynomial expression in the coefficients \(a_k\) of \(f\text{.}\)
