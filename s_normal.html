    Section 1.11 Normal extensions

    As defined in Definition 1.11.7, a field extension \(K/F\) is normal if \(K\) is the splitting field of some collection of polynomials over \(F\text{.}\) Normal fields satisfy a particularly useful property with respect to extensions of field embeddings that will be important when studying Galois groups: in particular the normal subgroups of these Galois groups.

    Subsection Uniqueness of splitting fields and algebraic closures

    Theorem 1.11.1. Algebraic extensions of embeddings.

    Let \(K/F\) be an algebraic extension. Given an embedding \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\text{,}\) where \(L\) is an algebraically closed field, there is an extension \(\tau\) of \(\sigma\) to \(K\text{.}\)
    We postpone the proof of this theorem to the end of the section, as it relies on a general version of Zorn’s lemma. Two important consequences of Theorem 1.11.1 are that splitting fields of sets of polynomials and algebraic closures are unique up to isomorphism.

    Corollary 1.11.2. Uniqueness of splitting fields.

    Let \(F\) be a field, and let \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\) be a collection of polynomials over \(F\text{.}\) Given any splitting fields \(K/F\) and \(K'/F\) of \(\mathcal{P}\text{,}\) there is an \(F\)-isomorphism \(\sigma\colon K\rightarrow K'\text{.}\) In other words, any two splitting fields of \(\mathcal{P}\) are isomorphic over \(F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(K/F\) and \(K'/F\) be two splitting fields of \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\text{,}\) and let \(A_K\) and \(A_{K'}\) be defined as
    \begin{align} A_K \amp = \{\alpha\in K\colon f(\alpha)=0 \text{ for some } f\in \mathcal{P} \}\tag{1.23}\\ A_{K'} \amp = \{\alpha\in K'\colon f(\alpha)=0 \text{ for some } f\in \mathcal{P} \}\text{.}\tag{1.24} \end{align}
    In other words, \(A_K\) and \(A_{K'}\) are the sets of all roots of all elements of \(\mathcal{P}\) lying in \(K\text{,}\) respectively \(K'\text{.}\) Recall that, being splitting fields, \(K\) and \(K'\) are both algebraic over \(F\) and are generated over \(F\) by the roots of elements of \(\mathcal{P}\text{:}\) i.e., we have
    \begin{align*} K\amp =F(A_K) \amp K'=F(A_{K'}) \text{.} \end{align*}
    Let \(L/K'\) be an algebraic closure of \(K'\text{.}\) Applying Theorem 1.11.1 to the inclusion map \(i\colon F\hookrightarrow K'\leq L\text{,}\) we get an \(F\)-embedding \(\tau\colon K\rightarrow L\text{.}\)
    We claim that in fact \(\tau(K)=K'\leq L\text{,}\) in which case restricting the codomain yields an isomorphism \(\tau\colon K\rightarrow K'\text{.}\) We use the generator descriptions (1.23)–(1.24) to this end. Consider \(\tau(A_K)\) the image under \(\tau\) of the set of all roots of elements of \(\mathcal{P}\text{.}\) Since \(\tau\) is an \(F\)-embedding, it is easy to see that \(\tau(A_K)\subseteq A_{K'}\text{.}\) Indeed given \(\alpha\in A_K\text{,}\) if \(\alpha\) is a root of \(f(x)=\anpoly\in \mathcal{P}\text{,}\) then we have
    \begin{align*} 0=\tau(0)\amp=\tau(f(\alpha)) \\ \amp = \tau(a_n\alpha^{n}+\cdots+a_1\alpha+a_0)\\ \amp = a_n(\tau(\alpha))^n+\cdots +a_1\tau(\alpha)+a_0 \amp (\tau\in \Hom_F(K,L))\text{.} \end{align*}
    This argument shows, in general, that for any \(f\in \mathcal{P}\text{,}\) \(\tau\) maps the roots \(R_{f,K}\) of \(f\) in \(K\) to the roots \(R_{f,K'}\) of \(f\) in \(K'\text{:}\) i.e., restricting \(\tau\) to \(R_{f,K}\) yields an injective function \(\tau\colon R_{f,K}\rightarrow R_{f,K'}\text{.}\) In fact, this map is a bijection: since both \(K\) and \(K'\) split \(f\) completely, we have \(\abs{R_{f,K}}=\abs{R_{f,K'}}< \infty\text{,}\) and thus since \(\tau\) is injective it is also surjective! We conclude that \(\tau(R_{f,K})=R_{f,K'}\) for all polynomials \(f\in \mathcal{P}\text{,}\) from whence it follows immediately that \(\tau(A_K)=A_{K'}\text{.}\)
    Lastly, using the fact that \(K=F(A_K)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \tau(K)\amp =\tau(F(A_K)) \\ \amp = F(\tau(A_{K'}))\\ \amp = F(A_{K'})\\ \amp = K' \text{,} \end{align*}
    as claimed. The equality \(\tau(F(A_K))=F(\tau(A_K))\) above follows pretty much directly from the minimality property of generated fields: e.g., \(\tau(F(A_K))\) is a field containing \(F\) and \(\tau(A_K)\text{,}\) thus \(F(\tau(A_K))\leq \tau(F_K(A_K)) \)

    Corollary 1.11.3. Uniqueness of algebraic closure.

    Let \(F\) be a field. Given any two algebraic closures \(K/F\) and \(K'/F\text{,}\) there is an \(F\)-isomorphism \(\sigma\colon K\rightarrow K'\text{.}\) In other words, any two algebraic closures of \(F\) are isomorphic over \(F\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    By Corollary 1.8.6 any algebraic closure of \(F\) is a splitting field of \(\mathcal{P}=F[x]\text{.}\) The result now follows from Corollary 1.11.2.

    Subsection Automorphisms of fields

    Definition 1.11.4. Automorphisms.

    A ring isomorphism \(\sigma\colon R\rightarrow R\) from a ring to itself is called an automorphism.
    Given a field extension \(K/F\text{,}\) an \(F\)-embedding \(\sigma\colon K\rightarrow K\) that is also an automorphism is called an \(F\)-automorphism. We denote by \(\Aut_F(K)\) the set of all \(F\)-automorphisms of \(K\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \Aut_F(K)=\{\sigma\in \Hom_F(K,K)\colon \sigma \text{ an isomorphism}\}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.11.5. Automorphisms of algebraic extensions.

    Let \(K/F\) be an algebraic extension. If \(\sigma\colon K\rightarrow K\) is an \(F\)-embedding, then \(\sigma\) is an isomorphism.

    Proof.

    Homework exercise.

    Example 1.11.6. Automorphism.

    The condition that \(K/F\) be algebraic is indeed a necessary one for Theorem 1.11.5. Consider by contrast the transcendental extension \(F(x)/F\text{,}\) where as usual \(F(x)\) is the field of rational functions over \(F\text{.}\) It is easy to see that the map
    \begin{align*} \sigma\colon F(x) \amp \rightarrow F(x) \\ f(x)/g(x) \amp \mapsto f(x^2)/g(x^2) \end{align*}
    is an \(F\)-embedding, but is not surjective, hence not an automorphism.

    Definition 1.11.7.

    An field extension \(K/F\) is normal if \(K\) is the splitting field of a collection \(\mathcal{P}\subseteq F[x]\) of polynomials over \(F\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.11.8. Normal extensions.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension, and let \(\overline{K}\) be an algebraic closure of \(K\text{.}\) The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(K/F\) is normal.
    2. \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) and for all \(\sigma\in \Hom_F(K,\overline{K})\text{,}\) \(\sigma(K)=K\text{:}\) i.e., \(\Hom_F(K,\overline{K})=\Aut_F(K)\text{.}\)
    3. \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) and for all \(\alpha\in K\) the minimal polynomial \(m_{\alpha, F}(x)\) splits completely over \(K\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Subsection Zorn’s lemma and proof of Theorem 1.11.1

    The proof of Theorem 1.11.1 makes us of Zorn’s lemma, the statement of which requires some terminology about partially ordered sets.

    Definition 1.11.9. Partial ordering axioms.

    Let \(R\) denote a binary relation on the set \(I\text{.}\) We write \(iRj\) to assert that the relation \(R\) holds between elements \(i,j\in I\text{.}\) The relation \(R\) is a partial ordering if it satisfies the following three axioms.
    • Reflexive (PO1).
      For all \(i\in I\text{,}\) we have \(iRi\text{.}\)
    • Transitive (PO2).
      For all \(i,j,k\in I\text{,}\) if \(iRj\) and \(jRk\text{,}\) then \(iRk\text{.}\)
    • Antisymmetric (PO3).
      For all \(i,j\in I\text{,}\) if \(iRj\) and \(jRi\text{,}\) then \(i=j\text{.}\)
    A partially ordered set (POSET, for short) is a pair \((I,\leq)\text{,}\) where \(\leq\) is a partial ordering on \(I\text{.}\)

    Example 1.11.10. POSETS.

    1. The usual “less than or equal to” relation \(x\leq y\) is a partial ordering on \(\R\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(X\) be a set, and let \(I=\mathcal{P}(X)\) be the power set of of \(X\text{.}\) The relation \(A\leq B \iff A\subseteq B\) defines a partial ordering on \(I\text{.}\)
    3. Let \(X\) be a set, and let \(I=\mathcal{P}(X)\) be the power set of of \(X\text{.}\) The relation \(A\leq B \iff A\supseteq B\) also defines a partial ordering on \(I\text{.}\)
    4. Recall that an integer \(m\) divides an integer \(n\text{,}\) written \(m\mid n\text{,}\) if there is an integer \(q\) such that \(n=mq\text{.}\) The relation \(m\leq n\iff m\mid n\) defines a partial ordering on the set of nonnegative integers \(\mathbb{N}=\{n\in \Z\colon n\geq 0\}\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.11.11. Upper bounds, maximal elements, chains.

    Let \((I, \leq)\) be a partially ordered set.
    An upper bound on a subset \(J\subseteq I\) is an element \(i\in I\) such that \(i\geq j\) for all \(j\in J\text{.}\)
    An element \(i\in I\) is maximal if given any \(i'\in I\text{,}\) if \(i\leq i'\text{,}\) then \(i=i'\text{.}\)
    A subset \(J\subseteq I\) is a chain (or totally ordered) if for all \(j, j'\in J\) we have \(j\leq j'\) or \(j'\leq j\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.11.12. Zorn’s lemma.

    Let \((I,\leq)\) be a nonempty partially ordered set. If every chain \(J\subseteq I\) has an upper bound, then \(I\) has a maximal element: i.e., there is an \(i\in I\) such that for all \(i'\in I\text{,}\) if \(i\leq i'\text{,}\) then \(i=i'\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Zorn’s lemma is equivalent to the axiom of choice. You can find a proof of this fact in most introductory set theory texts. We will not give a proof here.
    We are ready at last to prove Theorem 1.11.1.

    Proof of Theorem 1.11.1.

    Assume \(K/F\) is algebraic and that \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\) is an embedding, with \(L\) algebraically closed. We apply Zorn’s lemma to the partially ordered set \((I,\leq)\text{,}\) where \(I\) is the set of all pairs \((K',\tau_{K'})\) consisting of a subextension \(K'/F\) of \(K/F\) and an extension \(\tau_{K'}\colon K'\rightarrow L\) of the embedding \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\text{,}\) and where the partial ordering \(\leq\) is defined as follows
    \begin{align*} (K',\tau_{K'})\leq (K'',\tau_{K''})\amp \iff K'\leq K'' \text{ and } \tau_{K''}\vert_{K'}=\tau_{K'} \text{.} \end{align*}
    It is straightforward to show that \(\leq\) as defined is a partial ordering of \(I\text{.}\) Our steps are then as follows:
    1. Use Zorn’s lemma to show that \(I\) contains a maximal elmenent \((K^*, \tau)\) with respect to the defined ordering \(\leq\text{.}\)
    2. Show that \(K^*=K\text{.}\)

    Proof:\((I,\leq)\) has maximal element.

    First observe that \(I\) is nonempty. Indeed, the pair \((F,\sigma)\) is an element of \(I\text{.}\)
    Next, let \(J\subseteq I\) be a chain with respect to \(\leq\text{.}\) We must show that \(J\) has an upper bound in \(I\text{.}\) Define \((K^\dagger, \tau)\) as follows:
    \begin{align*} K^\dagger \amp = \bigcup_{(K',\tau_{K'})\in J} K'\\ \tau \colon K^\dagger \amp\rightarrow L \\ \alpha \amp \mapsto \tau_{K'}(\alpha) \text{ for any } (K', \tau_{K'})\in J \text{ with } \alpha\in K'\text{.} \end{align*}
    It is not difficult to show that \(K^\dagger\) is a field (being a union of nested fields), that the map \(\tau\) is well-defined (i.e., it doesn’t depend on the pair \((K',\tau_{K'})\)), and is an extension of \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\text{.}\) It follows essentially by definition of \((K^\dagger, \tau)\) and \(\leq\) that \((K', \tau_{K'})\leq (K^\dagger, \tau)\) for all \((K', \tau_{K'})\in J\text{.}\) Thus \((K^\dagger, \tau)\) is an upper bound of \(J\text{.}\) Since \(J\) was arbitrary, we conclude by Zorn’s lemma that \(I\) has a maximal element \((K^*,\tau)\text{.}\)

    Proof: extension to \(K\) exists.

    Let \((K^*, \tau)\) be a maximal element of the partially ordered set \((I, \leq)\text{.}\) We claim that \(K^*=K\text{,}\) in which case \(\tau\colon K^*=K\rightarrow L\) is our desired extension of \(\sigma\text{.}\) By definition, \(K^*\leq K\text{.}\) To prove the other inclusion, take any \(\alpha\in K\text{.}\) Since \(K\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) it is also algebraic over \(K^*\text{,}\) in which case \(K^*(\alpha)/K^*\) is an algebraic simple extension. We apply Theorem 1.10.3 to the embedding \(\tau\colon K^*\rightarrow L\text{.}\) Let \(f(x)=m_{\alpha,K^*}(x)\in K^*[x]\text{.}\) Since \(L\) is algebraically closed, there is a root \(\beta\) of \(f^\tau(x)\in L[x]\text{,}\) and hence an extension \(\tau'\colon K^*(\alpha)\rightarrow L\text{.}\) Since \(K^*\leq K^*(\alpha)\text{,}\) and since \(\tau'\vert_K^*=\tau\) (by definition of embedding extensions), we see that
    \begin{equation*} (K^*, \tau)\leq (K^*(\alpha), \tau')\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \((K^*, \tau)\) is maximal with respect to our partial ordering, we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} (K^*, \tau)= (K^*(\alpha), \tau')\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and hence \(K^*=K^*(\alpha)\text{.}\) In particular, we have \(\alpha\in K^*\text{,}\) as desired.
