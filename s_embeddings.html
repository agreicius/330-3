    Section 1.10 Field embeddings

    In this question we investigate what looks to be a somewhat technical topic: given a ring homomorphism \(\sigma F\rightarrow L\) and a field extension \(K/F\text{,}\) how do we describe the set of all extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(K\text{?}\) An extension is just a ring homomorphism \(\tau\colon K\rightarrow L\) satisfying \(\tau\vert_F=\sigma\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.10.1. Field embedding.

    A field embedding (or simply embedding) is a ring homomorphism \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow K\) between two fields. Such a map is guaranteed to be injective since its kernel is trivial.

    Definition 1.10.2. Extension of field embedding.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension. Given a field embedding \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\text{,}\) an extension of \(\sigma\) to \(K\) is an embedding \(\tau\colon K\rightarrow L\) satisfying \(\tau\vert_F=\sigma\text{.}\) In other words, \(\tau\) is an embedding making the following diagram commutative
    Here the vertical line between \(F\) and \(K\) should be understood as the inclusion map \(F\hookrightarrow K\text{.}\)
    Using our new terminology, Theorem 1.4.7 and Theorem 1.4.14 essentially immediately imply the following elegant result.

    Theorem 1.10.3. Extensions of embeddings: simple extension.

    Let \(K=F(\alpha)\) be a simple algebraic extension of degree \(n\text{,}\) and let \(f(x)=m_{\alpha,F}(x)\text{.}\) Given a field embedding \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\text{,}\) define
    \begin{equation*} X=\{\tau\colon F(\alpha)\rightarrow L\ \colon \tau \text{ an embedding}, \tau\vert_F=\sigma\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} R=\{\beta\in L\colon f^\sigma(\beta)=0\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, \(X\) is the set of all extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(F(\alpha)\text{,}\) and \(R\) is the set of all roots of \(f^\sigma(x)\) in \(L\text{.}\)
    1. Given any \(\beta\in R\text{,}\) the map \(\tau\colon F(\alpha)\rightarrow L\) defined as
      \begin{equation} \tau(a_0+a_1\alpha+\cdots +a_{n-1}\alpha^{n-1})=\sigma(a_0)+\sigma(a_1)\beta+\cdots +\sigma(a_{n-1})\beta^{n-1}\tag{1.19} \end{equation}
      is an extension of \(\sigma\) to \(F(\alpha)\text{.}\)
    2. The map
      \begin{align*} X \amp \rightarrow R \\ \tau \amp \mapsto \tau(\alpha) \end{align*}
      defines a bijection between \(X\) and \(R\text{.}\) In other words, the distinct extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(K=F(\alpha)\) are in bijective correspondence with the roots of \(f^\sigma(x)\) in \(L\text{.}\)
    3. We have \(\abs{X}\leq n=\deg_F\alpha\text{.}\) In other words, there are at most \(n\) extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(K=F(\alpha)\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    First observe that (3) follows from (2) since \(f^\sigma\in L[x]\) has at most \(n=\deg f^\sigma=\deg f\) roots in \(L\text{.}\) Thus \(\abs{X}=\abs{R}\leq n\text{.}\)
    Now consider statements (1)-(2). In the special case where \(K=F[x]/(f)\) is a quotient of a polynomial ring, both statements follow immediately from Theorem 1.4.7. We will reduce to this special case by noting that our simple extension \(K=F(\alpha)\) is isomorphic to the quotient ring \(F[x]/(f)\text{,}\) where \(f(x)=m_{\alpha, F}(x)\text{,}\) via the isomorphism
    \begin{align*} \phi\colon F[x]/(f) \amp \rightarrow F(\alpha)\\ g(\overline{x}) \amp \mapsto g(\alpha)\text{.} \end{align*}
    The crucial idea will be that since \(F(\alpha)\) and \(F[x]/(f)\) are isomorphic, the set of extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(F(\alpha)\) is in bijection with the set of extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(F[x]/(f)\text{.}\)
    In more detail, let \(X\) and \(R\) be as stated in the theorem, and let
    \begin{equation*} X'=\{\tau'\colon F[x]/(f)\rightarrow L\ \colon \tau' \text{ an embedding}, \tau'\vert_F=\sigma\}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    the set of extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(F[x]/(f)\text{.}\) It is easy to see that the map
    \begin{align*} X\amp\rightarrow X'\\ \tau \amp\mapsto \tau'=\tau\circ \phi \end{align*}
    is a bijection (with inverse \(\tau'\mapsto \tau'\circ\phi^{-1}\)). Statement (1) of the theorem now follows since the map \(\tau\) described in (1.19) is precisely \(\tau'\circ \phi^{-1}\text{,}\) where \(\tau'\colon F[x]/(f)\rightarrow L\) is the map
    \begin{equation*} \tau'(a_0+a_1\overline{x}+\cdots +a_{n-1}\overline{x}^{n-1})=\sigma(a_0)+\sigma(a_1)\beta+\cdots +\sigma(a_{n-1})\beta^{n-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is an embedding by Theorem 1.4.7. Similarly, since the map
    \begin{align*} X' \amp \rightarrow R\\ \tau' \amp \mapsto \tau'(\overline{x}) \end{align*}
    is a bijection by Theorem 1.4.7, the composition
    \begin{equation*} X\rightarrow X'\rightarrow R \end{equation*}
    is also a bijection; and furthermore this composition satisfies
    \begin{equation*} \tau\in X \longmapsto \tau\circ\phi\in X' \longmapsto \tau(\phi(\overline{x}))=\tau(\alpha) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus the map
    \begin{align*} X \amp \rightarrow R\\ \tau \amp \mapsto \tau(\alpha) \end{align*}
    is a bijection, proving (2).

    Definition 1.10.4. \(F\)-embedding.

    Let \(K/F\) and \(L/F\) be field extensions of \(F\text{.}\) An \(F\)-embedding of \(K\) into \(L\) is an embedding \(\tau\colon K\rightarrow L\) satisfying
    \begin{equation*} \tau(a)=a \end{equation*}
    for all \(a\in F\text{.}\) In other words, an \(F\)-embedding of \(K\) is an extension to \(K\) of the inclusion map \(i\colon F\hookrightarrow L\text{.}\) We denote by \(\Hom_F(K,L)\) the set of all \(F\)-embeddings of \(K\) into \(L\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation} \Hom_F(K,L)=\{\sigma\colon K\rightarrow L\ \colon \sigma \text{ an embedding}, \sigma\vert_F=i\}\text{.}\tag{1.20} \end{equation}
    An \(F\)-isomorphism is an \(F\)-embedding \(\sigma\colon K\rightarrow L\) that is an isomorphism. We also say \(\sigma\) is an isomorphism over \(F\) in this case.
    Thus an \(F\)-embedding between field extensions \(K/F\) and \(L/F\) is just a special case of Definition 1.10.2, where the starting embedding \(F\rightarrow L\) is the inclusion map \(i\colon F\rightarrow L\text{.}\) This is illustrated by the commutative diagram in Figure 1.10.5.

    Example 1.10.6. \(\Q\)-embeddings.

    For each pair of extensions \(K/\Q\) and \(L/\Q\) compute \(\Hom_\Q(K,L)\text{.}\)
    1. \(K=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\text{,}\) \(L=\R\text{.}\)
    2. \(K=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\text{,}\) \(L=\C\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We use Theorem 1.10.3 throughout.
    1. Let \(\sqrt[3]{2}\text{.}\) We have \(m_{\sqrt[3]{2},\Q}(x)=x^3-2\text{.}\) The only root of \(x^3-2\) in \(\R\) is \(\sqrt[3]{2}\text{,}\) and thus the only element of \(\Hom_\Q(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2}),\R)\) is the map \(\tau\) defined as
      \begin{equation*} \tau(a+b\sqrt[3]{2}+c\sqrt[3]{2})=a+b\sqrt[3]{2}+c\sqrt[3]{4}, \end{equation*}
      for all \(a,b,c\in \Q\text{.}\) We recognize this embedding as none other than the inclusion map \(i\colon \Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\hookrightarrow \R\text{.}\)
    2. As above, we have \(m_{\Q,\alpha}(x)=x^3-2\text{.}\) The polynomial \(x^3-2\) has three distinct roots \(\beta_1=\sqrt[3]{2}, \beta_2=\zeta\sqrt[3]{2}, \beta_3=\zeta^2\sqrt[3]{2}\) in \(\C\text{,}\) where \(\zeta=e^{2\pi i/3}\text{.}\) These three roots give rise to exactly three \(\Q\)-embeddings \(\tau_i\text{,}\) \(1\leq i\leq 3\text{,}\) defined as
      \begin{equation*} \tau_i(a+b\alpha+c\alpha^2)=a+b\beta_i+c\beta_i^2, \end{equation*}
      for all \(a,b,c\in \Q\text{.}\) We conclude that \(\Hom_\Q(K, \R)=\{\tau_1,\tau_2,\tau_3\}\text{.}\)

    Example 1.10.7. \(\Q\)-embeddings in towers.

    Compute \(\Hom_\Q(\Q(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3}),\R)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We break the extension \(\Q(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3})/\Q\) into two simple towers \(\Q\leq \Q(\sqrt{2})\leq K=\Q(\sqrt{2})(\sqrt{3})\text{.}\)
    We have \(m_{\sqrt{2},\Q}(x)=x^2-2\text{,}\) which has two roots \(\pm \sqrt{2}\) in \(\R\text{.}\) It follows that \(\Hom_\Q(\Q(\sqrt{2}), \R)=\{\tau_1,\tau_2\}\text{,}\) where
    \begin{align*} \tau_1(a+b\sqrt{2}) \amp = a+b\sqrt{2}\\ \tau_2(a+b\sqrt{2}) \amp = a-b\sqrt{2}\text{,} \end{align*}
    for all \(a,b\in \Q\text{.}\)
    Now look at the second subextension \(\Q(\sqrt{2})(\sqrt{3})/\Q(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\) Since \(m_{\sqrt{3},\Q(\sqrt{2})}(x)=x^2-3\text{,}\) and since \(x^2-3\) has two roots \(\pm\sqrt{3}\) in \(\R\text{,}\) for each \(1\leq i\leq 2\) the embedding \(\tau_i\colon \Q(\sqrt{2})\rightarrow \R\) has two extensions to \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2})(\sqrt{3})\text{:}\) namely \(\tau_{i,1}\) and \(\tau_{i,2}\) defined as
    \begin{align*} \tau_{i1}(\alpha+\beta\sqrt{3}) \amp = \tau_{i}(\alpha)+\tau_i(\beta)\sqrt{3}\\ \tau_{i2}(\alpha+\beta\sqrt{3}) \amp = \tau_{i}(\alpha)-\tau_i(\beta)\sqrt{3}\text{,} \end{align*}
    for all \(\alpha,\beta\in \Q(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\) See Figure 1.10.8 for a nice visual synopsis of this argument.
    The four embeddings \(\tau_{i,j}\text{,}\) \(1\leq i,j\leq 2\) are easily seen to be distinct. (Compare their values at \(\sqrt{2}\) and \(\sqrt{3}\text{.}\)) Furthermore, since \(\tau_{i,j}\) is an extension of \(\tau_i\text{,}\) and \(\tau_i\) is a \(\Q\)-embedding, we have
    \begin{equation*} \tau_{i,j}\vert_\Q=\tau_i\vert_\Q=i\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(i\colon \Q\hookrightarrow \R\) is the inclusion map. Thus each \(\tau_{i,j}\) is a \(\Q\)-embedding of \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3})\) into \(\R\text{.}\)
    Lastly, we prove that if \(\tau\in \Hom_\Q(K,\R)\) is a \(\Q\)-embedding, then \(\tau=\tau_{i,j}\) for some \(1\leq i,j\leq 2\text{,}\) in which case we have
    \begin{equation*} \Hom_\Q(K,\R)=\{\tau_{1,1},\tau_{1,2}, \tau_{2,1}, \tau_{2,2}\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Given \(\tau\in \Hom_\Q(K,\R)\text{,}\) its restriction \(\tau\vert_{\Q(\sqrt{2})}\) is a \(\Q\)-embedding of \(\Q(\sqrt{2})\) into \(\R\text{:}\) i.e., \(\tau\vert_{\Q(\sqrt{2})}\in \Hom_{\Q}(\Q(\sqrt{2}), \R)\text{.}\) Since \(\Hom_{\Q}(\Q(\sqrt{2}),\R)=\{\tau_1,\tau_2\}\text{,}\) we have \(\tau\vert_{\Q(\sqrt{2})}=\tau_i\) for some \(1\leq i\leq 2\text{.}\) Next, since \(\tau\) is an extension of \(\tau\vert_{\Q(\sqrt{2}}\) to \(K\text{,}\) and since \(\Hom_{\Q(\sqrt{2})}(K,\R)=\{\tau_{i,j}\colon 1\leq i,j\leq 2\}\text{,}\) we must have \(\tau=\tau_{i,j}\) for some \(1\leq i,j\leq 2\text{,}\) as claimed.
    Unpacking the definitions of the \(\tau_{i,j}\) and using the fact that these maps are ring homomorphisms, we find that
    \begin{align*} \tau_{1,1}(a+b\sqrt{2}+c\sqrt{3}+d\sqrt{6}) \amp= a+b\sqrt{2}+c\sqrt{3}+d\sqrt{6}\\ \tau_{1,2}(a+b\sqrt{2}+c\sqrt{3}+d\sqrt{6}) \amp= a+b\sqrt{2}-c\sqrt{3}-d\sqrt{6}\\ \tau_{2,1}(a+b\sqrt{2}+c\sqrt{3}+d\sqrt{6}) \amp= a-b\sqrt{2}+c\sqrt{3}-d\sqrt{6}\\ \tau_{2,2}(a+b\sqrt{2}+c\sqrt{3}+d\sqrt{6}) \amp= a-b\sqrt{2}-c\sqrt{3}+d\sqrt{6}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Variants of the argument used in Example 1.10.7 appear frequently when accounting for extensions of embeddings. To spare us having to justify our counting principle repeatedly, we enshrine this argument in our counting extensions lemma.

    Lemma 1.10.9. Counting extensions of embeddings.

    Let \(K/K'/F\) be a tower of fields. Given a field embedding \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\text{,}\) let \(X\) be the set of all extensions \(\tau\colon K\rightarrow L\) of \(\sigma\) to \(K\text{,}\) let \(X'\) be the set of all extensions \(\tau'\colon K'\rightarrow L\) of \(\sigma\) to \(K'\text{,}\) and for each \(\tau'\in X'\text{,}\) let \(X_{\tau'}\) be the set of all extensions of \(\tau'\) to \(K\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{equation} X=\bigcup_{\tau'\in X'}X_{\tau'}\text{.}\tag{1.21} \end{equation}
    In particular, if the sets \(X'\) and \(X_{\tau'}\) are all finite, we have
    \begin{equation} \abs{X}=\sum_{\tau'\in X'}\abs{X_{\tau'}}\text{.}\tag{1.22} \end{equation}

    Proof.

    First observe that if \(\tau\in X_{\tau'}\) is an extension of \(\tau'\in X'\text{,}\) then we have
    \begin{align*} \tau\vert_F \amp = (\tau\vert_{K'})\vert_F \amp (F\leq K')\\ \amp =\tau'\vert_F\\ \amp = \sigma \end{align*}
    This proves \(X_{\tau'}\subseteq X\) for all \(\tau'\in X'\text{,}\) and hence
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{\tau'\in X'}X_{\tau'}\subseteq X \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly, given \(\tau\in X\text{,}\) its restriction \(\tau'=\tau\vert_{K'}\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} \tau'\vert_F=(\tau\vert_{K'})\vert_F=\tau\vert_F=\sigma\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\tau\in X_{\tau'}\text{,}\) where \(\tau'=\tau\vert_{K'}\text{.}\) This proves
    \begin{equation*} X\subseteq \bigcup_{\tau'\in X'}X_{\tau'}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    whence the equality (1.21) follows.
    Next, observe that the sets \(X_{\tau'}\) are disjoint: given \(X_{\tau'}\) and \(X_{\rho'}\) with \(\tau'\ne \rho'\text{,}\) we cannot have an embedding \(\phi\in X_{\tau'}\cap X_{\rho'}\text{,}\) since then we’d have
    \begin{equation*} \tau'=\phi\vert_{K'}=\rho'\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, when \(X'\) and the sets \(X_{\tau'}\) are all finite, equation (1.22) follows from a basic counting principle regarding unions of disjoint sets.
    The counting principle articulated by Lemma 1.10.9 is visually summarized by Figure 1.10.10.
    Equipped with Lemma 1.10.9, we can now extend Theorem 1.10.3 to give a bound on the number of extensions of an embedding to a finite extension.

    Theorem 1.10.11. Extensions of embeddings: finite extension.

    Let \(K/F\) be a field extension of degree \(n\text{.}\) Given a field embedding \(\sigma\colon F\rightarrow L\text{,}\) there are at most \(n\) extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(K\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    The proof is by strong induction on \(n=[K\colon F]\text{.}\)
    Base step: \(n=1\text{.}\) In this case \(K=F\text{,}\) and the only extension of \(\sigma\) to \(K\) is \(\sigma\) itself. Thus there are in fact exactly \(n=1\) extensions in this case.
    Strong induction step: let \(n> 1\) and assume the result holds for all field extensions of degree \(k\) with \(k< n\text{.}\) Since \(n\geq 2\text{,}\) we can find \(\alpha\in K-F\text{,}\) yielding a tower of extensions \(F\leq F(\alpha)\leq K\text{.}\) Since
    \begin{equation*} [K\colon F(\alpha)][F(\alpha)\colon F]=[K\colon F]=n\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and \([F(\alpha)\colon F]=d> 1\text{,}\) we have \(k=[K\colon F(\alpha)]< n\text{.}\)
    Making use of the notation in Lemma 1.10.9, let \(X\) be the set of extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(K\text{,}\) let \(X'\) be the set of extensions of \(\sigma\) to \(K'=F(\alpha)\text{,}\) and for each \(\tau'\in X'\text{,}\) let \(X_{\tau'}\) be the set of all extensions of \(\tau'\) to \(K\text{.}\) Since \(K'=F(\alpha)\) is simple, Theorem 1.10.11 implies that
    \begin{equation*} \abs{X'}\leq [K'\colon F]=d\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Next using the induction hypothesis on the extension \(K/K'\) of degree \([K\colon K']=k < n\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \abs{X_{\tau'}}\leq [K\colon K']=k \end{equation*}
    for each \(\tau'\in X'\text{.}\) Finally using (1.22), we conclude that
    \begin{align*} \abs{X} \amp =\sum_{\tau'\in X'}\abs{X_{\tau'}}\\ \amp \leq \sum_{\tau'\in X'}k\\ \amp = k\abs{X'}\\ \amp \leq kd\\ \amp = n\text{,} \end{align*}
    as desired.
